程心、罗辑和维德

罗辑是第一任执剑人。执剑人是一个变态的职位，它的全称是引力波威慑系统控制者。执剑人拥有启

动引力波广播毁灭两个世界的能力，意义是保持两个世界的战略平衡。

执剑人是面壁者计划的产物，所以执剑人又被称为“面壁者的幽灵”。

执剑人并不是地球政权体系的一部分，不过，地球以及三体人的任何决策都绕不开执剑人。所以，在

有了执剑人体系了，地球以及三体人的任何决策都会交给执剑人审核，执剑人同意后才可以得到有效

的实施。

所以，执剑人是一个事实上的独裁者。

这时的人类社会在经历了危机后，形成了空前民主和尊重人权，而执剑人的存在又让人类处于独裁者

的阴影之下。人类非常厌恶独裁者，也就厌恶执剑人。

随着年龄的增长，罗辑的身体也开始变差，人类决定更换执剑人。

当人类决定更换执剑人后，有很多候选人报名了执剑人的参选，这些人里有毕云峰、曹彬、伊万•安东

诺夫、A•J•霍普金斯，还有一位韩国的外交部副部长，还有一位律师，还有维德。

维德曾经是程心的上司，是PIA的负责人，他主导的阶梯计划事实上改变了人类和三体的关系。

维德是一个只知道目标而不择手段的人。他著名的口头禅就是：“前进！前进！！不择手段地前

进！！！”

罗辑本来是一个玩世不恭的花花公子，几十年的面壁者生涯让他成长出了对人类的责任，不过，他本

身还是一个玩世不恭的人。他当然希望保护人类，也希望保护三体人。不过，在面对决策时，丰富的

阅历和对黑暗森林法则的理解，让他可以迅速做出正确的决策。

维德本身就是一个对黑暗森林理解很深刻的人，而且是一个不择手段的人。他对人类的斗争史也非常

了解。

至于毕云峰、曹彬、伊万•安东诺夫、A•J•霍普金斯等，都是以前的老男人，他们也都具备一些男人的

控制欲和破坏性。

不过，这是一个极度尊重民主和人权的时代，这个时代的人对独裁也暴力非常厌恶和恐惧了。他们期

待和平的生活。他们认为最理想的执剑人是这样的：让三体世界害怕，同时却要让人类，也就是自己

不害怕。这样的人当然不存在，所以他们就倾向于让自己不害怕的。这时的人类对罗辑是害怕的，对

维德、是害怕的，甚至对毕云峰、曹彬等人也是害怕的。

他们喜欢程心这样的人。他们觉得，执剑人的威慑只是在那个系统。人只是一个符号。

所以，当罗辑卸任后，人类不再愿意选一个让“人”害怕的执剑人了。这时，程心就成为了最佳人选。

不过，执剑人毕竟是一个职业，就像总统、清洁工、保险业务员那样。

只要是职业，就有工作，只有有工作，就有绩效；只要有绩效，就有一个考核标准，也就有一个适应

的问题。

那么，程心或者维德，他们在面对执剑人的绩效时，会如何表现呢？

执剑人这个职位，其实只做一件事：当发生三体人对人类进攻，执剑人按下发射引力波的按钮。启动

双毁机制。

这个工作是不是很简单？

那么问题来了，罗辑、程心、维德，这三个人谁适合这个职位呢？当绩效考核时，谁可以得到一个要

的业绩呢？

最后，程心成了了第二任执剑人，也是最后一任执剑人。在程心继任执剑人10分钟后，水滴摧毁了引

力波系统，黑暗森林威慑终止。

黑暗森林威慑终止后，三体的代言人智子又找程心对了一下话，这次，智子已经非常强硬了。不过，

智子还是对程心说出了他对人类的几个候选人的绩效判断：

“知道吗？在我们的人格分析系统中，你的威慑度在百分之十上下波动，像一条爬行的小蚯蚓；罗辑的

威慑度曲线像一条凶猛的眼镜蛇，在百分之九十高度波动；而维德……他根本没有曲线，在所有外部环

境参数下，他的威慑度全顶在百分之一百，那个魔鬼！如果他成为执剑者，这一切都不会发生，和平

将继续，我们已经等了六十二年，都不得不继续等下去，也许再等半个世纪或更长。那时，三体世界

只能同在实力上已经势均力敌的地球文明战斗，或妥协……但我们知道，人们肯定会选择你的。”

是的，人类选择了程心。其实我们工作中也经常会选那些10%左右的人。你在工作中遇到过么？

The Danger of Safe Mediocrity

The constant temptation of every organization is safe mediocrity. The first requirement of

organizational health is a high demand on performance. Indeed, one of the major reasons

for demanding that management be by objectives and that it focus on the objective requirements

of the task is the need to have managers set high standards of performance for

themselves.

This requires that performance be understood properly. Performance is not hitting the

bull’s-eye with every shot—that is a circus act that can be maintained only over a few

minutes. Performance is rather the consistent ability to produce results over prolonged

periods of time and in a variety of assignments. A performance record must include mistakes.

It must include failures. It must reveal a man’s limitations as well as his strengths.

And there are as many different kinds of performance as there are different human beings.

One man will consistently do well, rarely falling far below a respectable standard, but also

rarely excel through brilliance or virtuosity. Another man will perform only adequately

under normal circumstances but will rise to the demands of a crisis or a major challenge

and then perform like a true “star.” Both are “performers.” Both need to be recognized.

But their performances will look quite different.

The one man to distrust, however, is the man who never makes a mistake, never commits

a blunder never fails in what he tries to do. He is either a phony, or he stays with the

safe, the tried, and the trivial.

A management which does not define performance as a batting average is a management

that mistakes conformity for achievement, and absence of weaknesses for strengths.

It is a management that discourages and demoralizes its organization. The better a man is,

the more mistakes he will make—for the more new things he will try.

The man who consistently renders poor or mediocre performance should be removed

from his job for his own good. People who find themselves in a job that exceeds their

capacities are frustrated, harassed, anxiety-ridden people. One does not do a man a service

by leaving him in a job he is not equal to. Not to face up to man’s failure in a job is

cowardice rather than compassion.

One also owes it to the man’s subordinates who have a right to be managed with competence,

dedication, and achievement. Subordinates have a right to a boss who performs,

for otherwise they themselves cannot perform.

One owes it finally to all the people in the organization not to put up with a manager

who fails to perform. The entire organization is diminished by the manager or career professional

who performs poorly or not at all. It is enriched by the man who performs superbly.

At first sight the Japanese seem to violate this rule. For few, if any, people ever get

fired for nonperformance in the Japanese organization. In actuality the Japanese organization

may be as demanding and even as competitive as any in the West. The poor or mediocre

performer is not fired; but he is quickly sidetracked and assigned the activities

which are in effect “made work.” And both he and the organization know it. Moreover,

while everyone advances in pay and title according to seniority, there is a day of reckoning

at or around age forty-five when the very few who will become company directors;

that is, top management, are chosen from the many others who will, ten years later, retire

as section managers or department directors.

The only thing that is proven by a man’s not performing in a given assignment is that

management has made a mistake by putting him in. It is a mistake that no manager can

avoid, no matter how carefully he works on the placement of people. The fact that a man

who has performed in earlier assignments does not perform in a new one proves nothing

except that he should not have been put in the job in the first place.

“Failure” in such a case may mean only that a first-rate career professional has been

miscast as a manager. It may mean that a man excellent at running an existing operation

has been miscast as an innovator and entrepreneur. Or it may mean the opposite: that a

man whose strength lies in doing new and different things has been miscast to head a continuing,

well-established, and highly routinized operation.

Failure to perform on the part of a man who has a record of proven performance is a

signal to think hard about the man and the job. And sometimes, of course (see the discussion

of the “widow-maker” job in Chapter 32) it is the job rather than the man that is at

fault.

George C. Marshall, Chief of Staff of the U.S. Army in World War II, was an uncompromising

and exacting boss who refused to tolerate mediocrity, let alone failure. “I have

a duty to the soldiers, their parents, and the country, to remove immediately any commander

who does net satisfy the highest performance demands,” Marshall said again and

again. But he always asserted, “It was my mistake to have put this or that man in a command

that was not the right command for him. It is therefore my job to think through

where he belongs.” Many of the men who emerged in World War II as highly successful

commanders in the U.S. Army were once in the course of their careers removed by Marshall

from an early assignment. But then Marshall thought through the mistake he had

made—and tried, to figure out where that man belonged. And this explains, an large

measure, why the American Army, which had gone into World War II without a single

one of its future general officers yet in a command position (these were all held by men

past retirement age who had to be moved out), produced an outstanding group of leaders

in a few short years.
