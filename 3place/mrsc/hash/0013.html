hate

Analysis of the word hate from the perspectives of etymology, root, and morphology

1. Etymology (Origin & Historical Development)

“Hate” as a noun entered English in the Old English period (before 1000 CE) as “hete,” meaning “hatred, envy, spite, or hostility.”

The verb form “hatian” appeared around the same time, meaning “to regard with extreme ill-will or treat as an enemy.”

By Middle English (c. 1200–1400), the spelling shifted to “hate,” influenced by the related noun “hater.”

The word has Germanic origins, with early uses in expressions of strong aversion.

Modern idiomatic extensions include “hate mail” (1970s) and “hate-watch” (2000s), reflecting cultural shifts in describing dislike.

In the 19th–20th centuries, it gained psychological depth, often tied to intense, persistent hostility.

2. Root & Classical Elements

From Proto-Germanic “hatiz” (hatred) for the noun and “hatōjanan” (to hate) for the verb, tracing to Proto-Indo-European “ḱeh₂d-” or “kad-” meaning “sorrow, hatred, or strong emotion.” This root emphasizes passionate aversion.

Cognates include German “Hass,” Dutch “haat,” and Old Norse “hatr,” all meaning “hatred.” No direct Latin or Greek ties, but related to broader PIE concepts of emotional intensity.

3. Morphology (Word Formation in English)

As a verb, “hate” is regular weak: hate – hated – hated. As a noun, it’s uncountable or countable in compounds.

Derivatives include “hater” (+ “-er,” one who hates, late 14th century), “hateful” (+ “-ful,” full of hate, mid-14th century), “hatred” (noun, c. 1225, hate + “-red,” abstract suffix meaning “condition”), and “hating” (participle).

Productive in slang like “haterade” (mock drink of hate, 2000s).

4. Summary of Core Traits

Original meaning: “hostility or extreme aversion” (Old English).

Modern: intense dislike.

Root: Proto-Germanic hatiz/hatōjanan (PIE kad- “sorrow/hatred”).

Native Germanic; a word of emotional force, evolving from enmity to modern expressions of disdain.

In short, hate stems from ancient Germanic “strong emotion,” embodying persistent hostility across history.

resent

Analysis of the word resent from the perspectives of etymology, root, and morphology

1. Etymology (Origin & Historical Development)

“Resent” entered English in the late 16th century (c. 1590–1600) as a verb meaning “to take ill, feel pain or regret from,” borrowed from Middle French “ressentir” (to feel strongly, be affected by), or directly from Old French “resentir.”

Initially neutral or positive (“feel deeply”), it shifted by the 17th century to negative “feel indignation at a perceived wrong.”

The noun “resentment” (c. 1610s) followed, meaning “indignant or ill feeling from a sense of injury.”

This evolution reflects French literary influences, where “ressentir” conveyed intense feeling.

2. Root & Classical Elements

From Latin “re-” (intensive “again” or “back”) + “sentīre” (“to feel, perceive”), from Proto-Indo-European “sent-” (“to feel”).

Literally “to feel again/intensely.” Cognates include French “ressentir” and English “sense” (from “sentīre”).

The root ties to perception, with the re- prefix adding recurrence or depth.

3. Morphology (Word Formation in English)

As a verb, regular weak: resent – resented – resented.

Derivatives include “resentful” (+ “-ful,” full of resentment, 1650s), “resentment” (noun, + “-ment,” state of resenting), “resentingly” (adverb, rare).

Productive in compounds like “resent-filled.”

4. Summary of Core Traits

Original meaning: “feel deeply” (16th century).

Modern: feel bitterness over a wrong.

Root: Latin re- + sentīre “feel” (PIE sent- “feel”).

Borrowed via French; from general feeling to specific indignation.

In short, resent means “feel again,” shifting from intense sensation to lingering bitterness over perceived slights.
