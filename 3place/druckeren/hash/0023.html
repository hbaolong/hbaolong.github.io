What is participatory democracy?

文/韩宝龙

2026年1月10日 13时03分02秒

什么是参与式民主？（Participatory Democracy）

参与式民主是一种强调公民直接、主动参与政治决策的民主形式。它不只是让人民每隔几年投一次票选代表，而是让普通公民能够持续地、实质性地介入公共事务的讨论、决策和执行过程。

核心特点

  • 直接参与：公民不是被动地“委托”代表，而是亲自参与决策（例如通过公民大会、公众咨询、参与式预算等）。
  • 持续性：参与不限于选举期间，而是贯穿整个政策制定和执行过程。
  • 包容性：特别注重让边缘群体、弱势群体也能发声和影响结果。
  • 赋权导向：通过参与过程，提升公民的民主能力、责任感和对公共事务的归属感。
  • 结合代表制：大多数现实中的参与式民主并不是完全取代代议制，而是作为代议制的补充和深化。

与传统代议制民主的对比

维度 代议制民主（Representative Democracy） 参与式民主（Participatory Democracy）
公民角色 主要是选民，选出代表后交给代表决定 公民是持续的决策参与者
参与频率 周期性（几年一次选举） 持续性、经常性
决策主体 主要由当选代表、专业官僚决定 公民与代表共同决定（公民有实质影响力）
优点 效率高，适合大国治理 更公平、更具回应性、更能反映真实民意
挑战 容易出现精英脱离群众、信任危机 参与门槛、时间成本、可能被少数活跃者主导

经典现实案例

  1. 参与式预算（最著名的例子）
    巴西波尔图阿雷格里（Porto Alegre）从1989年开始实行：居民自己开会决定一部分市政预算怎么花。
    结果：穷人区的公共设施明显改善，政府信任度大幅提升。
    目前全球有数千个城市/地区采用类似模式。

  2. 爱尔兰公民大会
    2016–2018年，随机抽选的99位普通公民 + 政治家一起讨论堕胎、同性婚姻等重大议题，提出建议后提交全民公投，最终推动法律变革。

  3. 社区层面的参与
    美国新英格兰地区的镇民大会（Town Meeting）、欧洲许多城市的市民提案平台、在线参与式规划等。

理论渊源

  • 古希腊雅典的直接民主（虽然范围有限）
  • 卢梭（人民主权思想）
  • 20世纪：卡罗尔·佩特曼（Carole Pateman）的《参与与民主理论》（1970）
  • 现代：哈贝马斯（沟通行动理论）、本杰明·巴伯（强民主）等

总结一句话

参与式民主的核心信念是：
真正的民主，不仅仅是“为人民做主”，而是“由人民共同做主”。

它试图解决现代代议制民主常见的弊端——民众疏离感、政治冷漠、代表与民众脱节等问题，让民主重新变得“可触摸、可参与、可感受”。

Participatory democracy is a form of democracy that emphasizes the direct and active involvement of citizens in political decision-making processes, rather than relying primarily on elected representatives to make decisions on their behalf.

Core Definition

Citizens participate individually and directly in shaping policies and decisions that affect their lives. This goes beyond just voting in elections — it involves ongoing engagement through mechanisms like public consultations, town hall meetings, citizen assemblies, participatory budgeting, referendums, or online platforms. The goal is to make governance more inclusive, responsive, and reflective of diverse community needs.

It is often described as a hybrid model: it combines elements of direct democracy (citizens deciding directly) with representative democracy (elected officials still play a role, but with greater public input and oversight).

Key Characteristics

  • Broad citizen engagement: Encourages ordinary people to contribute ideas, deliberate, and influence outcomes.
  • Beyond periodic voting: Participation is continuous, not limited to election cycles.
  • Focus on empowerment: Aims to build civic skills, trust in institutions, and a sense of ownership over public decisions.
  • Transparency and accountability: Policies emerge from public input, making government more responsive.
  • Often local or issue-specific: Works especially well at community or municipal levels, though national experiments exist.

Comparison with Representative Democracy

Aspect Representative Democracy Participatory Democracy
Decision-making Elected officials decide on behalf of citizens Citizens directly influence or co-decide
Citizen role Primarily voters who choose representatives Active participants in ongoing processes
Frequency of involvement Periodic (elections) Continuous and frequent
Strengths Efficient for large-scale governance More inclusive, builds trust, better reflects needs
Challenges Risk of elite capture, citizen disengagement Can be time-consuming, risk of low turnout or inequality in participation

Participatory democracy seeks to address some weaknesses of representative systems (e.g., distrust in politicians, low voter turnout) by giving people more direct say.

Historical and Theoretical Roots

  • Traces back to ancient Athenian democracy (though limited to free male citizens).
  • Modern theory developed in the 18th–19th centuries (Rousseau, John Stuart Mill) and expanded in the 20th century.
  • Carole Pateman’s influential 1970 book Participation and Democratic Theory argued that true democracy requires active participation in multiple spheres of life (including workplaces) to foster political efficacy and equality.
  • Other key thinkers: Jean-Jacques Rousseau, G.D.H. Cole, and later proponents of deliberative and empowered participatory governance.

Real-World Examples

  • Participatory budgeting (most famous in Porto Alegre, Brazil since 1989): Residents directly decide how part of the city budget is spent through assemblies and voting. This model has spread to thousands of cities worldwide and often benefits marginalized groups.
  • Town hall meetings in New England (USA): Local residents gather to debate and decide on community issues.
  • Citizen assemblies: Randomly selected citizens deliberate on major issues (e.g., Ireland’s Citizens’ Assembly on abortion and climate change).
  • Digital platforms: Cities like Paris or Vienna use online tools for residents to propose and vote on urban projects.
  • Post-disaster planning: New Orleans residents helped shape rebuilding plans after Hurricane Katrina.

Strengths and Challenges

Strengths: - Increases trust in government - Leads to more equitable resource allocation - Builds civic skills and community bonds

Challenges: - Participation can be unequal (e.g., time, resources, or access favor certain groups) - Risk of low turnout or dominance by vocal minorities - Difficult to scale to very large populations or complex national issues

In short, participatory democracy is about making democracy more active and inclusive — shifting from “government for the people” to “government by the people” in a more direct sense. It complements rather than replaces representative systems in most modern applications.
