Here’s a clear and complete explanation of the common English phrasal verb “get by” (with examples for different situations).

1. Most common meaning: Manage to survive or succeed with very little (money, resources, skills, etc.)

This is the meaning you hear most often in everyday English.

  • Finance / Money

“I only earn $2,000 a month, but I can get by in this small town.”

(= I have just enough money to live, nothing extra)

  • Basic needs

“We don’t have much food left, but we’ll get by until the shops open tomorrow.”

(= We can survive with what we have)

  • Language skills

“My Japanese is pretty basic, but I can get by when I travel in Tokyo.”

(= I can handle simple situations like ordering food or asking directions)

  • Work / Ability

“He’s not the best programmer on the team, but he gets by.”

(= His skills are just good enough; he manages to do the job)

2. Physical meaning: Pass someone or something (especially in a tight space)

Less common today, but still used.

  • “Excuse me, can I get by?”

(said when you need to squeeze past someone in a narrow aisle, train, hallway, etc.)

  • “The truck was so big it couldn’t get by on this little road.”

3. Informal meaning: Be accepted or escape punishment (often for something mediocre or slightly wrong)

Usually with “with”.

  • “You can’t get by with that kind of attitude at this company.”

(= That behavior won’t be tolerated)

  • “His report was full of mistakes, but somehow he got by with it.”

(= He wasn’t punished or criticized)

Summary of nuances

Meaning Typical context Example sentence Tone
Survive with little Money, food, language, skills “We’re broke, but we get by.” Neutral / modest
Physically pass Crowded places, narrow spaces “Let me get by, please.” Polite / practical
Escape criticism Work, school, rules “You won’t get by with being late every day.” Slightly negative/warning

“Get by” almost always implies “barely enough” or “just acceptable” – never luxurious or excellent.

If you want to sound more positive, use “get along” (for relationships) or “do well/thrive” instead.
