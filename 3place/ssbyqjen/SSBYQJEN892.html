Scene 1 ／ running scene 4 509

Enter two Gentlemen at several 510 doors

FIRST GENTLEMAN Whither away so fast?

SECOND GENTLEMAN O, God save ye:

Ev’n to the hall 511 , to hear what shall become

Of the great Duke of Buckingham.

FIRST GENTLEMAN I’ll save you

That labour, sir. All’s now done but the ceremony

Of bringing back the prisoner.

SECOND GENTLEMAN Were you there?

FIRST GENTLEMAN Yes, indeed was I.

SECOND GENTLEMAN Pray speak what has happened.

FIRST GENTLEMAN You may guess quickly what.

SECOND GENTLEMAN Is he found guilty?

FIRST GENTLEMAN Yes, truly is he, and condemned upon’t.

SECOND GENTLEMAN I am sorry for’t.

FIRST GENTLEMAN So are a number more.

SECOND GENTLEMAN But pray, how passed it 512 ?

FIRST GENTLEMAN I’ll tell you in a little 513 . The great duke

Came to the bar, where to his accusations 514

He pleaded still 515 not guilty, and alleged 516

Many sharp reasons to defeat the law 517 .

The king’s attorney, on the contrary,

Urged on the examinations 518 , proofs 519 , confessions

Of divers 520 witnesses, which the duke desired

To him brought 521 viva voce 522 to his face:

At which appeared against him his surveyor,

Sir Gilbert Perk his chancellor, and John Car,

Confessor to him, with that devil monk,

Hopkins, that made this mischief.

SECOND GENTLEMAN That was he

That fed him with his prophecies.

FIRST GENTLEMAN The same:

All these accused him strongly, which he fain 523

Would have flung from him, but indeed he could not:

And so his peers, upon this evidence,

Have found him guilty of high treason. Much

He spoke, and learnedly, for life, but all

Was either pitied in him 524 or forgotten 525 .

SECOND GENTLEMAN After all this, how did he bear himself?

FIRST GENTLEMAN When he was brought again to th’bar, to hear

His knell 526 rung out, his judgement, he was stirred

With such an agony, he sweat 527 extremely,

And something spoke in choler 528 , ill 529 and hasty:

But he fell to 530 himself again, and sweetly

In all the rest showed a most noble patience.

SECOND GENTLEMAN I do not think he fears death.

FIRST GENTLEMAN Sure he does not:

He never was so womanish: the cause

He may a little grieve at.

SECOND GENTLEMAN Certainly

The cardinal is the end 531 of this.

FIRST GENTLEMAN ’Tis likely

By all conjectures: first, Kildare’s attainder 532 ,

Then deputy 533 of Ireland, who, removed,

Earl Surrey was sent thither, and in haste too,

Lest he should help his father 534 .

SECOND GENTLEMAN That trick of state 535

Was a deep envious 536 one.

FIRST GENTLEMAN At his 537 return

No doubt he will requite 538 it: this is noted,

And generally 539 , whoever the king favours,

The card’nal instantly will find employment,

And far enough from court too.

SECOND GENTLEMAN All the commons

Hate him perniciously 540 and, o’my conscience,

Wish him ten fathom deep: this duke as much

They love and dote on, call him ’bounteous Buckingham,

The mirror 541 of all courtesy’—

Enter Buckingham from his arraignment, Tipstaves 542 before him, the axe with the edge towards him 543 , Halberds 544 on each side, accompanied with Sir Thomas Lovell, Sir Nicholas Vaux, Sir William Sands, and Common People etc.

FIRST GENTLEMAN Stay there, sir,

And see the noble ruined man you speak of.

SECOND GENTLEMAN Let’s stand close 545 and behold him.

BUCKINGHAM All good people,

You that thus far have come to pity me:

Hear what I say, and then go home and lose 546 me.

I have this day received a traitor’s judgement 547 ,

And by that name must die: yet heaven bear witness,

And if I have a conscience, let it sink 548 me,

Even as the axe falls, if I be not faithful.

The law I bear no malice for my death,

’T has done upon the premises 549 , but justice:

But those that sought it I could wish more 550 Christians:

Be what they will, I heartily forgive ’em:

Yet let ’em look 551 they glory not in mischief,

Nor build their evils 552 on the graves of great men,

For then my guiltless blood must cry against ’em.

For further life in this world I ne’er hope,

Nor will I sue 553 , although the king have mercies

More than I dare make faults 554 . You few that loved me,

And dare be bold to weep for Buckingham,

His noble friends and fellows, whom to leave

Is only bitter to him, only dying 555 :

Go with me like good angels to my end,

And as the long divorce of steel 556 falls on me,

Make of your prayers one sweet sacrifice 557 ,

And lift my soul to heaven.— Lead on, i’God’s name.

To Lovell

LOVELL I do beseech your grace, for charity,

If ever any malice in your heart

Were hid against me, now to forgive me frankly.

BUCKINGHAM Sir Thomas Lovell, I as free forgive you

As I would be forgiven: I forgive all.

There cannot be those numberless offences

Gainst me that I cannot take 558 peace with: no black envy 559

Shall make my grave. Commend me to his grace:

And if he speak of Buckingham, pray tell him

You met him half in heaven: my vows and prayers

Yet 560 are the king’s, and, till my soul forsake 561 ,

Shall cry for blessings on him. May he live

Longer than I have time to tell 562 his years:

Ever beloved and loving may his rule be:

And when old time 563 shall lead him to his end,

Goodness and he fill up one monument 564 .

LOVELL To th’water-side I must conduct your grace,

Then give my charge 565 up to Sir Nicholas Vaux,

Who undertakes 566 you to your end.

VAUX Prepare there,

The duke is coming: see the barge be ready,

And fit it with such furniture 567 as suits

The greatness of his person.

BUCKINGHAM Nay, Sir Nicholas,

Let it alone: my state 568 now will but mock me.

When I came hither, I was Lord High Constable

And Duke of Buckingham: now, poor Edward Bohun:

Yet I am richer than my base 569 accusers,

That never knew what truth 570 meant. I now seal 571 it,

And with that blood will make ’em one day groan for’t.

My noble father, Henry of Buckingham,

Who first raised head 572 against usurping Richard 573 ,

Flying for succour to his servant Banister,

Being distressed, was by that wretch betrayed,

And without trial fell: God’s peace be with him.

Henry the Seventh succeeding, truly pitying

My father’s loss, like a most royal prince,

Restored me to my honours, and out of ruins

Made my name once more noble. Now his son,

Henry the Eighth, life, honour, name and all

That made me happy, at one stroke 574 has taken

For ever from the world. I had my trial,

And must needs say a noble one, which makes me

A little happier 575 than my wretched father:

Yet thus far we are one in fortunes: both

Fell by our servants, by those men we loved most:

A most unnatural and faithless service.

Heaven has an end 576 in all: yet, you that hear me,

This from a dying man receive as certain 577 :

Where you are liberal of 578 your loves and counsels,

Be sure you be not loose 579 : for those you make friends

And give your hearts to, when they once perceive

The least rub 580 in your fortunes, fall away

Like water from ye, never found again

But 581 where they mean to sink ye. All good people,

Pray for me. I must now forsake ye: the last hour

Of my long weary life is come upon me. Farewell:

And when you would say something that is sad,

Speak how I fell. I have done, and God forgive me.

Exeunt Duke and train

FIRST GENTLEMAN O, this is full of pity. Sir, it calls,

I fear, too many curses on their heads

SECOND GENTLEMAN If the duke be guiltless,

’Tis full of woe: yet I can give you inkling

Greater than this.

FIRST GENTLEMAN Good angels keep it from us.

SECOND GENTLEMAN This secret is so weighty, ’twill require

FIRST GENTLEMAN Let me have it:

I do not talk much.

SECOND GENTLEMAN I am confident 585 :

You shall 586 , sir: did you not of late 587 days hear

FIRST GENTLEMAN Yes, but it held not 589 :

SECOND GENTLEMAN But that slander, sir,

FIRST GENTLEMAN ’Tis the cardinal:

And merely to revenge him on the emperor 597

SECOND GENTLEMAN I think you have hit the mark 599 : but is’t not cruel

That she should feel the smart 600 of this? The cardinal

Will have his will, and she must fall.

FIRST GENTLEMAN ’Tis woeful.

We are too open 601 here to argue this:

Let’s think in private more.
