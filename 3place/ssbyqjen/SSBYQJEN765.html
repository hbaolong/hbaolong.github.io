Scene 1 1 / running scene 1

Dead march 2 . Enter the funeral of King Henry the Fifth, attended on by the Duke of Bedford, Regent of France; the Duke of Gloucester, Protector; the Duke of Exeter, [the Earl of ] Warwick, the Bishop of Winchester, and the Duke of Somerset, [and Heralds ]

BEDFORD Hung be the heavens with black 3 : yield, day, to night!

Comets 4 , importing change of times and states 5 ,

Brandish 6 your crystal tresses 7 in the sky,

And with them scourge 8 the bad revolting 9 stars 10

That have consented unto 11 Henry’s death:

King Henry the Fifth, too famous to live long 12 :

England ne’er lost a king of so much worth.

GLOUCESTER England ne’er had a king until his time:

Virtue 13 he had, deserving to command:

His brandished sword did blind men with his 14 beams:

His arms spread wider than a dragon’s wings:

His sparkling eyes, replete with 15 wrathful fire,

More dazzlèd and drove back his enemies

Than midday sun, fierce bent against their faces.

What should I say 16 ? His deeds exceed all speech:

He ne’er lift up his hand but conquerèd 17 .

EXETER We mourn in black: why mourn we not in blood 18 ?

Henry is dead and never shall revive 19 :

Upon a wooden 20 coffin we attend,

And death’s dishonourable victory

We with our stately presence glorify,

Like captives bound to a triumphant car 21 .

What, shall we curse the planets of mishap 22 ,

That plotted 23 thus our glory’s overthrow?

Or shall we think the subtle-witted 24 French

Conjurers and sorcerers, that, afraid of him,

By magic verses 25 have contrived 26 his end?

WINCHESTER He was a king blessed of the King of Kings 27 .

Unto the French the dreadful 28 judgement day

So dreadful will not be as was his sight.

The battles of the Lord of hosts 29 he fought:

The church’s prayers made him so prosperous 30 .

GLOUCESTER The church? Where is it? Had not churchmen prayed 31 ,

His thread of life 32 had not so soon decayed.

None do you like but an effeminate prince 33 ,

Whom like a schoolboy you may overawe 34 .

WINCHESTER Gloucester, whate’er we like, thou art Protector 35

And lookest 36 to command the Prince and realm.

Thy wife is proud: she holdeth thee in awe,

More than God or religious churchmen may.

GLOUCESTER Name not religion, for thou lov’st the flesh 37 ,

And ne’er throughout the year to church thou go’st

Except it be to pray against thy foes.

BEDFORD Cease, cease these jars 38 , and rest your minds in peace:

Let’s to the altar: heralds, wait on us:

[Exeunt funeral procession ]

Instead of gold, we’ll offer up our arms:

Since arms avail not 39 now that Henry’s dead:

Posterity, await for 40 wretched years,

When, at their mothers’ moistened eyes, babes shall suck 41 ,

Our isle be made a nourish 42 of salt tears,

And none but women left to wail the dead.

Henry the Fifth, thy ghost I invocate 43 :

Prosper this realm, keep it from civil broils 44 ,

Combat with adverse planets 45 in the heavens:

A far more glorious star thy soul will make

Than Julius Caesar 46 , or bright—

Enter a Messenger

FIRST MESSENGER My honourable lords, health to you all:

Sad tidings bring I to you out of France,

Of loss, of slaughter and discomfiture 47 :

Guyenne, Champaigne 48 , Rouen, Rheims, Orléans,

Paris, Gisors, Poitiers, are all quite lost.

BEDFORD What say’st thou, man, before dead Henry’s corpse?

Speak softly, or the loss of those great towns

Will make him burst his lead 49 and rise from death.

GLOUCESTER Is Paris lost? Is Rouen yielded up?

If Henry were recalled to life again,

These news would cause him once more yield the ghost 50 .

EXETER How were they lost? What treachery was used?

FIRST MESSENGER No treachery, but want 51 of men and money.

Amongst the soldiers this is mutterèd:

That here you maintain several 52 factions,

And whilst a field 53 should be dispatched 54 and fought,

You are disputing of 55 your generals.

One would have lingering wars with little cost:

Another would fly swift, but wanteth wings:

A third thinks, without expense at all,

By guileful 56 fair words peace may be obtained.

Awake, awake, English nobility!

Let not sloth dim your honours new-begot 57 :

Cropped are the flower-de-luces 58 in your arms 59 :

Of England’s coat 60 , one half is cut away.

[Exit ]

EXETER Were our tears wanting to 61 this funeral,

These tidings would call forth her 62 flowing tides 63 .

BEDFORD Me they concern: Regent 64 I am of France:

Give me my steelèd coat 65 : I’ll fight for France.

Away with these disgraceful wailing robes!

He removes his mourning robes

Wounds will I lend the French instead of eyes,

To weep their intermissive 66 miseries.

Enter to them another Messenger

SECOND MESSENGER Lords, view these letters, full of bad mischance 67 .

France is revolted from the English quite 68 ,

Except some petty 69 towns of no import.

The dauphin 70 Charles is crownèd king in Rheims:

The Bastard 71 of Orléans with him is joined:

Reignier, Duke of Anjou, doth take his part:

The Duke of Alençon flieth to his side.

Exit

EXETER The dauphin crownèd king! All fly 72 to him?

O whither shall we fly from this reproach 73 ?

GLOUCESTER We will not fly, but to our enemies’ throats.

Bedford, if thou be slack, I’ll fight it out.

BEDFORD Gloucester, why doubt’st thou of my forwardness 74 ?

An army have I mustered in my thoughts,

Wherewith already France is overrun.

Enter another Messenger

THIRD MESSENGER My gracious lords, to add to your laments,

Wherewith you now bedew 75 King Henry’s hearse,

I must inform you of a dismal 76 fight 77

Betwixt the stout Lord Talbot and the French.

WINCHESTER What? Wherein Talbot overcame — is’t so?

THIRD MESSENGER O no: wherein Lord Talbot was o’erthrown:

The circumstance 78 I’ll tell you more at large.

The tenth of August 79 last, this dreadful 80 lord,

Retiring from the siege of Orléans,

Having full scarce 81 six thousand in his troop,

By three and twenty thousand of the French

Was round encompassèd 82 and set upon:

No leisure had he to enrank 83 his men.

He wanted 84 pikes 85 to set before his archers:

Instead whereof, sharp stakes plucked out of hedges

They pitchèd in the ground confusedly 86 ,

To keep the horsemen off from breaking in.

More than three hours the fight continuèd,

Where valiant Talbot above human thought 87

Enacted wonders with his sword and lance.

Hundreds he sent to hell, and none durst stand 88 him:

Here, there, and everywhere, enraged he slew.

The French exclaimed the devil was in arms:

All the whole army stood agazed on 89 him.

His soldiers spying his undaunted spirit

‘A 90 Talbot! A Talbot!’ cried out amain 91

And rushed into the bowels 92 of the battle.

Here had the conquest fully been sealed up 93 ,

If Sir John Falstaff 94 had not played the coward.

He, being in the vanguard, placed behind

With 95 purpose to relieve and follow them,

Cowardly fled, not having struck one stroke.

Hence grew the general wrack 96 and massacre;

Enclosèd 97 were they with their enemies.

A base Walloon 98 , to win the dauphin’s grace 99 ,

Thrust Talbot with a spear into the back,

Whom all France with their chief assembled strength

Durst not presume to look once in the face.

BEDFORD Is Talbot slain then? I will slay myself,

For living idly here in pomp 100 and ease,

Whilst such a worthy leader, wanting aid,

Unto his dastard foemen 101 is betrayed.

THIRD MESSENGER O no, he lives, but is took prisoner,

And Lord Scales with him, and Lord Hungerford:

Most of the rest slaughtered, or took likewise.

BEDFORD His ransom there is none but I shall pay.

I’ll hale 102 the dauphin headlong from his throne:

His crown shall be the ransom of my friend:

Four of their lords I’ll change 103 for one of ours.

Farewell, my masters: to my task will I.

Bonfires in France forthwith I am to make,

To keep 104 our great Saint George’s feast 105 withal 106 .

Ten thousand soldiers with me I will take,

Whose bloody deeds shall make all Europe quake.

THIRD MESSENGER So you had need, for Orléans is besieged.

The English army is grown weak and faint:

The Earl of Salisbury craveth supply 107 ,

And hardly 108 keeps his men from mutiny,

Since they, so few, watch such a multitude.

[Exit ]

EXETER Remember, lords, your oaths to Henry sworn:

Either to quell 109 the dauphin utterly,

Or bring him in obedience to your yoke.

BEDFORD I do remember it, and here take my leave,

To go about my preparation.

Exit Bedford

GLOUCESTER I’ll to the Tower 110 with all the haste I can,

To view th’artillery and munition,

And then I will proclaim young Henry king.

Exit Gloucester

EXETER To Eltham 111 will I, where the young king is,

Being 112 ordained his special governor 113 ,

And for his safety there I’ll best devise.

Exit

WINCHESTER Each hath his place and function to attend:

I am left out; for me nothing remains:

But long I will not be Jack-out-of-office 114 .

The king from Eltham I intend to steal

And sit at chiefest stern 115 of public weal 116 .

Exit
