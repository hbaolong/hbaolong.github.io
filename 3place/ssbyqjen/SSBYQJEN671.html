Scene 1 361 ／running scene 5

Enter Gaunt, sick 362 , with York [and Attendants ]

GAUNT Will the king come, that I may breathe my last

In wholesome counsel to his unstaid 363 youth?

YORK Vex not yourself, nor strive not with your breath,

For all in vain comes counsel to his ear.

GAUNT O, but they say the tongues of dying men

Enforce attention like deep harmony.

Where words are scarce, they are seldom spent in vain,

For they breathe truth that breathe their words in pain.

He that no more must say is listened 364 more

Than they whom youth and ease have taught to gloze 365 .

More are men’s ends marked 366 than their lives before.

The setting sun and music is the close 367 ,

As the last 368 taste of sweets is sweetest last,

Writ in remembrance more than things long past.

Though Richard my life’s counsel would not hear,

My death’s sad 369 tale may yet undeaf his ear.

YORK No, it is stopped with other flatt’ring sounds,

As praises, of his state 370 : then there are found

Lascivious metres 371 , to whose venom 372 sound

The open ear of youth doth always listen,

Report of fashions in proud 373 Italy,

Whose manners still our tardy apish 374 nation

Limps after in base imitation.

Where doth the world thrust forth a vanity 375 ——

So 376 it be new, there’s no respect 377 how vile ——

That is not quickly buzzed 378 into his ears?

That 379 all too late comes counsel to be heard,

Where will doth mutiny with wit’s regard 380 .

Direct not him whose way himself will choose.

’Tis breath thou lack’st, and that breath wilt thou lose.

GAUNT Methinks I am a prophet new inspired 381

And thus expiring 382 do foretell of him.

His rash fierce blaze of riot 383 cannot last,

For violent fires soon burn out themselves.

Small 384 showers last long, but sudden storms are short.

He tires betimes 385 that spurs too fast betimes.

With eager feeding food doth choke the feeder.

Light vanity 386 , insatiate cormorant 387 ,

Consuming means 388 soon preys upon itself.

This royal throne of kings, this sceptred 389 isle,

This earth of majesty 390 , this seat of Mars 391 ,

This other Eden, demi-paradise,

This fortress built by nature for herself

Against infection and the hand of war,

This happy breed 392 of men, this little world,

This precious stone set in the silver sea,

Which serves it in the office 393 of a wall,

Or as a moat defensive to a house,

Against the envy of less happier lands,

This blessèd plot 394 , this earth, this realm, this England,

This nurse, this teeming 395 womb of royal kings,

Feared by their breed 396 and famous for their birth,

Renownèd for their deeds as far from home,

For Christian service and true chivalry,

As is the sepulchre 397 in stubborn Jewry 398

Of the world’s ransom, blessèd Mary’s son 399 :

This land of such dear souls, this dear dear land,

Dear for her reputation through the world,

Is now leased out —— I die pronouncing it ——

Like to a tenement 400 or pelting 401 farm.

England, bound in 402 with the triumphant sea,

Whose rocky shore beats back the envious 403 siege

Of watery Neptune 404 , is now bound 405 in with shame,

With inky blots and rotten parchment bonds 406 .

That England, that was wont 407 to conquer others,

Hath made a shameful conquest of itself.

Ah, would the scandal vanish with my life,

How happy then were my ensuing death!

Enter King, Queen, Aumerle, Bushy, Green, Bagot, Ross and Willoughby

YORK The king is come. Deal mildly with his youth,

For young hot colts being raged do rage the more.

QUEEN How fares our noble uncle Lancaster?

KING RICHARD What comfort, man? How is’t with agèd Gaunt?

GAUNT O, how that name befits my composition 408 !

Old Gaunt indeed, and gaunt in being old.

Within me grief hath kept a tedious fast,

And who abstains from meat 409 that is not gaunt?

For sleeping England long time have I watched 410 .

Watching breeds leanness, leanness is all gaunt.

The pleasure that some fathers feed upon,

Is my strict fast —— I mean, my children’s looks,

And therein fasting, hast thou made me gaunt.

Gaunt am I for the grave, gaunt as a grave,

Whose hollow womb inherits 411 nought but bones.

KING RICHARD Can sick men play so nicely 412 with their names?

GAUNT No, misery makes sport to mock 413 itself.

Since thou dost seek to kill my name in me 414 ,

I mock my name, great king, to flatter thee.

KING RICHARD Should dying men flatter those that live?

GAUNT No, no, men living flatter those that die.

KING RICHARD Thou, now a-dying, say’st thou flatter’st me.

GAUNT O no, thou diest, though I the sicker be.

KING RICHARD I am in health, I breathe, I see thee ill 415 .

GAUNT Now he that made me knows I see thee ill:

Ill in myself to see, and in thee seeing ill.

Thy death-bed is no lesser than the land

Wherein thou liest in reputation sick.

And thou, too careless patient as thou art,

Commit’st thy anointed body to the cure

Of those physicians that first wounded thee.

A thousand flatterers sit within thy crown,

Whose compass 416 is no bigger than thy head.

And yet, encagèd in so small a verge 417 ,

The waste 418 is no whit lesser than thy land.

O, had thy grandsire 419 with a prophet’s eye

Seen how his son’s son should destroy his sons 420 ,

From forth 421 thy reach he would have laid thy shame,

Deposing 422 thee before thou wert possessed 423 ,

Which art possessed now to depose thyself.

Why, cousin 424 , were thou regent 425 of the world,

It were a shame to let his land 426 by lease.

But for thy world enjoying but this land,

Is it not more than shame to shame it so?

Landlord of England art thou and not king.

Thy state of law 427 is bondslave to the law, and——

KING RICHARD And thou, a lunatic lean-witted fool,

Presuming 428 on an ague 429 ’s privilege,

Dar’st with thy frozen 430 admonition

Make pale our cheek, chasing the royal blood

With fury from his native residence?

Now, by my seat 431 ’s right royal majesty,

Wert thou not brother to great Edward’s son,

This tongue that runs so roundly 432 in thy head

Should run thy head from thy unreverent 433 shoulders.

GAUNT O, spare me not, my brother’s —— Edward’s —— son,

For that 434 I was his father Edward’s son.

That blood already, like the pelican 435 ,

Thou hast tapped out 436 and drunkenly caroused 437 .

My brother Gloucester, plain well-meaning soul ——

Whom fair 438 befall in heaven ’mongst happy souls! ——

May be a precedent and witness good

That thou respect’st not spilling Edward’s blood.

Join with the present sickness that I have,

And thy unkindness 439 be like crookèd age,

To crop at once a too long withered flower.

Live in thy shame, but die not shame with thee:

These words hereafter thy tormentors be!

Convey me to my bed, then to my grave:

Love they to live that love and honour have.

Exit

Carried off by Attendants

KING RICHARD And let them die that age and sullens 440 have,

For both hast thou, and both become 441 the grave.

YORK I do beseech your majesty, impute his words

To wayward sickliness and age in him.

He loves you, on my life, and holds you dear

As Harry 442 Duke of Hereford, were he here.

KING RICHARD Right, you say true. As Hereford’s love, so his;

As theirs, so mine, and all be as it is.

Enter Northumberland

NORTHUMBERLAND My liege, old Gaunt commends him to your majesty.

KING RICHARD What says he?

NORTHUMBERLAND Nay, nothing. All is said.

His tongue is now a stringless instrument.

Words, life and all, old Lancaster hath spent 443 .

YORK Be York the next that must be bankrupt so!

Though death be poor, it ends a mortal woe.

KING RICHARD The ripest fruit first falls, and so doth he.

His time is spent, our pilgrimage 444 must be.

So much for that. Now for our Irish wars:

We must supplant 445 those rough rug-headed 446 kerns 447 ,

Which live like venom where no venom else

But only they have privilege to live.

And for these great affairs do ask some charge 448 ,

Towards our assistance we do seize 449 to us

The plate 450 , coin, revenues and movables 451 ,

Whereof our uncle Gaunt did stand possessed.

YORK How long shall I be patient? O, how long

Shall tender duty make me suffer wrong?

Not Gloucester’s death, nor Hereford’s banishment,

Nor Gaunt’s rebukes 452 , nor England’s private wrongs 453 ,

Nor the prevention of poor Bullingbrook

About his marriage 454 , nor my own disgrace

Have ever made me sour my patient cheek,

Or bend one wrinkle 455 on my sovereign’s face.

I am the last of noble Edward’s sons,

Of whom thy father, Prince of Wales, was first.

In war was never lion raged more fierce,

In peace was never gentle lamb more mild,

Than was that young and princely gentleman.

His face thou hast, for even so looked he,

Accomplished with the number of thy hours 456 .

But when he frowned, it was against the French

And not against his friends. His noble hand

Did win what he did spend and spent not that

Which his triumphant father’s hand had won.

His hands were guilty of no kindred’s blood,

But bloody with the enemies of his kin.

O Richard, York is too far gone with grief,

Or else he never would compare between.

KING RICHARD Why, uncle, what’s the matter?

YORK O my liege,

Pardon me, if you please; if not, I, pleased

Not to be pardoned, am content withal 457 .

Seek you to seize 458 and grip into your hands

The royalties 459 and rights of banished Hereford?

Is not Gaunt dead? And doth not Hereford live?

Was not Gaunt just? And is not Harry true 460 ?

Did not the one deserve to have an heir?

Is not his heir a well-deserving son?

Take Hereford’s rights away, and take from time

His 461 charters and his customary rights 462 :

Let not tomorrow then ensue 463 today.

Be not thyself. For how art thou a king

But by fair sequence and succession?

Now, afore God —— God forbid I say true! ——

If you do wrongfully seize Hereford’s right,

Call in his letters patents 464 that he hath

By his attorneys-general to sue

His livery 465 , and deny his offered homage 466 ,

You pluck 467 a thousand dangers on your head,

You lose a thousand well-disposèd hearts

And prick 468 my tender patience to those thoughts

Which honour and allegiance cannot think.

KING RICHARD Think what you will, we seize into our hands

His plate, his goods, his money and his lands.

YORK I’ll not be by 469 the while. My liege, farewell:

What will ensue hereof, there’s none can tell.

But by bad courses 470 may be understood

That their events 471 can never fall out good.

Exit

KING RICHARD Go, Bushy, to the Earl of Wiltshire straight.

Bid him repair 472 to us to Ely House

To see 473 this business.—— Tomorrow next 474

We will for Ireland, and ’tis time, I trow 475 .

And we create, in absence of ourself,

Our uncle York Lord Governor of England,

For he is just and always loved us well.——

Come on, our queen. Tomorrow must we part.

Be merry, for our time of stay is short.

Flourish .

[Exeunt all ] except Northumberland, Willoughby and Ross

NORTHUMBERLAND Well, lords, the Duke of Lancaster is dead.

ROSS And living too, for now his son is duke.

WILLOUGHBY Barely in title, not in revenue.

NORTHUMBERLAND Richly in both, if justice had her right.

ROSS My heart is great 476 , but it must break with silence 477 ,

Ere’t be disburdened with a liberal 478 tongue.

NORTHUMBERLAND Nay, speak thy mind, and let him ne’er speak more

That speaks thy words again to do thee harm!

WILLOUGHBY Tends that thou wouldst speak to 479 th’Duke of Hereford?

If it be so, out with it boldly, man.

Quick is mine ear to hear of good towards him.

ROSS No good at all that I can do for him,

Unless you call it good to pity him,

Bereft 480 and gelded 481 of his patrimony.

NORTHUMBERLAND Now, afore heaven, ’tis shame such wrongs are borne

In him, a royal prince, and many more

Of noble blood in this declining land.

The king is not himself, but basely led

By flatterers. And what they will inform,

Merely in hate, gainst any of us all,

That will the king severely prosecute 482

Gainst us, our lives, our children, and our heirs.

ROSS The commons hath he piled 483 with grievous taxes,

And quite lost their hearts. The nobles hath he fined

For ancient 484 quarrels, and quite lost their hearts.

WILLOUGHBY And daily new exactions 485 are devised,

As blanks 486 , benevolences 487 , and I wot 488 not what.

But what, o’God’s name, doth become of this 489 ?

NORTHUMBERLAND Wars hath not wasted it, for warred he hath not,

But basely yielded upon compromise 490

That which his ancestors achieved with blows.

More hath he spent in peace than they in wars.

ROSS The Earl of Wiltshire hath the realm in farm 491 .

WILLOUGHBY The king’s grown bankrupt, like a broken 492 man.

NORTHUMBERLAND Reproach and dissolution hangeth over him.

ROSS He hath not money for these Irish wars,

His burdenous taxations notwithstanding,

But by the robbing of the banished duke.

NORTHUMBERLAND His noble kinsman. Most degenerate king!

But, lords, we hear this fearful tempest sing,

Yet seek no shelter to avoid the storm.

We see the wind sit sore 493 upon our sails,

And yet we strike 494 not, but securely 495 perish.

ROSS We see the very wreck that we must suffer,

And unavoided is the danger now,

For suffering 496 so the causes of our wreck.

NORTHUMBERLAND Not so: even through the hollow eyes of death

I spy life peering, but I dare not say

How near the tidings 497 of our comfort is.

WILLOUGHBY Nay, let us share thy thoughts, as thou dost ours.

ROSS Be confident to speak, Northumberland.

We three are but thyself, and speaking so,

Thy words are but as thoughts: therefore be bold.

NORTHUMBERLAND Then thus: I have from Port le Blanc, a bay

In Brittany, received intelligence

That Harry Duke of Hereford, Rainold Lord Cobham,

That late broke 498 from the Duke of Exeter 499 ,

His 500 brother, Archbishop late of Canterbury,

Sir Thomas Erpingham, Sir John Rainston,

Sir John Norbery, Sir Robert Waterton and Francis Quoint,

All these well furnished 501 by the Duke of Brittany

With eight tall 502 ships, three thousand men of war,

Are making hither with all due expedience 503

And shortly mean to touch our northern shore.

Perhaps they had ere this 504 , but that they stay 505

The first departing of the king for Ireland.

If then we shall shake off our slavish yoke,

Imp out 506 our drooping country’s broken wing,

Redeem from broking pawn 507 the blemished crown,

Wipe off the dust that hides our sceptre’s gilt 508

And make high majesty look like itself,

Away with me in post 509 to Ravenspurgh 510 .

But if you faint 511 , as fearing to do so,

Stay and be secret, and myself will go.

ROSS To horse 512 , to horse! Urge doubts to them that fear.

WILLOUGHBY Hold out my horse, and I will first be there.
