Enter Benvolio and Romeo

BENVOLIO Tut, man, one fire burns out another’s 170 burning,

One pain is lessened by another’s anguish:

Turn giddy, and be holp 171 by backward turning 172 :

One desperate grief cures with another’s languish 173 :

Take thou some new infection to thy eye,

And the rank poison of the old will die.

ROMEO Your plaintain 174 leaf is excellent for that.

BENVOLIO For what, I pray thee?

ROMEO For your broken 175 shin.

BENVOLIO Why, Romeo, art thou mad?

ROMEO Not mad, but bound more than a madman 176 is:

Shut up in prison, kept without my food,

Whipped and tormented and— Good e’en 177 , good fellow.

SERVINGMAN God gi’ 178 good e’en. I pray, sir, can you read?

ROMEO Ay, mine own fortune in my misery.

SERVINGMAN Perhaps you have learned it without book 179 : but, I pray, can you read anything you see?

ROMEO Ay, if I know the letters and the language.

SERVINGMAN Ye say honestly, rest you merry 180 !

ROMEO Stay, fellow, I can read.

He reads the letter

‘Signior Martino and his wife and daughters, County Anselme and his beauteous sisters, the lady widow of Utruvio, Signior Placentio and his lovely nieces, Mercutio and his brother Valentine, mine uncle Capulet, his wife and daughters, my fair niece Rosaline, Livia, Signior Valentio and his cousin Tybalt, Lucio and the lively Helena.’ A fair assembly: whither 181 should they come?

SERVINGMAN Up.

ROMEO Whither? To supper?

SERVINGMAN To our house.

ROMEO Whose house?

SERVINGMAN My master’s.

ROMEO Indeed, I should have asked you that before.

SERVINGMAN Now I’ll tell you without asking: my master is the great rich Capulet, and if you be not of the house of Montagues, I pray come and crush 182 a cup of wine.

Rest you merry.
