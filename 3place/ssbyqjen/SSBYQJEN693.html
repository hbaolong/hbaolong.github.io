Enter the King, Northumberland, Worcester, Hotspur, Sir Walter Blunt and others

KING HENRY IV My blood hath been too cold and temperate 232 ,

Unapt 233 to stir at these indignities,

And you have found me 234 ; for accordingly

You tread upon 235 my patience. But be sure

I will from henceforth rather be myself 236 ,

Mighty and to be feared, than my condition 237 ,

Which hath been smooth as oil, soft as young down,

And therefore lost that title of 238 respect

Which the proud 239 soul ne’er pays but to the proud.

And majesty might never yet endure

The moody frontier 245 246 of a servant brow.

Your use 248 and counsel, we shall send for you.—

You were about to speak.

NORTHUMBERLAND Yea, my good lord.

Those prisoners in your highness’ name demanded,

Which Harry Percy here at Holmedon took,

Were, as he says, not with such strength denied 249

As was delivered 250 to your majesty,

Who either through envy or misprision 251

Was guilty of this fault and not my son.

HOTSPUR My liege, I did deny no prisoners.

To the King

But I remember, when the fight was done,

When I was dry with rage 252 and extreme toil,

Breathless and faint, leaning upon my sword,

Came there a certain lord, neat 253 and trimly 254 dressed,

Fresh as a bridegroom, and his chin new reaped 255

Showed like a stubble-land at harvest-home 256 .

He was perfumèd like a milliner 257 ,

And ’twixt his finger and his thumb he held

A pouncet-box 258 , which ever and anon 259

He gave 260 his nose and took’t away again,

Who therewith angry 261 , when it next came there,

Took it in snuff 262 . And still 263 he smiled and talked,

And as the soldiers bore dead bodies by,

He called them untaught 264 knaves, unmannerly,

To bring a slovenly 265 unhandsome 266 corpse

Betwixt the wind and his nobility 267 .

With many holiday and lady terms 268

He questioned 269 me, among the rest demanded

My prisoners in your majesty’s behalf.

I then, all smarting with my wounds being cold 270 ,

To be so pestered with a popinjay 271 ,

Out of my grief 272 and my impatience,

Answered neglectingly 273 I know not what,

He should or should not. For he made me mad

To see him shine so brisk 274 and smell so sweet

And talk so like a waiting-gentlewoman 275

Of guns and drums and wounds — God save the mark! 276 —

And telling me the sovereign’st 277 thing on earth

Was parmaceti 278 for an inward bruise,

And that it was great pity, so it was,

That villainous saltpetre 279 should be digged

Out of the bowels of the harmless earth,

Which many a good tall 280 fellow had destroyed

So cowardly, and but for these vile guns,

He would himself have been a soldier.

This bald unjointed chat 281 of his, my lord,

Made me to answer indirectly 282 , as I said,

And I beseech 283 you let not this report

Come current 284 for an accusation

Betwixt my love and your high majesty.

BLUNT The circumstance considered, good my lord,

To the king

Whatever Harry Percy then had said

To such a person and in such a place,

At such a time, with all the rest retold 285 ,

May reasonably die 286 and never rise

To do him wrong or any way impeach 287

What then he said, so 288 he unsay it now.

KING HENRY IV Why, yet he 289 doth deny 290 his prisoners,

But with proviso and exception 291 ,

That we at our own charge 292 shall ransom straight 293

His brother-in-law, the foolish Mortimer,

Who, in my soul, hath wilfully betrayed

The lives of those that he did lead to fight

Against the great magician, damned Glendower,

Whose daughter, as we hear, the Earl of March 294

Hath lately married. Shall our coffers 295 , then,

Be emptied to redeem a traitor home?

Shall we buy treason, and indent 296 with fears 297

When they have lost and forfeited themselves?

No, on the barren mountain let him starve,

For I shall never hold that man my friend

Whose tongue shall ask me for one penny cost

To ransom home revolted 298 Mortimer.

HOTSPUR Revolted Mortimer?

He never did fall off 299 , my sovereign liege,

But by the chance of war. To prove that true

Needs no more but one tongue for all those wounds,

Those mouthèd 300 wounds, which valiantly he took

When on the gentle Severn’s sedgy bank 301 ,

In single opposition, hand to hand,

He did confound 302 the best part of an hour

In changing hardiment 303 with great Glendower.

Three times they breathed 304 and three times did they drink,

Upon agreement 305 , of swift Severn’s flood 306 ;

Who then, affrighted 307 with their bloody looks,

Ran fearfully among the trembling reeds,

And hid his crisp 308 head in the hollow 309 bank,

Bloodstainèd with these valiant combatants.

Never did base and rotten policy 310

Colour 311 her working with such deadly wounds;

Nor never could the noble Mortimer

Receive so many, and all willingly.

Then let him not be slandered 312 with revolt 313 .

KING HENRY IV Thou dost belie 314 him, Percy, thou dost belie him;

He never did encounter 315 with Glendower.

I tell thee, he durst 316 as well have met the devil alone

As Owen Glendower for an enemy.

Art thou not ashamed? But, sirrah, henceforth

Let me not hear you speak of Mortimer.

Send me your prisoners with the speediest means,

Or you shall hear in such a kind 317 from me

As will displease ye.— My lord Northumberland,

We license 318 your departure with your son.—

Send us your prisoners, or you’ll hear of it.

To Hotspur

Exeunt King [Henry, Blunt and train ]

HOTSPUR An if 319 the devil come and roar for them

I will not send them. I will after 320 straight

And tell him so, for I will ease my heart,

Although it be with hazard of 321 my head.

NORTHUMBERLAND What? Drunk with choler 322 ? Stay and pause awhile.

Here comes your uncle.

Enter Worcester

HOTSPUR Speak of Mortimer?

Yes, I will speak of him, and let my soul

Want mercy 323 , if I do not join with him.

In his behalf I’ll empty all these veins,

And shed my dear blood drop by drop i’th’dust,

But I will lift the downfall 324 Mortimer

As high i’th’air as this unthankful king,

As this ingrate 325 and cankered 326 Bullingbrook 327 .

NORTHUMBERLAND Brother, the king hath made your nephew mad.

WORCESTER Who struck this heat 328 up after I was gone?

HOTSPUR He will, forsooth 329 , have all my prisoners.

And when I urged 330 the ransom once again

Of my wife’s brother, then his cheek looked pale,

And on my face he turned an eye of death 331 ,

Trembling even at the name of Mortimer.

WORCESTER I cannot blame him: was he 332 not proclaimed

By Richard 333 , that dead is, the next of blood 334 ?

NORTHUMBERLAND He was. I heard the proclamation.

And then it was when the unhappy 335 king —

Whose wrongs in us 336 God pardon! — did set forth

Upon his Irish expedition,

From whence he intercepted 337 did return

To be deposed and shortly murderèd.

WORCESTER And for whose death we in the world’s wide mouth

Live scandalized and foully spoken of.

HOTSPUR But soft 338 , I pray you; did King Richard then

Proclaim my brother 339 Mortimer

Heir to the crown?

NORTHUMBERLAND He did. Myself did hear it.

HOTSPUR Nay, then I cannot blame his cousin 340 king,

That wished him on the barren mountains starved.

But shall it be that you that set the crown

Upon the head of this forgetful man

And for his sake wore the detested blot 341

Of murderous subornation 342 , shall it be,

That you a world of curses undergo,

Being the agents, or base second means 343 ,

The cords, the ladder, or the hangman rather?

O, pardon if that I descend so low,

To show the line 344 and the predicament 345

Wherein you range 346 under this subtle 347 king.

Shall it for shame be spoken in these days,

Or fill up chronicles in time to come,

That men of your nobility and power

Did gage 348 them both in an unjust behalf,

As both of you — God pardon it! — have done,

To put down Richard, that sweet lovely rose,

And plant this thorn, this canker 349 , Bullingbrook?

And shall it in more shame be further spoken,

That you are fooled 350 , discarded and shook off

By him for whom these shames ye underwent?

No. Yet time serves wherein you may redeem

Your banished honours and restore yourselves

Into the good thoughts of the world again,

Revenge the jeering and disdained contempt

Of this proud king, who studies 351 day and night

To answer 352 all the debt he owes unto you

Even with the bloody payment of your deaths:

Therefore, I say—

WORCESTER Peace, cousin, say no more.

And now I will unclasp 353 a secret book,

And to your quick-conceiving 354 discontents

I’ll read you matter deep 355 and dangerous,

As full of peril and adventurous spirit

As to o’er-walk 356 a current roaring loud

On the unsteadfast 357 footing of a spear 358 .

HOTSPUR If he fall in, goodnight 359 , or sink or swim 360 .

Send danger from the east unto the west,

So 361 honour cross 362 it from the north to south,

And let them grapple. The blood more stirs

To rouse a lion than to start a hare!

NORTHUMBERLAND Imagination of some great exploit

To Worcester

Drives him beyond the bounds of patience.

HOTSPUR By heaven, methinks it were an easy leap,

To pluck bright honour from the pale-faced moon,

Or dive into the bottom of the deep,

Where fathom-line 363 could never touch the ground,

And pluck up drownèd honour by the locks,

So he that doth redeem 364 her thence 365 might wear

Without corrival 366 , all her dignities:

But out upon 367 this half-faced fellowship 368 !

WORCESTER He apprehends 369 a world of figures 370 here,

To Northumberland

But not the form 371 of what he should attend 372 .—

Good cousin, give me audience for a while and list 373 to me.

HOTSPUR I cry you mercy 374 .

WORCESTER Those same noble Scots

That are your prisoners —

HOTSPUR I’ll keep them all.

By heaven, he shall not have a Scot 375 of them.

No, if a Scot would save his soul, he shall not.

I’ll keep them, by this hand.

WORCESTER You start away

And lend no ear unto my purposes.

Those prisoners you shall keep.

HOTSPUR Nay, I will; that’s flat.

He said he would not ransom Mortimer,

Forbade my tongue to speak of Mortimer,

But I will find him when he lies asleep,

And in his ear I’ll holla 376 ‘Mortimer!’

Nay, I’ll have a starling shall be taught to speak

Nothing but ‘Mortimer’, and give it him

To keep his anger still 377 in motion.

WORCESTER Hear you, cousin, a word.

HOTSPUR All studies here I solemnly defy 378 ,

Save how to gall and pinch 379 this Bullingbrook.

And that same sword-and-buckler 380 381 Prince of Wales,

But that 382 I think his father loves him not

And would be glad he met with some mischance 383 ,

I would have poisoned him with a pot of ale.

WORCESTER Farewell, kinsman. I’ll talk to you

When you are better tempered to attend 384 .

NORTHUMBERLAND Why, what a wasp-tongued and impatient fool

To Hotspur

Art thou to break into this woman’s mood,

Tying thine ear to no tongue but thine own 385 !

HOTSPUR Why, look you, I am whipped and scourged with rods,

Nettled and stung with pismires 386 , when I hear

Of this vile politician 387 , Bullingbrook.

’Twas where the madcap duke his uncle 388 kept 389 ,

HOTSPUR You say true.

We’ll stay 396 your leisure.

HOTSPUR I have done, in sooth 397 .
