Enter Kent

Disguised

KENT If but as will 325 I other accents borrow,

That can my speech defuse 326 , my good intent

May carry through itself to that full issue 327

For which I razed my likeness 328 . Now, banished Kent,

If thou canst serve where thou dost stand condemned,

So may it come thy master whom thou lov’st,

Horns within. Enter Lear and Attendants [his Knights ]

LEAR Let me not stay 329 a jot for dinner: go get it ready.—

How now, what art thou?

To Kent

KENT A man, sir.

LEAR What dost thou profess 330 ? What wouldst thou 331 with us 332 ?

KENT I do profess to be no less than I seem; to serve him truly that will put me in trust, to love him that is honest, to converse with him that is wise and says little, to fear judgement 333 , to fight when I cannot choose 334 and to eat no fish 335 .

LEAR What art thou?

KENT A very honest-hearted fellow, and as poor as the king.

LEAR If thou be’st as poor for a subject as he’s for a king, thou art poor enough. What wouldst thou?

KENT Service.

LEAR Who wouldst thou serve?

KENT You.

LEAR Dost thou know me, fellow?

KENT No, sir, but you have that in your countenance which I would fain call master.

LEAR What’s that?

KENT Authority.

LEAR What services canst thou do?

KENT I can keep honest counsel 336 , ride, run, mar a curious tale 337 in telling it, and deliver a plain message bluntly: that which ordinary men are fit for, I am qualified in, and the best of me is diligence.

LEAR How old art thou?

KENT Not so young, sir, to love a woman for singing, nor so old to dote on her for anything 338 : I have years on my back forty-eight.
