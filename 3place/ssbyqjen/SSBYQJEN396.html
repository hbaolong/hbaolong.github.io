Enter Romeo alone

Turn back, dull earth 425 , and find thy centre 426 out.

BENVOLIO Romeo! My cousin Romeo, Romeo!

MERCUTIO He is wise,

And on my life hath stol’n him home to bed.

BENVOLIO He ran this way and leapt this orchard 427 wall.

Call, good Mercutio.

MERCUTIO Nay, I’ll conjure 428 too.

Romeo! Humours! 429 Madman! Passion! Lover!

Appear thou in the likeness of a sigh,

Speak but one rhyme 430 , and I am satisfied:

Cry but ‘Ay me’, pronounce but ‘love’ and ‘dove’,

Speak to my gossip 431 Venus 432 one fair word,

One nickname for her purblind 433 son and heir,

Young Abraham Cupid 434 , he that shot so true,

Aside

The ape is dead 436 , and I must conjure him.—

I conjure thee by Rosaline’s bright eyes,

By her high forehead and her scarlet lip,

By her fine foot, straight leg and quiv’ring thigh,

And the demesnes 437 that there adjacent lie,

That in thy likeness thou appear to us.

BENVOLIO An if he hear thee, thou wilt anger him.

MERCUTIO This cannot anger him: ’twould anger him

To raise a spirit 438 in his mistress’ circle 439

Of some strange 440 nature, letting it there stand 441

Till she had laid it and conjured it down 442 :

That were some spite 443 . My invocation

Is fair and honest 444 , and in his mistress’ name

I conjure only but to raise up him.

To be consorted with 445 the humorous 446 night:

Blind is his love and best befits the dark.

Now will he sit under a medlar 448 tree,

And wish his mistress were that kind of fruit

O 449 Romeo, that she were, O, that she were

This field-bed 453 is too cold for me to sleep.—

ROMEO He jests at scars that never felt a wound.

[Enter Juliet above 454 ]

But, soft, what light through yonder window breaks?

It is the east, and Juliet is the sun.

Arise, fair sun, and kill the envious moon,

Who is already sick and pale with grief,

That thou her maid 455 art far more fair than she:

Be not her maid, since she is envious:

Her vestal livery 456 is but sick and green

And none but fools do wear it, cast it off.

It is my lady, O, it is my love!

O, that she knew she were!

She speaks yet she says nothing: what of that?

Her eye discourses: I will answer it.

I am too bold, ’tis not to me she speaks:

Two of the fairest stars in all the heaven,

Having some business, do entreat her eyes

To twinkle in their spheres 457 till they return.

What if her eyes were there, they in her head?

The brightness of her cheek would shame those stars,

As daylight doth a lamp, her eye in heaven

Would through the airy region stream so bright

That birds would sing and think it were not night.

See how she leans her cheek upon her hand!

O, that I were a glove upon that hand,

That I might touch that cheek!

JULIET Ay me!

ROMEO She speaks:

Aside

O, speak again, bright angel, for thou art

As glorious 458 to this night, being o’er my head

As is a wingèd messenger of heaven

Unto the white upturnèd 459 wond’ring eyes

Of mortals that fall back to gaze on him

When he bestrides the lazy puffing clouds,

And sails upon the bosom of the air.

JULIET O Romeo, Romeo, wherefore 460 art thou Romeo?

Deny thy father and refuse thy name,

Or if thou wilt not, be but sworn my love,

And I’ll no longer be a Capulet.

ROMEO Shall I hear more, or shall I speak at this?

Aside

JULIET ’Tis but thy name that is my enemy,

Thou art thyself, though 461 not a Montague.

What’s Montague? It is nor hand, nor foot,

Nor arm, nor face, nor any other part

Belonging to a man. O, be some other name.

What’s in a name? That which we call a rose

By any other word would smell as sweet,

So Romeo would, were he not Romeo called,

Retain that dear perfection which he owes 462

Without that title. Romeo, doff 463 thy name,

And for 464 thy name, which is no part of thee,

Take all myself.

ROMEO I take thee at thy word:

To her

Call me but love, and I’ll be new baptized,

Henceforth I never will be Romeo.

JULIET What man art thou that thus bescreened 465 in night

So stumblest on my counsel 466 ?

ROMEO By a name

I know not how to tell thee who I am:

My name, dear saint, is hateful to myself,

Because it is an enemy to thee.

Had I it written, I would tear the word.

JULIET My ears have yet not drunk a hundred words

Of thy tongue’s uttering, yet I know the sound:

Art thou not Romeo and a Montague?

ROMEO Neither, fair maid, if either thee dislike.

JULIET How cam’st thou hither, tell me, and wherefore?

The orchard walls are high and hard to climb,

And the place death, considering who thou art,

If any of my kinsmen find thee here.

ROMEO With love’s light wings did I o’er-perch 467 these walls,

For stony limits cannot hold love out,

And what love can do that dares love attempt:

Therefore thy kinsmen are no stop to me.

JULIET If they do see thee, they will murder thee.

ROMEO Alack, there lies more peril in thine eye

Than twenty of their swords: look thou but sweet,

And I am proof 468 against their enmity.

JULIET I would not for the world they saw thee here.

ROMEO I have night’s cloak to hide me from their eyes,

And but 469 thou love me, let them find me here:

My life were better ended by their hate,

Than death proroguèd 470 , wanting of 471 thy love.

JULIET By whose direction found’st thou out this place?

ROMEO By love, that first did prompt me to inquire:

He lent me counsel 472 and I lent him eyes.

I am no pilot 473 , yet wert thou as far

As that vast shore washed with the farthest sea,

I should adventure for such merchandise.

JULIET Thou know’st the mask of night is on my face,

Else would a maiden blush bepaint my cheek

For that which thou hast heard me speak tonight

Fain 474 would I dwell on form 475 , fain, fain deny

What I have spoke: but farewell compliment 476 !

Dost thou love me? I know thou wilt say ‘Ay’,

And I will take thy word. Yet if thou swear’st,

Thou mayst prove false: at lovers’ perjuries

They say Jove 477 laughs. O gentle Romeo,

If thou dost love, pronounce 478 it faithfully:

Or if thou think’st I am too quickly won,

I’ll frown and be perverse and say thee nay,

So 479 thou wilt woo, but else 480 not for the world.

In truth, fair Montague, I am too fond 481 ,

And therefore thou mayst think my behaviour light 482 :

But trust me, gentleman, I’ll prove more true

Than those that have more coying 483 to be strange 484 .

I should have been more strange, I must confess,

But that thou overheard’st, ere I was ware 485 ,

My true love’s passion: therefore pardon me,

And not impute this yielding to light love,

Which 486 the dark night hath so discoverèd 487 .

ROMEO Lady, by yonder blessèd moon I vow

That tips with silver all these fruit-tree tops—

JULIET O, swear not by the moon, th’inconstant moon,

That monthly changes in her circlèd orb 488 ,

Lest that thy love prove likewise variable.

ROMEO What shall I swear by?

JULIET Do not swear at all:

Or if thou wilt, swear by thy gracious 489 self,

Which is the god of my idolatry,

And I’ll believe thee.

ROMEO If my heart’s dear love—

JULIET Well, do not swear. Although I joy in thee,

I have no joy of this contract 490 tonight:

It is too rash, too unadvised, too sudden,

Too like the lightning, which doth cease to be

Ere one can say ‘It lightens’. Sweet, goodnight!

This bud of love, by summer’s ripening breath,

May prove a beauteous flower when next we meet.

Goodnight, goodnight, as sweet repose and rest

Come to thy heart as that within my breast!

ROMEO O, wilt thou leave me so unsatisfied?

JULIET What satisfaction 491 canst thou have tonight?

ROMEO Th’exchange of thy love’s faithful vow for mine.

JULIET I gave thee mine before thou didst request it:

And yet I would it were 492 to give again.

ROMEO Wouldst thou withdraw it? For what purpose, love?

JULIET But to be frank 493 and give it thee again.

And yet I wish but for the thing I have.

My bounty 494 is as boundless as the sea,

My love as deep: the more I give to thee,

The more I have, for both are infinite.

I hear some noise within. Dear love, adieu!—

[Nurse ] calls within

Anon, good nurse!— Sweet Montague, be true.

Stay but a little, I will come again.

ROMEO O blessèd, blessèd night! I am afeard,

Being in night, all this is but a dream,

Too flattering-sweet to be substantial.

[Enter Juliet, above ]

JULIET Three words, dear Romeo, and goodnight indeed.

If that thy bent 495 of love be honourable,

Thy purpose marriage, send me word tomorrow,

By one that I’ll procure to come to thee,

Where and what time thou wilt perform the rite,

And all my fortunes at thy foot I’ll lay,

And follow thee my lord throughout the world.

[Nurse calls ] within ‘Madam!’

I come, anon.— But if thou mean’st not well,

I do beseech thee—

[Nurse calls ] within ‘Madam!’

By and by, I come.—

To cease thy strife 496 , and leave me to my grief.

Tomorrow will I send.

ROMEO So thrive my soul—

JULIET A thousand times goodnight!

ROMEO A thousand times the worse, to want 497 thy light.

Love goes toward love as schoolboys from their books,

But love from love, toward school with heavy looks.

Romeo starts to go

JULIET Hist 498 , Romeo, hist! O, for a falc’ner’s voice,

To lure this tassel-gentle 499 back again!

Bondage is hoarse 500 , and may not speak aloud,

With repetition of my ‘Romeo’.

ROMEO It is my soul that calls upon my name.

How silver-sweet sound lovers’ tongues by night,

Like softest music to attending ears!

JULIET Romeo!

ROMEO My nyas 502 ?

JULIET What o’clock tomorrow shall I send to thee?

ROMEO By the hour of nine.

JULIET I will not fail: ’tis twenty years till then.

I have forgot why I did call thee back.

ROMEO Let me stand here till thou remember it.

JULIET I shall forget, to 503 have thee still 504 stand there,

Rememb’ring how I love thy company.

ROMEO And I’ll still stay, to have thee still forget,

Forgetting any other home but this.

JULIET ’Tis almost morning, I would have thee gone:

And yet no further than a wanton’s 505 bird,

That lets it hop a little from his hand,

Like a poor prisoner in his twisted gyves 506 ,

And with a silken thread plucks it back again,

So loving-jealous of his liberty.

ROMEO I would I were thy bird.

JULIET Sweet, so would I:

Yet I should kill thee with much cherishing.

Goodnight, goodnight! Parting is such sweet sorrow,

That I shall say goodnight till it be morrow.
