Scene 1 286 / running scene 4

A march. Enter Edward, Richard and their power 287

EDWARD I wonder how our princely father scaped,

Or whether he be scaped away or no

From Clifford’s and Northumberland’s pursuit?

Had he been ta’en 288 , we should have heard the news:

Had he been slain, we should have heard the news:

Or had he scaped, methinks we should have heard

The happy tidings of his good escape.

How fares my brother? Why is he so sad?

RICHARD I cannot joy, until I be resolved 289

Where our right valiant father is become 290 .

I saw him in the battle range 291 about

And watched him how he singled Clifford forth 292 .

Methought he bore him 293 in the thickest troop

As doth a lion in a herd of neat 294 ,

Or as a bear encompassed round with dogs,

Who having pinched 295 a few and made them cry,

The rest stand all aloof 296 , and bark at him.

So fared our father with his enemies,

So fled his enemies my warlike father.

Methinks, ’tis prize 297 enough to be his son.

Three suns appear

See how the morning opes 298 her golden gates,

And takes her farewell of the glorious sun.

How well resembles it the prime of youth,

Trimmed 299 like a younker 300 prancing 301 to his love.

EDWARD Dazzle mine eyes, or do I see three suns 302 ?

RICHARD Three glorious suns, each one a perfect sun,

Not separated with the racking 303 clouds,

But severed 304 in a pale clear-shining sky.

See, see: they join, embrace, and seem to kiss,

As if they vowed some league inviolable.

Now are they but one lamp, one light, one sun.

In this the heaven figures 305 some event.

EDWARD ’Tis wondrous strange, the like yet never heard of.

I think it cites 306 us, brother, to the field,

That we, the sons of brave Plantagenet,

Each one already blazing by our meeds 307 ,

Should notwithstanding join our lights together

And overshine 308 the earth, as this 309 the world.

Whate’er it bodes, henceforward will I bear

Upon my target 310 three fair-shining suns.

RICHARD Nay, bear three daughters 311 : by your leave, I speak it,

You love the breeder 312 better than the male.

Enter one [a Messenger ] blowing 313

But what art thou, whose heavy 314 looks foretell

Some dreadful story hanging on thy tongue?

MESSENGER Ah, one that was a woeful looker-on

Whenas 315 the noble Duke of York was slain,

Your princely father and my loving lord!

EDWARD O, speak no more, for I have heard too much.

RICHARD Say how he died, for I will hear it all.

MESSENGER Environèd 316 he was with many foes,

And stood against them, as the hope of Troy 317

Against the Greeks that would have entered Troy.

But Hercules 318 himself must yield to odds,

And many strokes, though with a little axe,

Hews down and fells the hardest-timbered oak.

By many hands your father was subdued,

But only slaughtered by the ireful arm

Of unrelenting Clifford and the queen,

Who crowned the gracious duke in high despite 319 ,

Laughed in his face, and when with grief he wept,

The ruthless queen gave him to dry his cheeks

A napkin steepèd in the harmless 320 blood

Of sweet young Rutland, by rough 321 Clifford slain.

And after many scorns, many foul taunts,

They took his head, and on the gates of York

They set the same, and there it doth remain,

The saddest spectacle that e’er I viewed.

[Exit ]

EDWARD Sweet Duke of York, our prop to lean upon,

Now thou art gone, we have no staff, no stay 322 .

O Clifford, boist’rous 323 Clifford, thou hast slain

The flower of Europe for his chivalry,

And treacherously hast thou vanquished him,

For hand to hand he would have vanquished thee.

Now my soul’s palace 324 is become a prison.

Ah, would she 325 break from hence, that this my body

Might in the ground be closèd up in rest,

For never henceforth shall I joy again:

Never, O, never, shall I see more joy 326 !

RICHARD I cannot weep, for all my body’s moisture

Scarce serves to quench my furnace-burning heart.

Nor can my tongue unload my heart’s great burden,

For selfsame wind 327 that I should speak withal

Is kindling coals that fires all my breast,

And burns me up with flames that tears would quench.

To weep is to make less the depth of grief:

Tears then for babes; blows and revenge for me.

Richard, I bear thy name, I’ll venge 328 thy death,

Or die renownèd by attempting it.

EDWARD His name that valiant duke hath left with thee:

His dukedom and his chair 329 with me is left.

RICHARD Nay, if thou be that princely eagle’s bird,

Show thy descent by gazing gainst the sun 330 .

For chair and dukedom, throne and kingdom say,

Either that 331 is thine, or else thou wert not his.

March. Enter Warwick, Marquis [of ] Montague and their army

WARWICK How now, fair lords? What fare 332 ? What news abroad 333 ?

RICHARD Great Lord of Warwick, if we should recount

Our baleful 334 news, and at each word’s deliverance

Stab poniards 335 in our flesh till all were told,

The words would add more anguish than the wounds.

O, valiant lord, the Duke of York is slain!

EDWARD O Warwick, Warwick, that Plantagenet

Which held thee dearly as his soul’s redemption,

Is by the stern 336 Lord Clifford done to death.

WARWICK Ten days ago I drowned these news in tears,

And now, to add more measure 337 to your woes,

I come to tell you things sith 338 then befall’n.

After the bloody fray at Wakefield 339 fought,

Where your brave father breathed his latest 340 gasp,

Tidings, as swiftly as the posts 341 could run,

Were brought me of your loss and his depart 342 .

I, then in London, keeper 343 of the king,

Mustered my soldiers, gathered flocks of friends,

Marched toward St Albans 344 to intercept the queen,

Bearing the king in my behalf 345 along.

For by my scouts I was advertis 346 èd

That she was coming with a full intent

To dash 347 our late 348 decree in parliament

Touching 349 King Henry’s oath and your succession.

Short tale to make, we at St Albans met

Our battles 350 joined, and both sides fiercely fought.

But whether ’twas the coldness 351 of the king,

Who looked full 352 gently on his warlike queen,

That robbed my soldiers of their heated spleen 353 ,

Or whether ’twas report of her success,

Or more than common fear of Clifford’s rigour 354 ,

Who thunders to his captives blood and death,

I cannot judge: but to conclude with truth,

Their weapons like to 355 lightning came and went,

Our soldiers’ like the night-owl’s lazy flight,

Or like an idle thresher with a flail 356 ,

Fell gently down, as if they struck their friends.

I cheered them up with justice of our cause,

With promise of high pay and great rewards,

But all in vain: they had no heart to fight,

And we in them no hope to win the day,

So that we fled. The king unto the queen,

Lord George your brother, Norfolk and myself,

In haste, post-haste, are come to join with you,

For in the marches 357 here we heard you were,

Making another head 358 to fight again.

EDWARD Where is the Duke of Norfolk, gentle Warwick?

And when came George from Burgundy 359 to England?

WARWICK Some six miles off the duke is with the soldiers,

And for your brother, he was lately sent

From your kind aunt, Duchess of Burgundy,

With aid of soldiers to this needful 360 war.

RICHARD ’Twas odds, belike 361 , when valiant Warwick fled;

Oft have I heard his praises in pursuit 362 ,

But ne’er till now his scandal of retire 363 .

WARWICK Nor now my scandal, Richard, dost thou hear,

For thou shalt know this strong right hand of mine

Can pluck the diadem from faint Henry’s head,

And wring 364 the awful 365 sceptre from his fist,

Were he as famous and as bold in war

As he is famed for mildness, peace, and prayer.

RICHARD I know it well, Lord Warwick, blame me not.

’Tis love I bear thy glories make me speak.

But in this troublous time, what’s to be done?

Shall we go throw away our coats of steel,

And wrap our bodies in black mourning gowns,

Numb’ring 366 our Ave Maries 367 with our beads 368 ?

Or shall we on the helmets of our foes

Tell 369 our devotion with revengeful arms?

If for the last, say ay, and to it, lords.

WARWICK Why, therefore 370 Warwick came to seek you out,

And therefore comes my brother Montague.

Attend 371 me, lords: the proud insulting queen,

With Clifford and the haught 372 Northumberland,

And of their feather many more proud birds,

Have wrought 373 the easy-melting king like wax.

He swore consent to your succession,

His oath enrollèd 374 in the parliament.

And now to London all the crew 375 are gone,

To frustrate 376 both his oath and what beside 377

May make 378 against the house of Lancaster.

Their power, I think, is thirty thousand strong.

Now, if the help of Norfolk and myself,

With all the friends that thou, brave Earl of March,

Amongst the loving Welshmen canst procure,

Will but amount to five-and-twenty thousand,

Why, via 379 , to London will we march,

And once again bestride 380 our foaming steeds,

And once again cry ‘Charge!’ upon our foes,

But never once again turn back and fly.

RICHARD Ay, now methinks I hear great Warwick speak;

Ne’er may he live to see a sunshine day,

That cries ‘Retire!’ 381 if Warwick bid him stay.

EDWARD Lord Warwick, on thy shoulder will I lean,

And when thou fail’st — as God forbid the hour —

Must Edward fall, which peril heaven forfend 382 !

WARWICK No longer Earl of March, but Duke of York:

The next degree 383 is England’s royal throne.

For King of England shalt thou be proclaimed

In every borough as we pass along.

And he that throws not up his cap for joy

Shall for the fault make forfeit of his head.

King Edward, valiant Richard, Montague,

Stay we no longer, dreaming of renown,

But sound the trumpets, and about our task.

RICHARD Then, Clifford, were thy heart as hard as steel,

As thou hast shown it flinty by thy deeds,

I come to pierce it or to give thee mine.

EDWARD Then strike up drums. God and Saint George 384 for us!

Enter a Messenger

WARWICK How now? What news?

MESSENGER The Duke of Norfolk sends you word by me,

The queen is coming with a puissant host 385 ,

And craves 386 your company for speedy counsel.

WARWICK Why then it sorts 387 , brave warriors, let’s away.

Exeunt
