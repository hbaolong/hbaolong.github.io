Enter Maria and Clown [Feste 187 ]

MARIA Nay, either tell me where thou hast been, or I will not open my lips so wide as a bristle may enter in 188 way of thy excuse. My lady will hang thee for thy absence.

FESTE Let her hang me: he that is well hanged 189 in this world needs to fear no colours 190 .

MARIA Make that good 191 .

FESTE He shall see none to fear 192 .

MARIA A good lenten 193 answer. I can tell thee where that saying was born, of ‘I fear no colours.’

FESTE Where, good Mistress Mary?

MARIA In the wars, and that may you be bold 194 to say in your foolery.

FESTE Well, God give them wisdom that have it, and those that are fools, let them use their talents.

MARIA Yet you will be hanged for being so long absent, or, to be turned away 195 , is not that as good as a hanging to you?

FESTE Many a good hanging prevents a bad marriage 196 , and, for 197 turning away, let summer bear it out 198 .

MARIA You are resolute, then?

FESTE Not so, neither. But I am resolved on two points 199 .

MARIA That if one break, the other will hold, or if both break, your gaskins 200 fall.

FESTE Apt 201 , in good faith, very apt. Well, go thy way. If Sir Toby would leave drinking 202 , thou wert as witty a piece of Eve’s flesh 203 as any in Illyria.

MARIA Peace, you rogue, no more o’that. Here comes my lady: make your excuse wisely, you were best 204 .

[Exit ]

Enter Lady Olivia with Malvolio 205 [and Attendants ]

FESTE Wit, an’t 206 be thy will, put me into good fooling! Those wits, that think they have thee, do very oft prove fools, and I that am sure I lack thee, may pass for a wise man. For what says Quinapalus 207 ? ‘Better a witty fool than a foolish wit.’—God bless thee, lady.

FESTE Two faults, Madonna 211 , that drink and good counsel will amend. For give the dry 212 fool drink, then is the fool not dry: bid the dishonest man mend 213 himself. If he mend, he is no longer dishonest; if he cannot, let the botcher 214 mend him. Anything that’s mended is but patched 215 : virtue that transgresses is but patched with sin, and sin that amends is but patched with virtue. If that this simple 216 syllogism 217 will serve, so 218 . If it will not, what remedy? As there is no true cuckold 219 but calamity 220 , so beauty’s a flower 221 . The lady bade take away the fool: therefore, I say again, take her away.

FESTE Misprision 222 in the highest degree! Lady, cucullus non facit monachum 223 : that’s as much to say as I wear not motley 224 in my brain. Good madonna, give me leave 225 to prove you a fool.

OLIVIA Can you do it?

FESTE Dexteriously 226 , good madonna.

OLIVIA Make your proof.

FESTE I must catechize 227 you for it, madonna. Good my mouse of virtue 228 , answer me.

OLIVIA Well, sir, for want of other idleness 229 , I’ll bide 230 your proof.

FESTE Good madonna, why mourn’st thou?

OLIVIA Good fool, for my brother’s death.

FESTE I think his soul is in hell, madonna.

OLIVIA I know his soul is in heaven, fool.

FESTE The more fool, madonna, to mourn for your brother’s soul being in heaven. Take away the fool, gentlemen.

OLIVIA What think you of this fool, Malvolio? Doth he not mend 231 ?

MALVOLIO Yes, and shall do till the pangs of death shake him. Infirmity, that decays the wise, doth ever make the better fool.

FESTE God send you, sir, a speedy infirmity, for the better increasing your folly! Sir Toby will be sworn that I am no fox 232 , but he will not pass 233 his word for twopence that you are no fool.

OLIVIA How say you to that, Malvolio?

MALVOLIO I marvel your ladyship takes delight in such a barren 234 rascal. I saw him put down 235 the other day with 236 an ordinary fool 237 that has no more brain than a stone 238 . Look you now, he’s out of his guard 239 already. Unless you laugh and minister occasion 240 to him, he is gagged. I protest 241 , I take these wise men, that crow 242 so at these set 243 kind of fools, no better than the fools’ zanies 244 .

OLIVIA O, you are sick of 245 self-love, Malvolio, and taste with a distempered 246 appetite. To be generous, guiltless and of free 247 disposition is to take those things for bird-bolts 248 that you deem cannon-bullets. There is no slander in an allowed 249 fool, though he do nothing but rail 250 ; nor no railing in a known discreet 251 man, though he do nothing but reprove 252 .

FESTE Now Mercury endue thee with leasing 253 , for thou speak’st well of fools.

MARIA Madam, there is at the gate a young gentleman much desires to speak with you.

OLIVIA From the Count Orsino, is it?

MARIA I know not, madam. ’Tis a fair young man, and well attended.

OLIVIA Who of my people hold him in delay?

MARIA Sir Toby, madam, your kinsman.

OLIVIA Fetch him off, I pray you. He speaks nothing but madman 254 . Fie 255 on him!—

FESTE Good Sir Toby!

OLIVIA Cousin, cousin, how have you come so early by this lethargy 262 ?

SIR TOBY Lechery? I defy lechery. There’s one 263 at the gate.

OLIVIA What’s a drunken man like, fool?

FESTE Like a drowned man, a fool and a madman: one draught above heat 266 makes him a fool, the second mads him, and a third drowns 267 him.

OLIVIA Go thou and seek the crowner 268 , and let him sit o’my coz 269 , for he’s in the third degree of drink: he’s drowned. Go look after him.

FESTE He is but mad yet, madonna, and the fool shall look to the madman.

[Exit ]

Enter Malvolio

MALVOLIO Madam, yond 270 young fellow swears he will speak with you. I told him you were sick, he takes on him to understand 271 so much, and therefore comes to speak with you. I told him you were asleep — he seems to have a foreknowledge of that too — and therefore comes to speak with you. What is to be said to him, lady? He’s fortified against any denial.

OLIVIA Tell him he shall not speak with me.

MALVOLIO He’s been told so, and he says he’ll stand at your door like a sheriff’s post 272 , and be the supporter to a bench 273 , but he’ll speak with you.

OLIVIA What kind o’man is he?

MALVOLIO Why, of mankind 274 .

OLIVIA What manner of man?

MALVOLIO Of very ill manner 275 . He’ll speak with you, will you or no 276 .

OLIVIA Of what personage 277 and years is he?

MALVOLIO Not yet old enough for a man, nor young enough for a boy. As a squash 278 is before ’tis a peascod, or a codling 279 when ’tis almost an apple: ’tis with him in standing water 280 , between boy and man. He is very well-favoured 281 and he speaks very shrewishly 282 . One would think his mother’s milk were scarce out of him.

OLIVIA Let him approach. Call in my gentlewoman.

MALVOLIO Gentlewoman, my lady calls.

Exit

Enter Viola [and Attendants ]

VIOLA The honourable lady of the house, which is she?

OLIVIA Speak to me, I shall answer for her. Your will?

VIOLA Most radiant, exquisite and unmatchable beauty — I pray you tell me if this be the lady of the house, for I never saw her. I would be loath to cast away 284 my speech, for besides that it is excellently well penned 285 , I have taken great pains to con 286 it. Good beauties, let me sustain 287 no scorn; I am very comptible 288 , even to the least sinister 289 usage.

OLIVIA Whence came you, sir?

VIOLA I can say little more than I have studied 290 , and that question’s out of my part 291 . Good gentle one, give me modest 292 assurance, if you be the lady of the house, that I may proceed in my speech.

OLIVIA Are you a comedian 293 ?

VIOLA No, my profound heart 294 . And yet, by the very fangs of malice, I swear I am not that I play 295 . Are you the lady of the house?

OLIVIA If I do not usurp 296 myself, I am.

VIOLA Most certain, if you are she, you do usurp yourself, for what is yours to bestow is not yours to reserve 297 . But this is from 298 my commission 299 . I will on with my speech in your praise, and then show you the heart of my message.

OLIVIA Come to what is important in’t. I forgive 300 you the praise.

VIOLA Alas, I took great pains to study it, and ’tis poetical.

OLIVIA It is the more like to be feigned 301 . I pray you keep it in 302 . I heard you were saucy 303 at my gates, and allowed your approach rather to wonder 304 at you than to hear you. If you be not mad, be gone. If you have reason, be brief 305 . ’Tis not that time of moon with me 306 to make one 307 in so skipping 308 a dialogue.

MARIA Will you hoist sail 309 , sir? Here lies your way.

VIOLA No, good swabber 310 , I am to hull 311 here a little longer. Some mollification 312 for your giant 313 , sweet lady; tell me your mind, I am a messenger.

OLIVIA Sure, you have some hideous matter to deliver, when the courtesy 314 of it is so fearful 315 . Speak your office 316 .

VIOLA It alone concerns your ear. I bring no overture 317 of war, no taxation of homage 318 ; I hold the olive 319 in my hand. My words are as full of peace as matter 320 .

OLIVIA Yet you began rudely 321 . What are you? What would you?

VIOLA The rudeness that hath appeared in me have I learned from my entertainment 322 . What I am, and what I would, are as secret as maidenhead 323 : to your ears, divinity 324 : to any other’s, profanation 325 .

OLIVIA Give us the place alone. We will hear this divinity.
