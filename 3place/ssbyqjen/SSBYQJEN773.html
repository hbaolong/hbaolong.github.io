Scene 3 451 / running scene 6

Enter [the ] Countess [of Auvergne and her Porter ]

COUNTESS Porter, remember what I gave in charge 452 ,

And when you have done so, bring the keys to me.

PORTER Madam, I will.

Exit

COUNTESS The plot is laid: if all things fall out right,

I shall as famous be by this exploit

As Scythian Tomyris by Cyrus’ death 453 .

Great is the rumour of this dreadful knight,

And his achievements of no less account:

Fain 454 would mine eyes be witness with mine ears,

To give their censure 455 of these rare 456 reports.

Enter Messenger and Talbot

MESSENGER Madam, according as your ladyship desired,

By message craved, so is Lord Talbot come.

COUNTESS And he is welcome. What, is this the man?

MESSENGER Madam, it is.

COUNTESS Is this the scourge of France?

Is this the Talbot, so much feared abroad 457

That with his name the mothers still 458 their babes?

I see report is fabulous 459 and false:

I thought I should have seen some Hercules 460 ,

A second Hector 461 , for 462 his grim aspect 463 ,

And large proportion 464 of his strong-knit 465 limbs.

Alas, this is a child, a silly 466 dwarf:

It cannot be this weak and writhled 467 shrimp

Should strike such terror to his enemies.

TALBOT Madam, I have been bold to trouble you:

But since your ladyship is not at leisure,

I’ll sort 468 some other time to visit you.

COUNTESS What means he now? Go ask him whither he goes.

MESSENGER Stay, my lord Talbot, for my lady craves

To know the cause of your abrupt departure.

TALBOT Marry 469 , for that 470 she’s in a wrong belief 471 ,

I go to certify her Talbot’s here 472 .

Enter Porter with keys

COUNTESS If thou be he, then art thou prisoner.

TALBOT Prisoner? To whom?

COUNTESS To me, bloodthirsty lord;

And for that cause I trained 473 thee to my house.

Long time thy shadow 474 hath been thrall 475 to me,

For in my gallery thy picture hangs:

But now the substance shall endure the like,

And I will chain these legs and arms of thine,

That hast by tyranny 476 these many years

Wasted 477 our country, slain our citizens,

And sent our sons and husbands captivate 478 .

TALBOT Ha, ha, ha!

COUNTESS Laughest thou, wretch? Thy mirth shall turn to moan.

TALBOT I laugh to see your ladyship so fond 479

To think that you have aught 480 but Talbot’s shadow

Whereon to practise your severity.

COUNTESS Why, art not thou the man?

TALBOT I am indeed.

COUNTESS Then have I substance too.

TALBOT No, no, I am but shadow of myself:

You are deceived, my substance is not here;

For what you see is but the smallest part

And least proportion of humanity 481 :

I tell you, madam, were the whole frame 482 here,

It is of such a spacious lofty pitch 483 ,

Your roof were not sufficient to contain’t.

COUNTESS This is a riddling merchant 484 for the nonce 485 :

He will be here, and yet he is not here:

How can these contrarieties 486 agree?

TALBOT That will I show you presently 487 .

How say you, madam? Are you now persuaded

That Talbot is but shadow of himself?

These are his substance, sinews, arms and strength,

With which he yoketh 490 your rebellious necks,

Razeth 491 your cities and subverts 492 your towns

And in a moment makes them desolate.

COUNTESS Victorious Talbot, pardon my abuse 493 :

I find thou art no less than fame hath bruited 494 ,

And more than may be gathered by thy shape.

Let my presumption not provoke thy wrath,

For I am sorry that with reverence

I did not entertain 495 thee as thou art.

TALBOT Be not dismayed, fair lady, nor misconster 496

The mind of Talbot, as you did mistake

The outward composition of his body.

What you have done hath not offended me:

Nor other satisfaction do I crave,

But only, with your patience 497 , that we may

Taste of your wine and see what cates 498 you have,

For soldiers’ stomachs 499 always serve them well.

COUNTESS With all my heart, and think me honourèd

To feast so great a warrior in my house.
