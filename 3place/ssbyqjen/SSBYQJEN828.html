Scene 2 113 / running scene 2

Enter Richard, Edward and Montague

RICHARD Brother, though I be youngest, give me leave 114 .

EDWARD No, I can better play the orator.

MONTAGUE But I have reasons strong and forcible 115 .

Enter the Duke of York

YORK Why, how now, sons and brother, at a strife?

What is your quarrel? How began it first?

EDWARD No quarrel, but a slight contention.

YORK About what?

RICHARD About that which concerns your grace and us:

The crown of England, father, which is yours.

YORK Mine boy? Not till King Henry be dead.

RICHARD Your right depends not on his life or death.

EDWARD Now you are heir: therefore enjoy it now.

By giving the House of Lancaster leave to breathe 116 ,

It will outrun 117 you, father, in the end.

YORK I took an oath that he should quietly 118 reign.

EDWARD But for a kingdom any oath may be broken:

I would break a thousand oaths to reign one year.

RICHARD No: God forbid your grace should be forsworn 119 .

YORK I shall be, if I claim by open war.

RICHARD I’ll prove the contrary, if you’ll hear me speak.

YORK Thou canst not, son: it is impossible.

RICHARD An oath is of no moment 120 , being not took

Before a true and lawful magistrate,

That hath authority over him that swears.

Henry had none, but did usurp the place.

Then, seeing ’twas he that made you to depose 121 ,

Your oath, my lord, is vain 122 and frivolous 123 .

Therefore to arms: and, father, do but think

How sweet a thing it is to wear a crown,

Within whose circuit 124 is Elysium 125

And all that poets feign 126 of bliss and joy.

Why do we linger thus? I cannot rest

Until the white rose that I wear be dyed 127

Even in the lukewarm blood of Henry’s heart.

YORK Richard, enough: I will be king or die.

Brother, thou shalt to London presently 128 ,

And whet on 129 Warwick to this enterprise.

Thou, Richard, shalt to the Duke of Norfolk,

And tell him privily 130 of our intent.

You Edward, shall unto my lord Cobham,

With whom the Kentishmen will willingly rise 131 .

In them I trust, for they are soldiers,

Witty 132 , courteous, liberal 133 , full of spirit 134 .

While you are thus employed, what resteth more 135 ,

But that I seek occasion 136 how to rise,

And yet the king not privy to my drift 137 ,

Nor any of the House of Lancaster?

Enter a Messenger

But stay 138 . What news? Why com’st thou in such post 139 ?

MESSENGER The queen with all the northern earls and lords

Intend here to besiege you in your castle.

She is hard 140 by with twenty thousand men,

And therefore fortify your hold 141 , my lord.

[Exit ]

YORK Ay, with my sword. What, think’st thou that we fear them?

Edward and Richard, you shall stay with me,

My brother Montague shall post 142 to London.

Let noble Warwick, Cobham and the rest,

Whom we have left protectors of the king,

With powerful policy 143 strengthen themselves,

And trust not simple 144 Henry nor his oaths.

MONTAGUE Brother, I go: I’ll win them, fear it not.

And thus most humbly I do take my leave.

Exit

Enter [John ] Mortimer and his brother [Hugh ]

YORK Sir John and Sir Hugh Mortimer, mine uncles,

You are come to Sandal in a happy 145 hour.

The army of the queen mean to besiege us.

JOHN MORTIMER She shall not need 146 : we’ll meet her in the field.

YORK What, with five thousand men?

RICHARD Ay, with five hundred, father, for a need 147 .

A woman’s general: what should we fear?

A march afar off

EDWARD I hear their drums: let’s set our men in order,

And issue forth and bid them battle straight 148 .

YORK Five men to twenty: though the odds be great,

I doubt not, uncle, of our victory.

Many a battle have I won in France,

Whenas 149 the enemy hath been ten to one.

Why should I not now have the like 150 success?

Alarum. Exeunt
