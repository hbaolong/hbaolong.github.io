Scene 5 / running scene 5 continues

Alarum. Enter King Henry alone

KING HENRY VI This battle fares like to the morning’s war,

When dying clouds contend with growing light,

What 517 time the shepherd, blowing of his nails 518 ,

Can neither call it perfect 519 day nor night.

Now sways it this way, like a mighty sea

Forced by the tide to combat with the wind.

Now sways it that way, like the selfsame sea

Forced to retire by fury of the wind.

Sometime the flood 520 prevails, and then the wind,

Now one the better, then another best;

Both tugging to be victors, breast to breast,

Yet neither conqueror nor conquerèd:

So is the equal poise 521 of this fell 522 war.

Here on this molehill will I sit me down.

To whom God will, there be the victory.

For Margaret my queen, and Clifford too,

Have chid 523 me from the battle, swearing both

They prosper best of all when I am thence.

Would I were dead, if God’s good will were so;

For what is in this world but grief and woe?

O, God! Methinks it were a happy life,

To be no better than a homely swain 524 ,

To sit upon a hill, as I do now,

To carve out dials 525 quaintly 526 , point by point,

Thereby to see the minutes how they run:

How many makes the hour full complete,

How many hours brings about 527 the day,

How many days will finish up the year,

How many years a mortal man may live.

When this is known, then to divide the times:

So many hours must I tend my flock,

So many hours must I take my rest,

So many hours must I contemplate,

So many hours must I sport 528 myself,

So many days my ewes have been with young 529 ,

So many weeks ere the poor fools will ean 530 ,

So many years ere I shall shear the fleece.

So minutes, hours, days, months and years,

Passed over to the end they 531 were created,

Would bring white hairs unto a quiet grave.

Ah, what a life were this! How sweet! How lovely!

Gives not the hawthorn bush a sweeter shade

To shepherds looking on their silly 532 sheep,

Than doth a rich embroidered canopy 533

To kings that fear their subjects’ treachery?

O, yes, it doth; a thousand-fold it doth.

And to conclude, the shepherd’s homely curds 534 ,

His cold thin drink out of his leather bottle,

His wonted 535 sleep under a fresh tree’s shade,

All which secure 536 and sweetly he enjoys,

Is far beyond a prince’s delicates 537 ,

His viands 538 sparkling in a golden cup,

His body couchèd in a curious 539 bed,

When care 540 , mistrust and treason waits on 541 him.

Alarum. Enter a Son that has killed his father, at one door, and a Father that hath killed his son at another door [with their bodies ]

SON Ill blows the wind that profits nobody.

This man, whom hand to hand I slew in fight,

May be possessèd with 542 some store of crowns 543 ,

And I, that haply 544 take them from him now,

May yet ere night yield both my life and them

To some man else, as this dead man doth me.

Who’s this? O, God! It is my father’s face,

Whom in this conflict I unwares 545 have killed.

O heavy 546 times, begetting such events!

From London by the king was I pressed 547 forth.

My father, being the Earl of Warwick’s man 548 ,

Came on the part 549 of York, pressed by his master.

And I, who at his hands received my life,

Have by my hands of life bereavèd him.

Pardon me, God, I knew not what I did.

And pardon, father, for I knew not thee.

My tears shall wipe away these bloody marks,

And no more words till they have flowed their fill.

KING HENRY VI O, piteous spectacle! O, bloody times!

Whiles lions war and battle for their dens,

Poor harmless lambs abide 550 their enmity.

Weep, wretched man: I’ll aid thee tear for tear,

And let our hearts and eyes, like civil war,

Be blind with tears and break o’ercharged 551 with grief.

[The ] Father [steps forward ], bearing of his Son

FATHER Thou that so stoutly 552 hath resisted me,

Give me thy gold, if thou hast any gold,

For I have bought it with an hundred blows.

But let me see: is this our foeman’s face?

Ah, no, no, no, it is mine only son!

Ah, boy, if any life be left in thee,

Throw up 553 thine eye! See, see what showers arise,

Blown with the windy tempest of my heart,

Upon thy wounds, that kills mine eye and heart.

O, pity, God, this miserable age!

What stratagems 554 , how fell, how butcherly,

Erroneous 555 , mutinous and unnatural,

This deadly quarrel daily doth beget!

O boy, thy father gave thee life too soon,

And hath bereft thee of thy life too late 556 !

KING HENRY VI Woe above woe! Grief more than common grief!

O, that my death would stay 557 these ruthful 558 deeds!

O, pity, pity, gentle heaven, pity!

The red rose and the white are on his face 559 ,

The fatal colours of our striving houses:

The one his purple 560 blood right well resembles,

The other his pale cheeks, methinks, presenteth 561 .

Wither one rose, and let the other flourish.

If you contend 562 , a thousand lives must wither.

SON How will my mother for a father’s death

Take on with 563 me and ne’er be satisfied!

FATHER How will my wife for slaughter of my son

Shed seas of tears and ne’er be satisfied!

KING HENRY VI How will the country for these woeful chances 564

Misthink 565 the king and not be satisfied!

SON Was ever son so rued 566 a father’s death?

FATHER Was ever father so bemoaned his son?

KING HENRY VI Was ever king so grieved for subjects’ woe?

Much is your sorrow; mine ten times so much.

SON I’ll bear thee hence, where I may weep my fill.

[Exit with the body ]

FATHER These arms of mine shall be thy winding-sheet 567 ,

My heart, sweet boy, shall be thy sepulchre,

For from my heart thine image ne’er shall go.

My sighing breast shall be thy funeral bell;

And so obsequious 568 will thy father be,

E’en for the loss of thee, having no more,

As Priam was for all his valiant sons 569 .

I’ll bear thee hence; and let them fight that will,

For I have murdered where I should not kill.

Exit [with the body ]

KING HENRY VI Sad-hearted men, much overgone 570 with care,

Here sits a king more woeful than you are.

Alarums. Excursions. Enter the Queen, the Prince and Exeter

PRINCE EDWARD Fly, father, fly! For all your friends are fled,

And Warwick rages like a chafèd 571 bull:

Away, for death doth hold us in pursuit.

QUEEN MARGARET Mount you, my lord, towards Berwick 572 post amain 573 .

Edward and Richard, like a brace 574 of greyhounds

Having the fearful flying hare in sight,

With fiery eyes sparkling for very 575 wrath,

And bloody steel grasped in their ireful hands,

Are at our backs, and therefore hence amain 576 .

EXETER Away, for vengeance comes along with them.

Nay, stay not to expostulate 577 , make speed,

Or else come after. I’ll away before.

KING HENRY VI Nay, take me with thee, good sweet Exeter:

Not that I fear to stay, but love to go

Whither the queen intends. Forward, away!

Exeunt
