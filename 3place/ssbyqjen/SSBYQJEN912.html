Enter Cleon the governor of Tarsus, with his wife [Dionyza ] and others

CLEON My Dionyza, shall we rest us here

And by relating tales of others’ griefs

See if ’twill teach us to forget our own?

DIONYZA That were to blow at fire in hope to quench it.

For who digs 244 hills because they do aspire,

Throws down one mountain to cast up a higher.

O, my distressèd lord, even such our griefs are:

Here they are but felt, and seen with mischief’s eyes 245 ,

But like to groves, being topped 246 they higher rise.

CLEON O, Dionyza,

Who wanteth 247 food and will not say he wants it,

Or can conceal his hunger till he famish?

Our tongues our sorrows do sound 248 deep,

Our woes into the air, our eyes to weep

Till tongues fetch breath that may proclaim them 249 louder,

That if heaven slumber while their creatures want,

They may awake their helpers to comfort them.

I’ll then discourse 250 our woes, felt several years,

And wanting breath to speak 251 , help me with tears.

DIONYZA I’ll do my best, sir.

CLEON This Tarsus, o’er which I have the government,

A city o’er whom plenty held full hand 252 ,

For riches 253 strewed herself even in her streets,

Whose towers bore heads so high they kissed the clouds,

And strangers ne’er beheld, but wondered at 254 .

Whose men and dames so jetted 255 and adorned 256 ,

Like one another’s glass to trim them by 257 ,

Their tables were stored full to glad 258 the sight,

And not so much to feed on as delight.

All poverty was scorned, and pride so great

The name of help grew odious to repeat 259 .

DIONYZA O, ’tis too true.

CLEON But see what heaven can do by this our change 260 .

These mouths who but of late earth, sea and air

Were all too little to content and please,

Although they gave their creatures in abundance,

As houses are defiled for want 261 of use,

They are now starved for want of exercise.

Those palates who, not yet two summers younger 262 ,

Must have inventions 263 to delight the taste

Would now be glad of bread and beg for it.

Those mothers who to nuzzle up 264 their babes

Thought naught too curious 265 , are ready now

To eat those little darlings whom they loved.

So sharp are hunger’s teeth, that man and wife,

Draw lots who first shall die to lengthen life 266 .

Here stands a lord, and there a lady, weeping.

Here many sink, yet those which see them fall,

Have scarce strength left to give them burial.

Is not this true?

DIONYZA Our cheeks and hollow eyes do witness it.

CLEON O, let those cities that of plenty’s cup

And her prosperities so largely 267 taste

With their superfluous riots 268 , hear these tears 269 !

The misery of Tarsus may be theirs.

Enter a Lord

LORD Where’s the lord governor?

CLEON Here.

Speak out thy sorrows, which thou bring’st in haste,

For comfort is too far for us to expect.

LORD We have descried 270 upon our neighbouring shore,

A portly 271 sail 272 of ships make hitherward 273 .

CLEON I thought as much.

One sorrow never comes but brings an heir,

That may succeed as his inheritor.

And so in ours: some neighbouring nation,

Taking advantage of our misery,

Hath stuffed the hollow vessels with their power 274 ,

To beat us down, the which are down already,

And make a conquest of unhappy 275 me,

Whereas no glory’s got to overcome 276 .

LORD That’s the least fear 277 , for by the semblance

Of their white flags 278 displayed, they bring us peace,

And come to us as favourers 279 , not as foes.

CLEON Thou speak’st like him’s untutored to repeat 280 :

Who 281 makes the fairest show means most deceit.

But bring they what they will and what they can,

What need we fear?

The ground’s the lowest, and we are halfway there 282 .

Go tell their general we attend him here,

To know from whence he comes and what he craves.

LORD I go, my lord.

[Exit ]

CLEON Welcome is peace, if he on peace consist 283 ,

If wars, we are unable to resist.

Enter Pericles with attendants

PERICLES Lord governor, for so we hear you are,

Let not our ships and number of our men

Be like a beacon 284 fired t’amaze 285 your eyes.

We have heard your miseries as far as Tyre,

And seen the desolation of your streets.

Nor come we to add sorrow to your tears 286 ,

But to relieve them of their heavy load,

And these our ships you happily 287 may think

Are like the Trojan horse was stuffed within

With bloody veins expecting overthrow,

Are stored with corn to make your needy bread 288 ,

And give them life whom hunger starved half dead.

ALL TARSIANS The gods of Greece protect you,

They kneel

And we’ll pray for you.

PERICLES Arise, I pray you, rise.

They rise

We do not look for reverence but for love,

And harbourage for ourself, our ships and men.

CLEON The which when any shall not gratify 289 ,

Or pay you with unthankfulness in thought 290 ,

Be it our wives, our children or ourselves,

The curse of heaven and men succeed 291 their evils!

Till when—the which, I hope, shall ne’er be seen—

Your grace is welcome to our town and us.

PERICLES Which welcome we’ll accept, feast here awhile,

Until our stars 292 that frown, lend us a smile.

Exeunt
