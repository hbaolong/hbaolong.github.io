Scene 1 ／ running scene 1 15

Enter Sampson and Gregory with swords and bucklers 16 , of the House of Capulet

SAMPSON Gregory, o’my word, we’ll not carry coals 17 .

GREGORY No, for then we should be colliers 18 .

SAMPSON I mean, if we be in choler 19 , we’ll draw 20 .

GREGORY Ay, while you live, draw your neck out o’th’collar 21 .

SAMPSON I strike 22 quickly, being moved 23 .

GREGORY But thou art not quickly moved to strike.

SAMPSON A dog of the house of Montague moves me.

GREGORY To move is to stir 24 ; and to be valiant 25 is to stand 26 : therefore, if thou art moved, thou runn’st away.

SAMPSON A dog of that house shall move me to stand. I will take the wall 27 of any man or maid of Montague’s.

GREGORY That shows thee a weak slave 28 , for the weakest goes to the wall 29 .

SAMPSON True, and therefore women being the weaker vessels are ever thrust to the wall 30 : therefore I will push Montague’s men 31 from the wall, and thrust his maids to the wall.

GREGORY The quarrel is between our masters and us their men.

SAMPSON ’Tis all one 32 , I will show myself a tyrant: when I have fought with the men, I will be civil with the maids, and cut off their heads.

GREGORY The heads of the maids?

SAMPSON Ay, the heads of the maids, or their maidenheads 33 , take 34 it in what sense 35 thou wilt.

GREGORY They must take it in sense that feel it.

SAMPSON Me they shall feel while I am able to stand 36 , and ’tis known I am a pretty piece of flesh 37 .

GREGORY ’Tis well thou art not fish 38 : if thou hadst, thou hadst been poor John 39 . Draw thy tool 40 , here comes of the house of the Montagues.

Enter two other Servingmen [Abraham and Balthasar ]

Sampson draws

SAMPSON My naked weapon 41 is out. Quarrel, I will back thee.

GREGORY How 42 , turn thy back and run?

SAMPSON Fear 43 me not.

GREGORY No, marry 44 , I fear thee!

SAMPSON Let us take the law of 45 our sides: let them begin.

GREGORY I will frown as I pass by, and let them take it as they list 46 .

Frowns

SAMPSON Nay, as they dare. I will bite my thumb 47 at them, which is a disgrace to them if they bear it.

Bites his thumb

ABRAHAM Do you bite your thumb at us, sir?

SAMPSON I do bite my thumb, sir.

ABRAHAM Do you bite your thumb at us, sir?

SAMPSON Is the law of our side, if I say ay?

Aside

GREGORY No.

SAMPSON No, sir, I do not bite my thumb at you, sir, but I bite my thumb, sir.

GREGORY Do you quarrel, sir?

ABRAHAM Quarrel sir? No, sir.

SAMPSON If you do, sir, I am for you. I serve as good a man as you.

ABRAHAM No better?

SAMPSON Well, sir.

Enter Benvolio

GREGORY Say ‘better’— here comes one of my master’s kinsmen.

Aside

SAMPSON Yes, better.

ABRAHAM You lie.

SAMPSON Draw, if you be men. Gregory, remember thy swashing 48 blow.

They fight

BENVOLIO Part, fools! Put up your swords, you know not what you do.

Draws and parts them

Enter Tybalt

TYBALT What, art thou drawn among these heartless hinds 49 ?

Turn thee, Benvolio, look upon thy death.

Draws

BENVOLIO I do but keep the peace. Put up thy sword,

Or manage 50 it to part these men with me.

TYBALT What, drawn, and talk of peace? I hate the word,

As I hate hell, all Montagues, and thee.

Have at thee, coward!

Fight

Enter three or four Citizens with clubs

OFFICER Clubs, bills and partisans 51 ! Strike! Beat them down! Down with the Capulets!

Down with the Montagues!

Enter Old Capulet in his gown 52 , and his Wife

CAPULET What noise is this? Give me my long sword 53 , ho!

LADY CAPULET A crutch, a crutch! Why call you for a sword?

CAPULET My sword, I say! Old Montague is come,

And flourishes his blade in spite of 54 me.

Enter Old Montague and his Wife

MONTAGUE Thou villain Capulet!— Hold me not, let me go.

LADY MONTAGUE Thou shalt not stir a foot to seek a foe.

Enter Prince Escalus with his train

PRINCE Rebellious subjects, enemies to peace,

Profaners of this neighbour-stainèd steel 55 —

Will they not hear?— What, ho! You men, you beasts,

That quench the fire of your pernicious 56 rage

With purple fountains issuing from your veins:

On pain of torture, from those bloody hands

Throw your mistempered 57 weapons to the ground,

And hear the sentence of your movèd 58 prince.

Three civil broils, bred of an airy word,

By thee, Old Capulet, and Montague,

Have thrice disturbed the quiet of our streets,

And made Verona’s ancient 59 citizens

Cast by their grave beseeming ornaments 60

To wield Old partisans, in hands as old,

Cankered 61 with peace, to part your cankered hate:

If ever you disturb our streets again,

Your lives shall pay the forfeit of the peace 62 .

For this time, all the rest depart away:

You Capulet, shall go along with me,

And, Montague, come you this afternoon,

To know our further pleasure 63 in this case,

To old Freetown, our common judgement-place.

Once more, on pain of death, all men depart.

Exeunt. [Montague, Lady Montague and Benvolio remain ]

MONTAGUE Who set this ancient quarrel new abroach 64 ?

Speak, nephew, were you by 65 when it began?

BENVOLIO Here were the servants of your adversary,

And yours, close fighting ere 66 I did approach:

I drew to part them: in the instant came

The fiery Tybalt, with his sword prepared 67 ,

Which, as he breathed defiance to my ears,

He swung about his head and cut the winds,

Who nothing hurt withal 68 hissed him in scorn:

While we were interchanging thrusts and blows,

Came more and more, and fought on part and part 69 ,

Till the prince came, who parted either part.

LADY MONTAGUE O, where is Romeo? Saw you him today?

Right glad am I he was not at this fray.

BENVOLIO Madam, an hour before the worshipped sun

Peered forth 70 the golden window of the east,

A troubled mind drave 71 me to walk abroad 72 ,

Where, underneath the grove of sycamore

That westward rooteth from the city side 73 ,

So early walking did I see your son:

Towards him I made, but he was ware 74 of me

And stole into the covert 75 of the wood:

I, measuring his affections 76 by my own,

Which then most sought where most might not be found 77 ,

Being one too many by my weary self,

Pursued my humour 78 , not pursuing his,

And gladly shunned who 79 gladly fled from me.

MONTAGUE Many a morning hath he there been seen,

With tears augmenting the fresh morning’s dew,

Adding to clouds more clouds with his deep sighs,

But all so soon as the all-cheering sun

Should in the farthest east begin to draw

The shady curtains from Aurora 80 ’s bed,

Away from light steals home my heavy 81 son 82 ,

And private in his chamber pens himself,

Shuts up his windows, locks fair daylight out

And makes himself an artificial night:

Black 83 and portentous must this humour 84 prove,

Unless good counsel may the cause remove.

BENVOLIO My noble uncle, do you know the cause?

MONTAGUE I neither know it nor can learn of 85 him.

BENVOLIO Have you importuned him by any means 86 ?

MONTAGUE Both by myself and many other friends,

But he, his own affections’ counsellor,

Is to himself — I will not say how true 87 —

But to himself so secret and so close 88 ,

So far from sounding 89 and discovery,

As is the bud bit with an envious 90 worm 91 ,

Ere he can spread his sweet leaves to the air,

Or dedicate his beauty to the same.

Could we but learn from whence his sorrows grow,

We would as willingly give cure as know.

Enter Romeo

BENVOLIO See, where he comes. So please you, step aside,

I’ll know his grievance, or be much denied.

MONTAGUE I would thou wert so happy by thy stay 92

To hear true shrift 93 .— Come, madam, let’s away.

Exeunt [Montague and Lady Montague ]

BENVOLIO Good morrow, cousin 94 .

ROMEO Is the day so young?

BENVOLIO But new struck nine.

ROMEO Ay me, sad hours seem long.

Was that my father that went hence so fast?

BENVOLIO It was. What sadness lengthens Romeo’s hours?

ROMEO Not having that, which, having, makes them short.

BENVOLIO In love?

ROMEO Out—

BENVOLIO Of love?

ROMEO Out of her favour where I am in love.

BENVOLIO Alas that love, so gentle in his view 95 ,

Should be so tyrannous and rough in proof 96 !

ROMEO Alas that love, whose view is muffled 97 still,

Should, without eyes, see pathways to his will!

Where shall we dine? O me! What fray was here?

Yet tell me not, for I have heard it all.

Here’s much to do with hate, but more with love:

Why, then, O brawling love, O loving hate,

O anything of nothing first create 98 !

O heavy lightness, serious vanity 99 ,

Misshapen chaos of well-seeming 100 forms,

Feather of lead, bright smoke, cold fire, sick health,

Still-waking 101 sleep that is not what it is!

This love feel I, that feel no love in this.

Dost thou not laugh?

BENVOLIO No, coz, I rather weep.

ROMEO Good heart, at what?

BENVOLIO At thy good heart’s oppression.

ROMEO Why, such is love’s transgression.

Griefs of mine own lie heavy in my breast,

Which thou wilt propagate 102 , to have it pressed

With more of thine: this love 103 that thou hast shown

Doth add more grief to too much of mine own.

Love is a smoke made with the fume of sighs,

Being purged 104 , a fire sparkling in lovers’ eyes,

Being vexed 105 , a sea nourished with loving tears.

What is it else? A madness most discreet 106 ,

A choking gall 107 and a preserving sweet.

Farewell, my coz 108 .

BENVOLIO Soft 109 , I will go along,

An if 110 you leave me so, you do me wrong.

ROMEO Tut, I have lost myself, I am not here:

This is not Romeo, he’s some other where.

BENVOLIO Tell me in sadness 111 , who is that you love.

ROMEO What, shall I groan and tell thee?

BENVOLIO Groan? Why, no. But sadly tell me who.

ROMEO A sick man in sadness makes his will:

A word ill-urged to one that is so ill.

In sadness, cousin, I do love a woman.

BENVOLIO I aimed so near 112 , when I supposed you loved.

ROMEO A right good mark-man 113 ! And she’s fair I love.

BENVOLIO A right fair mark 114 , fair coz, is soonest hit.

ROMEO Well, in that hit you miss: she’ll not be hit

With Cupid’s arrow 115 , she hath Dian’s wit 116 ,

And in strong proof 117 of chastity well armed,

From love’s 118 weak childish bow she lives uncharmed 119 .

She will not stay 120 the siege of loving terms,

Nor bide 121 th’ encounter 122 of assailing eyes,

Nor ope her lap to saint-seducing gold 123 :

O, she is rich in beauty, only poor

That when she dies with beauty dies her store 124 .

BENVOLIO Then she hath sworn that she will still 125 live chaste?

ROMEO She hath, and in that sparing 126 makes huge waste,

For beauty starved 127 with her severity

Cuts beauty off from all posterity.

She is too fair 128 , too wise, wisely too fair,

To merit bliss by making me despair 129 .

She hath forsworn to 130 love, and in that vow

Do I live dead that live to tell it now.

BENVOLIO Be ruled by me, forget to think of her.

ROMEO O, teach me how I should forget to think.

BENVOLIO By giving liberty unto thine eyes:

Examine other beauties.

ROMEO ’Tis the way

To call hers exquisite, in question more 131 :

These happy 132 masks that kiss fair ladies’ brows

Being black puts us in mind they hide the fair.

He that is strucken 133 blind cannot forget

The precious treasure of his eyesight lost:

Show me a mistress that is passing 134 fair,

What doth her beauty serve, but as a note 135

Where I may read who passed 136 that passing fair?

Farewell, thou canst not teach me to forget.

BENVOLIO I’ll pay that doctrine 137 , or else die in debt.

Exeunt
