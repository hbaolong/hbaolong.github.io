Scene 1 1 / running scene 1

Enter the King, Lord John of Lancaster, [the ] Earl of Westmorland, with others

KING HENRY IV So shaken as we 2 are, so wan 3 with care,

Find we 4 a time for frighted 5 peace to pant,

And breathe short-winded accents 6 of new broils 7

To be commenced in strands afar remote 8 .

No more the thirsty entrance 9 of this soil

Shall daub 10 her lips with her own children’s blood.

No more shall trenching 11 war channel her fields,

Nor bruise her flow’rets 12 with the armèd 13 hoofs

Of hostile paces 14 . Those opposèd 15 eyes,

Which, like the meteors 16 of a troubled heaven,

All of one nature, of one substance bred,

Did lately meet in the intestine shock 17

And furious close 18 of civil butchery

Shall now, in mutual well-beseeming 19 ranks,

March all one way and be no more opposed

Against acquaintance, kindred and allies.

The edge of war, like an ill-sheathèd knife,

No more shall cut his master. Therefore, friends,

As far as to the sepulchre of 20 Christ —

Whose soldier now, under whose blessèd cross

We are impressèd 21 and engaged 22 to fight —

Forthwith a power 23 of English shall we levy 24 ,

Whose arms 25 were moulded in their mother’s 26 womb

To chase these pagans in those holy fields 27

Over whose acres walked those blessèd feet 28

Which fourteen hundred years ago were nailed

For our advantage on the bitter 29 cross.

But this our purpose is a twelvemonth old,

And bootless 30 ’tis to tell you we will go:

Therefore we meet not now.— Then let me hear

Of you, my gentle 31 cousin 32 Westmorland,

What yesternight 33 our council did decree

In forwarding this dear 34 expedience 35 .

WESTMORLAND My liege 36 , this haste was hot in question 37 ,

And many limits of the charge 38 set down

But 39 yesternight, when all athwart 40 there came

A post 41 from Wales loaden 42 with heavy 43 news;

Whose worst was that the noble Mortimer,

Leading the men of Herefordshire to fight

Against the irregular and wild Glendower 44 ,

Was by the rude 45 hands of that Welshman taken,

And a thousand of his people butcherèd,

Upon whose dead corpse 46 there was such misuse,

Such beastly shameless transformation 47 ,

By those Welshwomen done as may not be

Without much shame retold or spoken of.

KING HENRY IV It seems then that the tidings 48 of this broil

Brake 49 off our business for the Holy Land.

WESTMORLAND This matched with 50 other like, my gracious lord.

Far more uneven 51 and unwelcome news

Came from the north and thus it did report:

On Holy 52 Rood day, the gallant Hotspur 53 there,

Young Harry Percy, and brave Archibald,

That ever-valiant and approvèd 54 Scot,

At Holmedon 55 met, where they did spend

A sad 56 and bloody hour,

As by discharge of their artillery,

And shape of likelihood 57 , the news was told,

For he that brought them, in the very heat

And pride of their contention 58 did take horse,

Uncertain of the issue 59 any 60 way.

KING HENRY IV Here 61 is a dear and true industrious friend,

Sir Walter Blunt, new lighted 62 from his horse,

Stained with the variation of each 63 soil

Betwixt 64 that Holmedon and this seat 65 of ours,

And he hath brought us smooth 66 and welcome news.

The Earl of Douglas is discomfited 67 ,

Ten thousand bold Scots, two and twenty knights,

Balked 68 in their own blood did Sir Walter see

On Holmedon’s plains. Of prisoners, Hotspur took

Mordake, Earl of Fife, and eldest son

To beaten Douglas 69 , and the Earl of Athol,

Of Murray, Angus, and Menteith 70 .

And is not this an honourable spoil 71 ?

A gallant 72 prize? Ha, cousin, is it not?

WESTMORLAND In faith, it is a conquest for a prince to boast of.

KING HENRY IV Yea, there thou mak’st me sad and mak’st me sin

In envy that my Lord Northumberland

Should be the father of so blest a son:

A son who is the theme 73 of honour’s tongue;

Amongst a grove, the very straightest plant 74 ,

Who is sweet Fortune’s minion 75 and her pride,

Whilst I, by looking on the praise of him,

See riot 76 and dishonour stain the brow

Of my young Harry. O, that it could be proved

That some night-tripping 77 fairy 78 had exchanged

In cradle-clothes our children where they lay,

And called mine Percy, his Plantagenet 79 :

Then would I have his Harry, and he mine.

But let him from 80 my thoughts. What think you, coz 81 ,

Of this young Percy’s pride? The prisoners,

Which he in this adventure 82 hath surprised 83 ,

To his own use he keeps, and sends me word

I shall have none but Mordake Earl of Fife.

WESTMORLAND This is his uncle’s teaching. This is Worcester,

Malevolent to you in all aspects,

Which makes him prune 84 himself, and bristle 85 up

The crest of youth against your dignity 86 .

KING HENRY IV But I have sent for him to answer this.

And for this cause 87 awhile we must neglect

Our holy purpose to Jerusalem.

Cousin, on Wednesday next our council we

Will hold at Windsor, and so inform the lords.

But come yourself with speed to us again,

For more is to be said and to be done

Than out of anger can be utterèd 88 .

WESTMORLAND I will, my liege.

Exeunt
