Enter Pericles with his Lords

PERICLES Let none disturb us!

[Exeunt the Lords ]

Why should this change of thoughts 149 ,

The sad companion, dull-eyed 150 melancholy,

Be my so used 151 a guest as 152 not an hour

In the day’s glorious walk 153 or peaceful night,

The tomb where grief should sleep, can breed me quiet 154 ?

Here pleasures court mine eyes and mine eyes shun them,

And danger which I feared is at Antioch,

Whose arm seems far too short to hit me here.

Yet neither pleasure’s art can joy 155 my spirits 156 ,

Nor yet the other’s 157 distance comfort me.

Then it is thus: the passions of the mind 158 ,

That have their first conception by misdread 159 ,

Have after-nourishment and life by care 160 ,

And what was first but fear what might be done,

Grows elder now, and cares 161 it be not done.

And so with me. The great Antiochus,

Gainst whom I am too little to contend,

Since he’s so great can 162 make his will his act 163 ,

Will think me speaking though I swear to silence.

Nor boots it me 164 to say ‘I honour’,

If he suspect I may dishonour him.

And what may make him blush in being known,

He’ll stop the course 165 by which it might be known.

With hostile forces he’ll o’erspread the land,

And with th’ostent 166 of war will look so huge,

Amazement 167 shall drive courage from the state,

Our men be vanquished ere 168 they do resist,

And subjects punished that ne’er thought offence 169 .

Which care of them, not pity of myself,

Who am no more but as the tops of trees,

Which fence 170 the roots they grow by and defend them 171 ,

Makes both my body pine and soul to languish,

And punish that before that he would punish 172 .

PERICLES Drew sleep out of mine eyes, blood from my cheeks,

HELICANUS Well, my lord, since you have given me leave to speak,

And justly too, I think, you fear the tyrant
