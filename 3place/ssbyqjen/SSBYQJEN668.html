Scene 2 152 ／running scene 2

Enter Gaunt and Duchess of Gloucester

GAUNT Alas, the part I had in Gloucester’s blood 153

Doth more solicit 154 me than your exclaims 155 ,

To stir 156 against the butchers of his life.

But since correction lieth in those hands 157

Which made the fault that we cannot correct,

Put we our quarrel to the will of heaven,

Who, when they see the hours ripe on earth,

Will rain hot vengeance on offenders’ heads.

DUCHESS Finds brotherhood in thee no sharper spur?

Hath love in thy old blood no living fire?

Edward’s 158 seven sons, whereof thyself art one,

Were as seven vials of his sacred blood,

Or seven fair branches springing from one root:

Some of those seven are dried by nature’s course,

Some of those branches by the Destinies 159 cut.

But Thomas, my dear lord, my life, my Gloucester,

One vial full of Edward’s sacred blood,

One flourishing branch of his most royal root,

Is cracked, and all the precious liquor 160 spilt,

Is hacked down, and his summer leaves all faded,

By envy’s 161 hand and murder’s bloody axe.

Ah, Gaunt, his blood was thine! That bed, that womb,

That metal 162 , that self-mould 163 that fashioned thee

Made him a man. And though thou liv’st and breath’st,

Yet art thou slain in him. Thou dost consent 164

In some large measure to thy father’s death,

In that thou see’st thy wretched brother die,

Who was the model 165 of thy father’s life.

Call it not patience, Gaunt, it is despair.

In suff’ring 166 thus thy brother to be slaughtered,

Thou show’st the naked 167 pathway to thy life,

Teaching stern murder how to butcher thee.

That which in mean 168 men we entitle patience

Is pale cold cowardice in noble breasts.

What shall I say? To safeguard thine own life,

The best way is to venge 169 my Gloucester’s death.

GAUNT Heaven’s is the quarrel, for heaven’s substitute,

His deputy 170 anointed in his sight 171 ,

Hath caused his death, the which if wrongfully,

Let heaven revenge, for I may never lift

An angry arm against his minister.

DUCHESS Where then, alas, may I complaint myself 172 ?

GAUNT To heaven, the widow’s champion 173 to defence.

DUCHESS Why, then, I will. Farewell, old Gaunt.

Thou go’st to Coventry, there to behold

Our cousin 174 Hereford and fell 175 Mowbray fight.

O, sit my husband’s wrongs on Hereford’s spear,

That it may enter butcher Mowbray’s breast!

Or if misfortune miss the first career 176 ,

Be Mowbray’s sins so heavy in his bosom,

That they may break his foaming courser 177 ’s back,

And throw the rider headlong in the lists 178 ,

A caitiff 179 recreant 180 to my cousin Hereford!

Farewell, old Gaunt: thy sometimes 181 brother’s wife

With her companion grief must end her life.

GAUNT Sister, farewell. I must to Coventry.

As much good stay with thee as go with me!

DUCHESS Yet one word more: grief boundeth 182 where it falls,

Not with the empty hollowness, but weight.

I take my leave before I have begun,

For sorrow ends not when it seemeth done.

Commend me to my brother 183 , Edmund York.

Lo 184 , this is all. Nay, yet depart not so:

Though this be all, do not so quickly go.

I shall remember more. Bid him —— O, what? ——

With all good speed at Plashy 185 visit me.

Alack, and what shall good old York there see

But empty lodgings and unfurnished walls,

Unpeopled offices 186 , untrodden stones?

And what hear there for welcome but my groans?

Therefore commend me, let him not come there

To seek out sorrow that dwells everywhere.

Desolate, desolate, will I hence and die:

The last leave of thee takes my weeping eye.

Exeunt
