Scene 3 198 / running scene 3

Enter three or four Petitioners 199 , [Peter ] the armourer’s man 200 being one

FIRST PETITIONER My masters 201 , let’s stand close 202 : my Lord Protector will come this way by and by, and then we may deliver our supplications in the quill 203 .

SECOND PETITIONER Marry, the Lord protect 204 him, for he’s a good man, Jesu bless him.

Enter Suffolk and Queen [Margaret ]

FIRST PETITIONER Here a 205 comes, methinks, and the queen with him. I’ll be the first, sure.

He goes to meet Suffolk and the Queen

SECOND PETITIONER Come back, fool: this is the Duke of Suffolk, and not my Lord Protector.

SUFFOLK How now, fellow: wouldst 206 anything with me?

FIRST PETITIONER I pray, my lord, pardon me: I took ye for my Lord Protector.

QUEEN MARGARET ‘To my Lord Protector!’— Are your supplications to his lordship?

Reading

Let me see them: what is thine?

FIRST PETITIONER Mine is, an’t 207 please your grace, against John Goodman, my lord cardinal’s man 208 , for keeping my house and lands and wife and all from me.

SUFFOLK Thy wife too? That’s some wrong indeed.— What’s yours?

To Second Petitioner

What’s here?— ‘Against the Duke of Suffolk, for enclosing the commons 209 of Melford.’— How now, Sir Knave?

Reading supplication

To Second Petitioner

SECOND PETITIONER Alas, sir, I am but a poor petitioner of our whole township.

PETER Against my master, Thomas Horner, for saying that the Duke of York was rightful heir to the crown.

Offering his petition

QUEEN MARGARET What say’st thou? Did the Duke of York say he was rightful heir to the crown?

PETER That my master was? No, forsooth 210 : my master said that he was, and that the king was an usurper.

SUFFOLK Who is there?

Enter Servant

Take this fellow in, and send for his master with a pursuivant 211 presently.— We’ll hear more of your matter before the king.

Exit [Servant with Peter ]

QUEEN MARGARET And as for you that love to be protected

Under the wings of our Protector’s grace,

Begin your suits anew, and sue to 212 him

Tear the supplication

Away, base cullions 213 !— Suffolk, let them go.

ALL [PETITIONERS ] Come, let’s be gone

Exeunt [Petitioners ]

QUEEN MARGARET My lord of Suffolk, say, is this the guise 214 ?

Is this the fashions 215 in the court of England?

Is this the government of Britain’s isle?

And this the royalty of Albion 216 ’s king?

What, shall King Henry be a pupil still

Under the surly Gloucester’s governance?

Am I a queen in title and in style 217 ,

And must be made a subject to a duke?

I tell thee, Pole 218 , when in the city Tours

Thou ran’st a-tilt 219 in honour of my love

And stol’st away the ladies’ hearts of France,

I thought King Henry had resemblèd thee

In courage, courtship and proportion 220 :

But all his mind is bent 221 to holiness,

To number 222 Ave-Maries 223 on his beads 224 :

His champions 225 are the prophets and apostles,

His weapons holy saws 226 of sacred writ,

His study is his tilt-yard 227 , and his loves

Are brazen images 228 of canonizèd saints.

I would the college of the cardinals 229

Would choose him Pope, and carry him to Rome,

And set the triple crown 230 upon his head:

That were a state 231 fit for his holiness 232 .

SUFFOLK Madam, be patient: as I was cause

Your highness came to England, so will I

In England work 233 your grace’s full content.

QUEEN MARGARET Beside the haughty Protector, have we Beaufort

The imperious churchman, Somerset, Buckingham,

And grumbling York: and not the least of these

But 234 can do more in England than the king.

SUFFOLK And he of these that can do most of all

Cannot do more in England than the Nevilles:

Salisbury and Warwick are no simple 235 peers.

QUEEN MARGARET Not all these lords do vex me half so much

As that proud dame, the Lord Protector’s wife:

She sweeps it 236 through the court with troops of ladies,

More like an empress than Duke Humphrey’s wife:

Strangers 237 in court do take her for the queen:

She bears a duke’s revenues 238 on her back 239 ,

And in her heart she scorns our poverty:

Shall I not live to be avenged on her?

Contemptuous 240 base-born callet 241 as she is,

She vaunted 242 ’mongst her minions 243 t’other day,

The very train of her worst wearing 244 gown

Was better worth 245 than all my father’s lands,

Till Suffolk gave two dukedoms for his daughter 246 .

SUFFOLK Madam, myself have limed a bush 247 for her,

And placed a choir of such enticing birds 248 ,

That she will light 249 to listen to the lays 250 ,

And never mount to trouble you again.

So let her rest: and, madam, list to me,

For I am bold to counsel you in this:

Although we fancy 251 not the cardinal,

Yet must we join with him and with the lords,

Till we have brought Duke Humphrey in disgrace.

As for the Duke of York, this late complaint 252

Will make but little for his benefit 253 :

So one by one we’ll weed 254 them all at last,

And you yourself shall steer the happy helm.

Sound a sennet 255 . Enter the King [Henry VI with Somerset and ] York [whispering on either side of him ], Duke Humphrey [of Gloucester ], Cardinal, Buckingham, Salisbury, Warwick and the Duchess [Eleanor ]

KING HENRY VI For my part, noble lords, I care not which:

Or 256 Somerset or York, all’s one to me.

YORK If York have ill demeaned 257 himself in France,

Then let him be denied the regentship 258 .

SOMERSET If Somerset be unworthy of the place,

Let York be regent: I will yield to him.

WARWICK Whether your grace be worthy, yea or no,

Dispute not that: York is the worthier.

CARDINAL Ambitious Warwick, let thy betters speak.

WARWICK The cardinal’s not my better in the field 259 .

BUCKINGHAM All in this presence 260 are thy betters, Warwick.

WARWICK Warwick may live to be the best of all.

SALISBURY Peace, son, and show some reason, Buckingham,

Why Somerset should be preferred in this.

QUEEN MARGARET Because the king, forsooth, will have it so.

GLOUCESTER Madam, the king is old enough himself

To give his censure 261 : these are no women’s matters.

QUEEN MARGARET If he be old enough, what needs your grace

To be Protector of his excellence?

GLOUCESTER Madam, I am Protector of the realm,

And at his pleasure will resign my place.

SUFFOLK Resign it then and leave 262 thine insolence.

Since thou wert king — as who is king but thou? —

The commonwealth hath daily run to wrack,

The dauphin 263 hath prevailed 264 beyond the seas,

And all the peers and nobles of the realm

Have been as bondmen 265 to thy sovereignty.

CARDINAL The commons hast thou racked 266 : the clergy’s bags 267

Are lank 268 and lean with thy extortions.

SOMERSET Thy sumptuous buildings and thy wife’s attire

Have cost a mass of public treasury 269 .

BUCKINGHAM Thy cruelty in execution

Upon offenders hath exceeded law,

And left thee to the mercy of the law.

QUEEN MARGARET Thy sale of offices 270 and towns in France,

If they were known, as the suspect 271 is great,

Would make thee quickly hop without thy head 272 .

Exit Gloucester

Queen Margaret drops her fan

Give me my fan: what, minion 273 , can ye not?

To Eleanor

She gives the Duchess [Eleanor ] a box on the ear

I cry you mercy 274 , madam: was it you?

ELEANOR Was’t I? Yea, I it was, proud Frenchwoman:

Could I come near your beauty with my nails,

I’d set my ten commandments 275 in your face.

KING HENRY VI Sweet aunt, be quiet: ’twas against her will 276 .

ELEANOR Against her will, good king? Look to’t 277 in time,

She’ll hamper 278 thee and dandle 279 thee like a baby:

Though in this place most master 280 wear no breeches,

She shall not strike Dame Eleanor unrevenged.

Exit Eleanor

BUCKINGHAM Lord Cardinal, I will follow Eleanor,

And listen after 281 Humphrey, how he proceeds:

She’s tickled 282 now, her fury needs no spurs,

She’ll gallop far enough to her destruction.

Exit Buckingham

Enter Humphrey [Duke of Gloucester ]

GLOUCESTER Now, lords, my choler being overblown

With walking once about the quadrangle,

I come to talk of commonwealth affairs.

As for your spiteful false objections 283 ,

Prove them, and I lie open to the law:

But God in mercy so deal with my soul,

As I in duty love my king and country.

But to the matter that we have in hand:—

I say, my sovereign, York is meetest 284 man

To be your regent in the realm of France.

SUFFOLK Before we make election 285 , give me leave 286

To show some reason, of no little force,

That York is most unmeet of any man.

YORK I’ll tell thee, Suffolk, why I am unmeet:

First, for I cannot flatter thee in pride 287 :

Next, if I be appointed for the place,

My Lord of Somerset will keep me here,

Without discharge 288 , money, or furniture 289 ,

Till France be won into the dauphin’s hands:

Last time I danced attendance on his will

Till Paris was besieged, famished, and lost.

WARWICK That can I witness, and a fouler fact 290

Did never traitor in the land commit.

SUFFOLK Peace, headstrong Warwick.

WARWICK Image 291 of pride, why should I hold my peace?

Enter [guarded, Horner the ] armourer and his man [Peter ]

SUFFOLK Because here is a man accused of treason:

Pray God the Duke of York excuse himself!

YORK Doth anyone accuse York for 292 a traitor?

KING HENRY VI What mean’st thou, Suffolk? Tell me, what 293 are these?

SUFFOLK Please it your majesty, this is the man

Indicating Peter

That doth accuse his master of high treason:

His words were these: that Richard, Duke of York,

Was rightful heir unto the English crown

And that your majesty was an usurper.

KING HENRY VI Say, man, were these thy words?

HORNER An’t 294 shall please your majesty, I never said nor thought any such matter:

God is my witness, I am falsely accused by the villain.

PETER By these ten bones 295 , my lords, he did speak them to me in the garret 296 one night, as we were scouring 297 my Lord of York’s armour.

Raising his hands

YORK Base dunghill villain and mechanical 298 ,

I’ll have thy head for this thy traitor’s speech.—

I do beseech your royal majesty,

Let him have all the rigour of the law.

HORNER Alas, my lord, hang me if ever I spake the words: my accuser is my prentice 299 , and when I did correct 300 him for his fault the other day, he did vow upon his knees he would be even with me — I have good witness of this — therefore I beseech your majesty, do not cast away an honest man for a villain’s accusation.

KING HENRY VI Uncle, what shall we say to this in law?

To Gloucester

GLOUCESTER This doom 301 , my lord, if I may judge:

Let Somerset be regent o’er the French,

Because in York this 302 breeds suspicion:

And let these have a day appointed them

Indicating Horner and Peter

For single combat in convenient 303 place,

For he hath witness of his servant’s malice:

Indicating Horner

This is the law, and this Duke Humphrey’s doom.

SOMERSET I humbly thank your royal majesty.

HORNER And I accept the combat willingly.

PETER Alas, my lord, I cannot fight: for God’s sake, pity my case: the spite of man prevaileth against me. O Lord, have mercy upon me! I shall never be able to fight a blow. O Lord, my heart!

GLOUCESTER Sirrah 304 , or 305 you must fight, or else be hanged.

KING HENRY VI Away with them to prison, and the day

Of combat shall be the last of the next month.

Come, Somerset, we’ll see thee sent away

Flourish. Exeunt
