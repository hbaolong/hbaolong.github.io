Scene 4 500 / running scene 7

A rose brier revealed

Enter Richard Plantagenet, Warwick, Somerset, Suffolk, [Vernon, and a Lawyer ]

RICHARD PLANTAGENET Great lords and gentlemen, what means this silence?

Dare no man answer in a case of truth 501 ?

SUFFOLK Within the Temple hall we were 502 too loud:

The garden here is more convenient.

RICHARD PLANTAGENET Then say at once if I maintained the truth:

Or else 503 was wrangling 504 Somerset in th’error?

SUFFOLK Faith, I have been a truant 505 in the law,

And never yet could frame 506 my will to it,

And therefore frame the law unto my will.

SOMERSET Judge you, my lord of Warwick, then between us.

WARWICK Between two hawks, which flies the higher pitch 507 ,

Between two dogs, which hath the deeper mouth 508 ,

Between two blades 509 , which bears the better temper 510 ,

Between two horses, which doth bear him 511 best,

Between two girls, which hath the merriest eye,

I have perhaps some shallow 512 spirit of judgement:

But in these nice 513 sharp 514 quillets 515 of the law,

Good faith, I am no wiser than a daw 516 .

RICHARD PLANTAGENET Tut, tut, here is a mannerly forbearance 517 :

The truth appears so naked 518 on my side

That any purblind 519 eye may find it out.

SOMERSET And on my side it is so well apparelled 520 ,

So clear, so shining, and so evident

That it will glimmer through a blind 521 man’s eye.

RICHARD PLANTAGENET Since you are tongue-tied and so loath to speak,

In dumb significants 522 proclaim your thoughts:

Let him that is a true-born gentleman

And stands 523 upon the honour of his birth,

If he suppose that I have pleaded 524 truth,

From off this brier pluck a white rose 525 with me.

He plucks a white rose

SOMERSET Let him that is no coward nor no flatterer,

But dare maintain the party 526 of the truth,

Pluck a red rose 527 from off this thorn with me.

He plucks a red rose

WARWICK I love no colours 528 , and without all colour 529

Of base insinuating flattery

I pluck this white rose with Plantagenet.

SUFFOLK I pluck this red rose with young Somerset

And say withal 530 I think he held the right.

VERNON Stay, lords and gentlemen, and pluck no more

Till you conclude that he upon whose side

The fewest roses are cropped from the tree

Shall yield 531 the other in the right opinion.

SOMERSET Good Master Vernon, it is well objected 532 :

If I have fewest, I subscribe 533 in silence.

RICHARD PLANTAGENET And I.

VERNON Then for the truth and plainness of the case.

I pluck this pale and maiden 534 blossom here,

Giving my verdict on the white rose side.

SOMERSET Prick not your finger as you pluck it off,

Lest bleeding you do paint the white rose red

And fall on my side so against your will.

VERNON If I, my lord, for my opinion bleed,

Opinion 535 shall be surgeon to my hurt

And keep me on the side where still 536 I am.

SOMERSET Well, well, come on, who else?

LAWYER Unless my study and my books be false,

The argument you held was wrong in law:

To Somerset

In sign whereof I pluck a white rose too.

RICHARD PLANTAGENET Now, Somerset, where is your argument?

SOMERSET Here in my scabbard 537 , meditating that 538

Shall dye your white rose in a bloody red.

RICHARD PLANTAGENET Meantime your cheeks do counterfeit 539 our roses:

For pale they look with fear, as witnessing

The truth on our side.

SOMERSET No, Plantagenet,

’Tis not for fear, but anger, that thy cheeks

Blush for pure shame to counterfeit our roses,

And yet thy tongue will not confess thy error.

RICHARD PLANTAGENET Hath not thy rose a canker 540 , Somerset?

SOMERSET Hath not thy rose a thorn, Plantagenet?

RICHARD PLANTAGENET Ay, sharp and piercing, to maintain his 541 truth,

Whiles thy consuming canker eats his falsehood.

SOMERSET Well, I’ll find friends to wear my bleeding roses,

That shall maintain what I have said is true,

Where false 542 Plantagenet dare not be seen.

RICHARD PLANTAGENET Now, by this maiden blossom in my hand,

I scorn thee and thy fashion 543 , peevish 544 boy.

SUFFOLK Turn not thy scorns this way, Plantagenet.

RICHARD PLANTAGENET Proud Pole 545 , I will, and scorn both him and thee.

SUFFOLK I’ll turn my part thereof into thy throat 546 .

SOMERSET Away, away, good William de la Pole:

We grace 547 the yeoman 548 by conversing with him.

WARWICK Now, by God’s will, thou wrong’st him, Somerset:

His grandfather 549 was Lionel Duke of Clarence,

Third son to the third Edward King of England:

Spring crestless 550 yeomen from so deep a root?

RICHARD PLANTAGENET He bears him on the place’s privilege 551 ,

Or durst not for his craven 552 heart say thus.

SOMERSET By him that made me, I’ll maintain 553 my words

On any plot of ground in Christendom.

Was not thy father, Richard Earl of Cambridge,

For treason executed in our late king’s days?

And by his treason, stand’st not thou attainted 554 ,

Corrupted, and exempt from ancient gentry?

His trespass yet lives guilty in thy blood,

And till thou be restored 555 , thou art a yeoman.

RICHARD PLANTAGENET My father was attachèd, not attainted 556 ,

Condemned to die for treason, but no traitor;

And that I’ll prove on better men than Somerset,

Were growing time once ripened to my will 557 .

For your partaker 558 Pole and you yourself,

I’ll note 559 you in my book of memory,

To scourge you for this apprehension 560 :

Look to it 561 well, and say you are well warned.

SOMERSET Ah, thou shalt find us ready for thee still,

And know us by these colours for thy foes,

For these my friends in spite 562 of thee shall wear.

RICHARD PLANTAGENET And by my soul, this pale and angry rose,

As cognizance 563 of my blood-drinking hate,

Will I for ever and my faction wear,

Until it wither with me to my grave,

Or flourish to the height of my degree 564 .

SUFFOLK Go forward and be choked with thy ambition:

And so farewell until I meet thee next.

Exit

SOMERSET Have with thee 565 , Pole.— Farewell, ambitious Richard.

Exit

RICHARD PLANTAGENET How I am braved 566 and must perforce 567 endure it!

WARWICK This blot that they object 568 against your house

Shall be wiped out in the next parliament,

Called for the truce of 569 Winchester and Gloucester:

And if thou be not then created York,

I will not live to be accounted Warwick.

Meantime, in signal 570 of my love to thee,

Against proud Somerset and William Pole,

Will I upon thy party 571 wear this rose.

And here I prophesy: this brawl today,

Grown to this faction 572 in the Temple garden,

Shall send, between the red rose and the white,

A thousand souls to death and deadly night.

RICHARD PLANTAGENET Good Master Vernon, I am bound to you,

That you on my behalf would pluck a flower.

VERNON In your behalf still 573 will I wear the same.

LAWYER And so will I.

RICHARD PLANTAGENET Thanks, gentles 574 .

Come, let us four to dinner: I dare say

This quarrel will drink blood another day.

Exeunt
