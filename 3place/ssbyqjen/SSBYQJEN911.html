Enter Thaliard alone

THALIARD So this is Tyre, and this the court. Here must I kill King Pericles, and if I do it not, I am sure to be hanged at home: ’tis dangerous. Well, I perceive he was a wise fellow, and had good discretion, that being bid to ask what he would of 225 the king, desired he might know none of his secrets 226 . Now do I see he had some reason for’t: for if a king bid a man be a villain, he’s bound by the indenture 227 of his oath to be one. Husht 228 , here comes the lords of Tyre.

Thaliard stands aside

Enter Helicanus, Escanes, with other Lords

HELICANUS You shall not need, my fellow peers of Tyre,

Further to question me of your king’s departure:

His sealed 229 commission 230 left in trust with me,

Does speak sufficiently 231 he’s gone to travel.

THALIARD How? The king gone?

Aside

Why — as it were unlicensed of your loves 232 —

He would depart, I’ll give some light 233 unto you.

Being at Antioch—

Aside

HELICANUS Royal Antiochus, on what cause I know not,

Took some displeasure at him — at least he judged so —

And doubting lest 234 he had erred or sinned,

To show his sorrow, he’d correct 235 himself:

So puts himself unto the shipman’s toil 236 ,

With whom each minute threatens life or death.

THALIARD Well, I perceive I shall not be hanged now although I would 237 . But since he’s gone, the king’s ears this must please 238 : he scaped the land to perish at the sea. I’ll present myself.—Peace to the lords of Tyre!

Aside

Aloud

HELICANUS Lord Thaliard from Antiochus is welcome.

THALIARD From him I come with message unto princely Pericles, but since my landing I have understood your lord has betook 239 himself to unknown travels, now message 240 must return from whence it 241 came.

HELICANUS We have no reason to desire it,

Commended 242 to our master, not to us,

Yet ere you shall depart, this we desire:

As friends to Antioch we may feast in Tyre.
