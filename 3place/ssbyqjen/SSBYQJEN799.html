Scene 2 144 / running scene 2

Enter Duke Humphrey [of Glouceste r] and his wife Eleanor

ELEANOR Why droops my lord like over-ripened corn,

Hanging the head at Ceres’ 145 plenteous load?

Why doth the great Duke Humphrey knit his brows,

As 146 frowning at the favours of the world?

Why are thine eyes fixed to the sullen 147 earth,

Gazing on that which seems to dim thy sight?

What see’st thou there? King Henry’s diadem,

Enchased 148 with all the honours of the world?

If so, gaze on, and grovel 149 on thy face,

Until thy head be circlèd with the same.

Put forth thy hand, reach at the glorious gold.

What, is’t 150 too short? I’ll lengthen it with mine,

And having both together heaved it up,

We’ll both together lift our heads to heaven,

And never more abase our sight so low

As to vouchsafe 151 one glance unto the ground.

GLOUCESTER O Nell, sweet Nell, if thou dost love thy lord,

Banish the canker 152 of ambitious thoughts:

And may that thought, when I imagine ill 153

Against my king and nephew, virtuous Henry,

Be my last breathing 154 in this mortal world.

My troublous dreams this night 155 doth make me sad.

ELEANOR What dreamed my lord? Tell me, and I’ll requite 156 it

With sweet rehearsal 157 of my morning’s dream 158 .

GLOUCESTER Methought this staff 159 , mine office-badge 160 in court,

Was broke in twain 161 : by whom I have forgot,

But, as I think, it was by th’cardinal:

And on the pieces of the broken wand

Were placed the heads of Edmund Duke of Somerset,

And William de la Pole, first Duke of Suffolk.

This was my dream: what it doth bode, God knows.

ELEANOR Tut, this was nothing but an argument 162

That he that breaks a stick of Gloucester’s grove 163

Shall lose his head for his presumption.

But list 164 to me, my Humphrey, my sweet duke:

Methought I sat in seat of majesty

In the cathedral church of Westminster,

And in that chair 165 where kings and queens are crowned,

Where Henry and Dame Margaret kneeled to me

And on my head did set the diadem.

GLOUCESTER Nay, Eleanor, then must I chide 166 outright:

Presumptuous dame, ill-nurtured 167 Eleanor,

Art thou not second woman in the realm,

And the Protector’s wife, beloved of 168 him?

Hast thou not worldly pleasure at command,

Above the reach or compass 169 of thy thought?

And wilt thou still be hammering 170 treachery,

To tumble down thy husband and thyself

From top of honour to disgrace’s feet?

Away from me, and let me hear no more!

ELEANOR What, what, my lord? Are you so choleric 171

With Eleanor for telling but 172 her dream?

Next time I’ll keep my dreams unto myself,

And not be checked 173 .

GLOUCESTER Nay, be not angry: I am pleased again.

Enter [a] Messenger

MESSENGER My Lord Protector, ‘tis his highness’ pleasure

You do prepare to ride unto St Albans 174 ,

Where as 175 the king and queen do mean to hawk 176 .

GLOUCESTER I go. Come, Nell, thou wilt ride with us?

ELEANOR Yes, my good lord, I’ll follow presently 177 .

Exit Humphrey [Duke of Gloucester with Messenger ]

Follow I must: I cannot go before,

While Gloucester bears this base 178 and humble mind.

Were I a man, a duke, and next of blood,

I would remove these tedious stumbling blocks

And smooth my way upon their headless necks.

And, being a woman, I will not be slack

To play my part in Fortune’s pageant 179 .—

Where are you there? Sir 180 John! Nay, fear not, man,

Calling

We are alone: here’s none but thee and I.

Enter Hume

HUME Jesus preserve your royal majesty.

ELEANOR What say’st thou? ‘Majesty’: I am but grace 181 .

HUME But by the grace of God, and Hume’s advice,

Your grace’s title shall be multiplied 182 .

ELEANOR What say’st thou, man? Hast thou as yet conferred

With Margery Jordan, the cunning 183 witch,

With Roger Bullingbrook, the conjurer 184 ?

And will they undertake to do me good?

HUME This they have promised to show your highness:

A spirit raised from depth of underground,

That shall make answer to such questions

As by your grace shall be propounded 185 him.

ELEANOR It is enough: I’ll think upon the questions:

When from St Albans we do make return,

We’ll see these things effected to the full.

Here, Hume, take this reward: make merry, man,

Giving him money

With thy confederates in this weighty cause.

Exit Eleanor

HUME Hume must make merry with the duchess’ gold:

Marry 186 , and shall: but how now, Sir John Hume?

Seal up your lips, and give no words but mum 187 :

The business asketh silent secrecy.

Dame Eleanor gives gold to bring the witch:

Gold cannot come amiss, were she a devil.

Yet have I gold flies 188 from another coast 189 :

I dare not say from the rich cardinal

And from the great and new-made Duke of Suffolk,

Yet I do find it so: for to be plain,

They, knowing Dame Eleanor’s aspiring humour 190 ,

Have hired me to undermine the duchess

And buzz 191 these conjurations 192 in her brain.

They say ‘A crafty knave does need no broker 193’,

Yet am I Suffolk and the cardinal’s broker.

Hume, if you take not heed, you shall go near 194

To call them both a pair of crafty knaves.

Well, so it stands: and thus, I fear, at last

Hume’s knavery will be the duchess’ wrack 195 ,

And her attainture 196 will be Humphrey’s fall:

Sort how it will 197 , I shall have gold for all.

Exit
