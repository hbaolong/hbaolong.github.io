Scene 2 117 / running scene 2

Sound a flourish 118 . Enter Charles [the Dauphin, the Duke of ] Alençon and Reignier [Duke of Anjou ], marching with Drum 119 and Soldiers

CHARLES Mars his true moving 120 , even as in the heavens

So in the earth, to this day is not known.

Late 121 did he shine upon the English side:

Now we are victors: upon us he smiles.

What towns of any moment 122 but we have?

At pleasure here we lie near Orléans;

Otherwhiles 123 the famished English, like pale ghosts,

Faintly besiege us one hour in a month.

ALENÇON They want 124 their porridge 125 and their fat bull-beeves 126 :

Either they must be dieted 127 like mules,

And have their provender 128 tied to their mouths,

Or piteous they will look, like drownèd mice.

REIGNIER Let’s raise 129 the siege: why live we idly here?

Talbot is taken, whom we wont 130 to fear:

Remaineth none but mad-brained Salisbury,

And he may well in fretting 131 spend his gall 132 ,

Nor men nor money hath he to make war.

CHARLES Sound, sound alarum 133 ! We will rush on them.

Now for the honour of the forlorn 134 French:

Him I forgive my death that killeth me

When he sees me go back one foot or fly.

Exeunt

Here alarum: they are beaten back by the English with great loss Enter Charles, Alençon and Reignier

CHARLES Who ever saw the like? What men have I?

Dogs, cowards, dastards 135 ! I would ne’er have fled,

But that they left me ’midst my enemies.

REIGNIER Salisbury is a desperate homicide 136 ;

He fighteth as one weary of his life:

The other lords, like lions wanting food,

Do rush upon us as their hungry 137 prey.

ALENÇON Froissart 138 , a countryman of ours, records

England all Olivers and Rowlands 139 bred,

During the time Edward the Third did reign:

More truly now may this be verified;

For none but Samsons and Goliases 140

It sendeth forth to skirmish 141 : one to ten 142 !

Lean raw-boned 143 rascals 144 , who would e’er suppose

They had such courage and audacity?

CHARLES Let’s leave this town, for they are hare-brained slaves 145 ,

And hunger will enforce them to be more eager 146 :

Of old I know them; rather with their teeth

The walls they’ll tear down than forsake 147 the siege.

REIGNIER I think by some odd gimmers 148 or device 149

Their arms are set, like clocks, still 150 to strike on;

Else ne’er could they hold out so as they do:

By my consent 151 , we’ll even 152 let them alone.

ALENÇON Be it so.

Enter the Bastard of Orléans

BASTARD Where’s the Prince Dauphin? I have news for him.

CHARLES Bastard of Orléans, thrice welcome to us.

BASTARD Methinks your looks are sad, your cheer appalled 153 .

Hath the late overthrow wrought 154 this offence 155 ?

Be not dismayed, for succour is at hand:

A holy maid hither with me I bring,

Which by a vision sent to her from heaven,

Ordainèd 156 is to raise 157 this tedious 158 siege

And drive the English forth 159 the bounds 160 of France:

The spirit of deep prophecy she hath,

Exceeding the nine sibyls 161 of old Rome:

What’s past and what’s to come she can descry 162 .

Speak, shall I call her in? Believe my words,

For they are certain and unfallible.

CHARLES Go, call her in.

[Exit Bastard of Orléans ]

But first, to try 163 her skill,

Reignier, stand thou as dauphin in my place:

Question her proudly 164 : let thy looks be stern:

By this means shall we sound 165 what skill she hath.

Enter [the Bastard of Orléans, with ] Joan 166 [la ] Pucelle [armed ]

REIGNIER Fair maid, is’t thou wilt do these wondrous feats?

As Charles

PUCELLE Reignier, is’t thou that thinkest to beguile 167 me?

Where is the dauphin?— Come, come from behind:

To Charles

I know thee well, though never seen before.

Be not amazed, there’s nothing hid from me;

In private will I talk with thee apart 168 :

Stand back, you lords, and give us leave awhile.

Reignier, Alençon and Bastard stand apart

REIGNIER She takes upon her 169 bravely at first dash 170 .

PUCELLE Dauphin, I am by birth a shepherd’s daughter,

My wit 171 untrained in any kind of art 172 :

Heaven and our Lady gracious 173 hath it pleased

To shine on my contemptible estate 174 .

Lo 175 , whilst I waited on my tender 176 lambs,

And to sun’s parching heat displayed my cheeks,

God’s mother deignèd to appear to me,

And in a vision full of majesty,

Willed me to leave my base vocation

And free my country from calamity:

Her aid she promised, and assured success.

In complete 177 glory she revealed herself:

And whereas I was black and swart 178 before,

With those clear rays which she infused on 179 me

That beauty am I blessed with, which you may see.

Ask me what question thou canst possible,

And I will answer unpremeditated 180 :

My courage try by combat, if thou dar’st,

And thou shalt find that I exceed my sex.

Resolve on 181 this, thou shalt be fortunate,

If thou receive me for thy warlike mate 182 .

CHARLES Thou hast astonished me with thy high terms 183 :

Only this proof 184 I’ll of thy valour make,

In single combat thou shalt buckle 185 with me,

And if thou vanquishest 186 , thy words are true:

Otherwise I renounce all confidence 187 .

PUCELLE I am prepared: here is my keen-edged 188 sword,

Decked 189 with five flower-de-luces 190 on each side,

The which at Touraine 191 , in Saint Katherine 192 ’s churchyard,

Out of a 193 great deal of old iron I chose forth.

CHARLES Then come, a God’s name: I fear no woman.

PUCELLE And while I live, I’ll ne’er fly from a man 194 .

Here they fight, and Joan la Pucelle overcomes

CHARLES Stay, stay thy hands! Thou art an Amazon 195

And fightest with the sword of Deborah 196 .

PUCELLE Christ’s mother helps me, else 197 I were too weak.

CHARLES Whoe’er helps thee, ’tis thou that must help me:

Impatiently I burn with thy desire 198 :

My heart and hands thou hast at once subdued.

Excellent Pucelle, if thy name be so,

Let me thy servant 199 and not sovereign be:

’Tis the French dauphin sueth to 200 thee thus.

PUCELLE I must not yield to any rites of love,

For my profession 201 ’s sacred from above:

When I have chased all thy foes from hence,

Then will I think upon a recompense 202 .

CHARLES Meantime, look gracious 203 on thy prostrate 204 thrall 205 .

REIGNIER My lord, methinks, is very long in talk.

To the others, apart

ALENÇON Doubtless he shrives 206 this woman to her smock 207 ,

Else ne’er could he so long protract 208 his speech.

REIGNIER Shall we disturb him, since he keeps no mean 209 ?

ALENÇON He may mean more than we poor men do know 210 .

These women are shrewd 211 tempters with their tongues 212 .

REIGNIER My lord, where are you 213 ? What devise you on 214 ?

Shall we give o’er 215 Orléans, or no?

PUCELLE Why, no, I say: distrustful recreants 216 ,

Fight till the last gasp: I’ll be your guard.

CHARLES What she says, I’ll confirm: we’ll fight it out.

PUCELLE Assigned am I to be the English scourge 217 .

This night the siege assuredly I’ll raise:

Expect Saint Martin’s summer 218 , halcyon’s days 219 ,

Since I have entered into these wars.

Glory is like a circle in the water,

Which never ceaseth to enlarge itself

Till by broad spreading, it disperse to naught.

With Henry’s death, the English circle ends:

Dispersèd are the glories it included:

Now am I like that proud insulting 220 ship

Which Caesar and his fortune bare at once 221 .

CHARLES Was Mahomet inspirèd with a dove 222 ?

Thou with 223 an eagle 224 art inspirèd then.

Helen 225 , the mother of great Constantine,

Nor yet Saint Philip’s daughters 226 were like thee.

Bright star of Venus 227 , fall’n down on the earth,

How may I reverently worship thee enough?

ALENÇON Leave off delays, and let us raise the siege.

REIGNIER Woman, do what thou canst to save our honours;

Drive them from Orléans, and be immortalized 228 .

CHARLES Presently 229 we’ll try: come, let’s away about it:

No prophet will I trust, if she prove false.

Exeunt
