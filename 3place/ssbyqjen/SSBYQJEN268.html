Scene 3 / running scene 3

Enter Sir Toby [Belch ] and Maria

SIR TOBY What a plague 63 means my niece 64 to take the death of her brother thus? I am sure care 65 ’s an enemy to life.

MARIA By my troth 66 , Sir Toby, you must come in earlier a-nights 67 : your cousin 68 , my lady, takes great exceptions to your ill 69 hours.

SIR TOBY Why, let her except, before excepted 70 .

MARIA Ay, but you must confine yourself within the modest 71 limits of order.

SIR TOBY Confine? I’ll confine myself no finer 72 than I am: these clothes are good enough to drink in, and so be these boots too. An 73 they be not, let them hang themselves in their own straps.

MARIA That quaffing 74 and drinking will undo you. I heard my lady talk of it yesterday, and of a foolish knight that you brought in one night here to be her wooer.

SIR TOBY Who, Sir Andrew Aguecheek 75 ?

MARIA Ay, he.

SIR TOBY He’s as tall 76 a man as any’s 77 in Illyria.

MARIA What’s that to th’purpose?

SIR TOBY Why, he has three thousand ducats 78 a year.

MARIA Ay, but he’ll have but a year in all these ducats 79 : he’s a very 80 fool and a prodigal 81 .

SIR TOBY Fie, that you’ll say so! He plays o’th’viol-de-gamboys 82 , and speaks three or four languages word for word without book 83 , and hath all the good gifts of nature.

MARIA He hath indeed, almost natural 84 , for, besides that he’s a fool, he’s a great quarreller: and but that he hath the gift of a coward to allay the gust he hath in 85 quarrelling, ’tis thought among the prudent he would quickly have the gift of a grave.

SIR TOBY By this hand, they are scoundrels and subtractors 86 that say so of him. Who are they?

MARIA They that add, moreover, he’s drunk nightly in your company.

SIR TOBY With drinking healths to my niece. I’ll drink to her as long as there is a passage in my throat and drink in Illyria. He’s a coward and a coystrill 87 that will not drink to my niece till his brains turn o’th’toe 88 like a parish top 89 . What, wench? Castiliano vulgo 90 ! For here comes Sir Andrew Agueface 91 .

Enter Sir Andrew [Aguecheek ]

SIR ANDREW Sir Toby Belch. How now, Sir Toby Belch?

SIR TOBY Sweet Sir Andrew.

SIR ANDREW Bless you, fair shrew 92 .

To Maria

MARIA And you too, sir.

SIR TOBY Accost 93 , Sir Andrew, accost.

SIR ANDREW What’s that?

SIR TOBY My niece’s chambermaid 94 .

SIR ANDREW Good Mistress Accost, I desire better acquaintance.

MARIA My name is Mary, sir.

SIR ANDREW Good Mistress Mary Accost—

SIR TOBY You mistake, knight. ‘Accost’ is front 95 her, board 96 her, woo her, assail 97 her.

SIR ANDREW By my troth, I would not undertake 98 her in this company 99 . Is that the meaning of ‘accost’?

MARIA Fare you well, gentlemen.

Starts to leave

SIR TOBY An thou let part so 100 , Sir Andrew, would thou mightst never draw sword 101 again.

SIR ANDREW An you part so, mistress, I would I might never draw sword again. Fair lady, do you think you have fools in hand 102 ?

MARIA Sir, I have not you by th’hand.

SIR ANDREW Marry 103 , but you shall have, and here’s my hand.

Gives her his hand

MARIA Now, sir, thought is free 104 . I pray you bring your hand to th’buttery-bar 105 and let it drink.

SIR ANDREW Wherefore 106 , sweetheart? What’s your metaphor?

MARIA It’s dry 107 , sir.

SIR ANDREW Why, I think so: I am not such an ass but I can keep my hand dry 108 . But what’s your jest?

MARIA A dry jest 109 , sir.

SIR ANDREW Are you full of them?

MARIA Ay, sir, I have them at my fingers’ ends 110 .

Lets go of his hand

Marry, now I let go your hand, I am barren 111 .

Exit Maria

SIR TOBY O knight, thou lack’st a cup of canary 112 . When did I see thee so put down 113 ?

SIR ANDREW Never in your life, I think, unless you see canary put me down. Methinks sometimes I have no more wit than a Christian 114 or an ordinary man has. But I am a great eater of beef and I believe that does harm to my wit 115 .

SIR TOBY No question.

SIR ANDREW An I thought that, I’d forswear it 116 . I’ll ride home tomorrow, Sir Toby.

SIR TOBY Pourquoi 117 , my dear knight?

SIR ANDREW What is ‘Pourquoi’ ? Do or not do? I would I had bestowed that time in the tongues 118 that I have in fencing, dancing and bear-baiting 119 . O, had I but followed the arts 120 !

SIR TOBY Then hadst thou had an excellent head of hair 121 .

SIR ANDREW Why, would that have mended 122 my hair?

SIR TOBY Past question, for thou see’st it will not curl by nature.

SIR ANDREW But it becomes 123 me well enough, does’t not?

SIR TOBY Excellent. It hangs like flax 124 on a distaff 125 , and I hope to see a housewife 126 take thee between her legs and spin it off 127 .

SIR ANDREW Faith, I’ll home tomorrow, Sir Toby. Your niece will not be seen, or if she be, it’s four to one she’ll none of me 128 . The count 129 himself here hard 130 by woos her.

SIR TOBY She’ll none o’th’count. She’ll not match above her degree 131 , neither in estate 132 , years, nor wit 133 ; I have heard her swear’t. Tut, there’s life in’t 134 , man.

SIR ANDREW I’ll stay a month longer. I am a fellow o’th’strangest 135 mind i’th’world:

I delight in masques and revels 136 sometimes altogether.

SIR TOBY Art thou good at these kickshawses 137 , knight?

SIR ANDREW As any man in Illyria, whatsoever he be, under the degree of my betters 138 , and yet I will not compare with an old man 139 .

SIR TOBY What is thy excellence in a galliard 140 , knight?

SIR ANDREW Faith, I can cut a caper 141 .

SIR TOBY And I can cut the mutton to’t 142 .

SIR ANDREW And I think I have the back-trick 143 simply as strong as any man in Illyria.

SIR TOBY Wherefore are these things hid? Wherefore have these gifts a curtain before ’em? Are they like to take 144 dust, like Mistress Mall’s picture 145 ? Why dost thou not go to church in a galliard and come home in 146 a coranto 147 ? My very walk should be a jig 148 , I would not so much as make water 149 but in a sink-a-pace 150 . What dost thou mean? Is it a world to hide virtues 151 in? I did think, by the excellent constitution of thy leg, it was formed under the star of a galliard 152 .

SIR ANDREW Ay, ’tis strong, and it does indifferent 153 well in a damned 154 coloured stock 155 . Shall we set about some revels?

SIR TOBY What shall we do else? Were we not born under Taurus?

SIR ANDREW Taurus? That’s sides and heart 156 .

SIR TOBY No, sir, it is legs and thighs 157 . Let me see thee caper. Ha? Higher, ha, ha! Excellent!

Sir Andrew dances

Exeunt
