Scene 2 503 / running scene 5

Enter Prince, Poins and Peto [and Bardolph ]

POINS Come, shelter, shelter. I have removed Falstaff’s horse, and he frets like a gummed velvet 504 .

PRINCE HENRY Stand close 505 .

Poins, Peto and Bardolph stand aside

Enter Falstaff

FALSTAFF Poins! Poins, and be hanged! Poins!

PRINCE HENRY Peace, ye fat-kidneyed 506 rascal. What a brawling dost thou keep 507 !

FALSTAFF What, Poins? Hal?

PRINCE HENRY He is walked up to the top of the hill. I’ll go seek him.

Stands aside with the others

FALSTAFF I am accursed to rob in that thief’s company. That rascal hath removed my horse, and tied him I know not where. If I travel but four foot by the square 508 509 further afoot, I shall break my wind 510 . Well, I doubt not but to die a fair death for all this, if I scape hanging for killing that rogue 511 . I have forsworn his company hourly any time this two-and-twenty year, and yet I am bewitched with the rogue’s company. If the rascal have not given me medicines 512 to make me love him, I’ll be hanged; it could not be else: I have drunk medicines. Poins, Hal, a plague upon you both! Bardolph! Peto! I’ll starve ere 513 I rob a foot further. An 514 ’twere not as good a deed as to drink, to turn true man 515 and to leave these rogues, I am the veriest varlet 516 that ever chewed with a tooth. Eight yards of uneven ground is threescore and ten miles afoot with me, and the stony-hearted villains know it well enough. A plague upon’t when thieves cannot be true one to another!

They whistle

Whew! 517 A plague light upon you all! Give me my horse, you rogues. Give me my horse, and be hanged!

The Prince, Poins, Peto and Bardolph come forward

PRINCE HENRY Peace, ye fat-guts! Lie down, lay thine ear close to the ground and list if thou can hear the tread of travellers.

FALSTAFF Have you any levers to lift me up again, being down? I’ll not bear mine own flesh so far afoot again for all the coin in thy father’s exchequer 518 . What a plague mean ye to colt 519 me thus?

PRINCE HENRY Thou liest. Thou art not colted, thou art uncolted 520 .

FALSTAFF I prithee, good Prince Hal, help me to 521 my horse, good king’s son.

PRINCE HENRY Out, you rogue! Shall I be your ostler?

FALSTAFF Go, hang thyself in thine own heir-apparent garters 522 ! If I be ta’en, I’ll peach 523 for this. An I have not ballads made on all 524 and sung to filthy 525 tunes, let a cup of sack be my poison. When a jest is so forward 526 , and afoot 527 too! I hate it.

Enter Gadshill

GADSHILL Stand.

FALSTAFF So I do, against my will.

POINS O, ’tis our setter 528 . I know his voice.

BARDOLPH What news?

GADSHILL Case ye 529 , case ye; on with your vizards. There’s money of the king’s coming down the hill, ’tis going to the king’s exchequer.

FALSTAFF You lie, you rogue, ’tis going to the King’s Tavern.

GADSHILL There’s enough to make us all 530 .

FALSTAFF To be hanged.

PRINCE HENRY You four shall front 531 them in the narrow lane. Ned and I will walk lower 532 ; if they scape from your encounter, then they light on 533 us.

PETO But how many be 534 of them?

GADSHILL Some eight or ten.

FALSTAFF Will they not rob us?

PRINCE HENRY What, a coward, Sir John Paunch?

FALSTAFF Indeed, I am not John of Gaunt 535 , your grandfather; but yet no coward, Hal.

PRINCE HENRY We’ll leave that to the proof 536 .

POINS Sirrah Jack, thy horse stands behind the hedge. When thou need’st him, there thou shalt find him. Farewell, and stand fast.

FALSTAFF Now cannot I strike him, if I should be hanged.

PRINCE HENRY Ned, where are our disguises?

To Poins

POINS Here, hard 537 by. Stand close.

To Prince Henry

[Exeunt Prince Henry and Poins ]

FALSTAFF Now, my masters, happy man be his dole 538 , say I. Every man to his business.

Enter Travellers

FIRST TRAVELLER Come, neighbour. The boy shall lead our horses down the hill. We’ll walk afoot awhile, and ease our legs.

THIEVES Stay!

TRAVELLERS Jesu bless us!

FALSTAFF Strike, down with them! Cut the villains’ throats. Ah, whoreson caterpillars 539 540 , bacon-fed 541 knaves! They hate us youth 542 , down with them, fleece them.

TRAVELLERS O, we are undone 543 , both we and ours for ever!

FALSTAFF Hang ye, gorbellied 544 knaves, are you undone? No, ye fat chuffs 545 , I would your store 546 were here! On, bacons 547 , on! What, ye knaves? Young men must live. You are grand-jurors 548 , are ye? We’ll jure 549 ye, i’faith.

Here they rob them and bind them [Exeunt ]

Enter the Prince and Poins

PRINCE HENRY The thieves have bound the true men. Now could thou and I rob the thieves and go merrily to London, it would be argument 550 for a week, laughter for a month and a good jest for ever.

POINS Stand close. I hear them coming.

Enter Thieves again

FALSTAFF Come, my masters 551 , let us share, and then to horse before day. An the prince and Poins be not two arrant 552 cowards, there’s no equity 553 stirring. There’s no more valour in that Poins than in a wild duck 554 .

PRINCE HENRY Your money!

POINS Villains!

As they are sharing, the Prince and Poins set upon them. They all run away, leaving the booty behind them

PRINCE HENRY Got with much ease. Now merrily to horse.

The thieves are scattered and possessed with fear

So strongly that they dare not meet each other:

Each takes his fellow for an officer.

Away, good Ned. Falstaff sweats to death,

And lards 555 the lean earth as he walks along.

Were’t not for laughing, I should pity him.

POINS How the rogue roared!

Exeunt
