Scene 1 2 ／running scene 1

Enter King Richard, John of Gaunt 3 , with other Nobles and Attendants

KING RICHARD Old John of Gaunt, time-honoured Lancaster,

Hast thou 4 according to thy oath and band 5

Brought hither Henry Hereford 6 thy bold son,

Here to make good the boist’rous 7 late 8 appeal 9 ,

Which then our 10 leisure 11 would not let us hear,

Against the Duke of Norfolk, Thomas Mowbray?

GAUNT I have, my liege 12 .

KING RICHARD Tell me, moreover, hast thou sounded 13 him,

If he appeal the duke on ancient 14 malice,

Or worthily, as a good subject should,

On some known ground 15 of treachery in him?

GAUNT As near as I could sift 16 him on that argument 17 ,

On some apparent 18 danger seen in him

Aimed at your highness, no inveterate 19 malice.

KING RICHARD Then call them to our presence.

[Exit an Attendant ]

Face to face,

And frowning brow to brow, ourselves will hear

Th’accuser and the accusèd freely speak;

High-stomached 20 are they both, and full of ire 21 ,

In rage deaf as the sea, hasty as fire.

Enter Bullingbrook and Mowbray

BULLINGBROOK Many years of happy days befall

My gracious 22 sovereign, my most loving liege!

MOWBRAY Each day still 23 better other’s happiness

Until the heavens, envying earth’s good hap 24 ,

Add an immortal title 25 to your crown!

KING RICHARD We thank you both. Yet one but 26 flatters us,

As well appeareth 27 by the cause you come 28 ,

Namely, to appeal 29 each other of high treason.

Cousin of Hereford, what dost thou object 30

Against the Duke of Norfolk, Thomas Mowbray?

BULLINGBROOK First, heaven be the record 31 to my speech!

In the devotion of a subject’s love,

Tend’ring 32 the precious safety of my prince,

And free from other misbegotten 33 hate,

Come I appellant 34 to this princely presence.

Now, Thomas Mowbray, do I turn to thee,

And mark 35 my greeting well, for what I speak

My body shall make good upon this earth,

Or my divine soul answer 36 it in heaven.

Thou art a traitor and a miscreant 37 ;

Too good 38 to be so and too bad to live,

Since the more fair and crystal 39 is the sky,

The uglier seem the clouds that in it fly.

Once more, the more to aggravate 40 the note 41 ,

With a foul traitor’s name stuff I thy throat;

And wish —— so please my sovereign —— ere 42 I move,

What my tongue speaks my right 43 drawn sword may prove.

MOWBRAY Let not my cold 44 words here accuse 45 my zeal 46 :

’Tis not the trial 47 of a woman’s war,

The bitter clamour of two eager 48 tongues,

Can arbitrate 49 this cause 50 betwixt 51 us twain 52 .

The blood 53 is hot that must be cooled 54 for this.

Yet can I not of such tame patience boast

As to be hushed and nought at all to say.

First, the fair reverence of 55 your highness curbs me

From giving reins and spurs to my free speech,

Which else 56 would post 57 until it had returned

These terms of treason doubly down his throat.

Setting aside his high blood’s royalty 58 ,

And let 59 him be no kinsman to my liege,

I do defy 60 him, and I spit at him,

Call him a slanderous coward and a villain,

Which to maintain I would allow him odds 61 ,

And meet 62 him, were I tied 63 to run afoot

Even to the frozen ridges of the Alps,

Or any other ground inhabitable 64

Wherever Englishman durst 65 set his foot.

Meantime, let this 66 defend my loyalty:

By all my hopes most falsely doth he lie.

BULLINGBROOK Pale trembling coward, there I throw my gage 67 ,

Throws down his gage

Disclaiming here the kindred of a king,

And lay aside my high blood’s royalty,

Which fear, not reverence, makes thee to except 68 .

If guilty dread hath left thee so much strength

As to take up mine honour’s pawn 69 , then stoop.

By that and all the rites of knighthood else,

Will I make good 70 against thee, arm to arm,

What I have spoken, or thou canst devise 71 .

MOWBRAY I take it up, and by that sword I swear

Takes up gage

Which gently 72 laid my knighthood on my shoulder,

I’ll answer thee in any fair degree 73 ,

Or chivalrous design of knightly trial:

And when I mount, alive may I not light 74 ,

If I be traitor or unjustly 75 fight!

KING RICHARD What doth our cousin lay to Mowbray’s charge 76 ?

It must be great that can inherit us 77

So much as of a thought of ill in him.

BULLINGBROOK Look 78 what I said: my life shall prove it true,

That Mowbray hath received eight thousand nobles 79

In name of lendings 80 for your highness’ soldiers,

The which he hath detained for lewd 81 employments,

Like a false traitor and injurious 82 villain.

Besides I say, and will in battle prove,

Or 83 here or elsewhere to the furthest verge

That ever was surveyed by English eye,

That all the treasons for these eighteen years

Complotted 84 and contrivèd in this land

Fetched 85 from false Mowbray their first head 86 and spring.

Further I say, and further will maintain

Upon his bad life to make all this good,

That he did plot the Duke of Gloucester 87 ’s death,

Suggest 88 his soon-believing adversaries,

And consequently, like a traitor coward,

Sluiced out 89 his innocent soul through streams of blood:

Which blood, like sacrificing 90 Abel 91 ’s, cries

Even from the tongueless caverns of the earth

To me for justice and rough chastisement 92 .

And by the glorious worth of my descent,

This arm shall do it, or this life be spent.

KING RICHARD How high a pitch 93 his resolution soars!

Thomas of Norfolk, what sayest thou to this?

MOWBRAY O, let my sovereign turn away his face

And bid his ears a little while be deaf,

Till I have told this slander of 94 his blood,

How God and good men hate so foul a liar.

KING RICHARD Mowbray, impartial are our eyes and ears.

Were he my brother, nay, our kingdom’s heir,

As he is but my father’s brother’s son,

Now, by my sceptre’s awe 95 , I make a vow,

Such neighbour 96 nearness to our sacred blood

Should nothing privilege him, nor partialize 97

The unstooping firmness of my upright soul.

He is our subject, Mowbray, so art thou.

Free speech and fearless I to thee allow.

MOWBRAY Then, Bullingbrook, as low as to thy heart,

Through the false passage of thy throat, thou liest.

Three parts of that receipt 98 I had for Calais

Disbursed I duly to his highness’ soldiers;

The other part reserved I by consent,

For that my sovereign liege was in my debt

Upon remainder of a dear account 99 ,

Since last I went to France to fetch 100 his queen.

Now swallow down that lie. For Gloucester’s death,

I slew him not; but to mine own disgrace

Neglected my sworn duty in that case.

For you, my noble lord of Lancaster 101 ,

The honourable father to my foe,

Once I did lay an ambush for your life ——

A trespass 102 that doth vex my grievèd soul.

But ere I last received the sacrament

I did confess it, and exactly 103 begged

Your grace’s pardon, and I hope I had it.

This is my fault. As for the rest appealed 104 ,

It issues from the rancour of a villain,

A recreant 105 and most degenerate traitor

Which 106 in myself I boldly will defend,

And interchangeably 107 hurl down my gage

Throws down his gage

Upon this overweening 108 traitor’s foot,

To prove myself a loyal gentleman

Even in 109 the best blood chambered 110 in his bosom.

In haste whereof 111 , most heartily I pray

Your highness to assign our trial day.

KING RICHARD Wrath-kindled gentlemen, be ruled by me:

Let’s purge 112 this choler 113 without letting 114 blood.

This we prescribe, though no physician:

Deep malice makes too deep incision.

Forget, forgive, conclude 115 and be agreed:

Our doctors 116 say this is no time to bleed.

Good uncle, let this end where it begun:

We’ll calm the Duke of Norfolk, you your son.

GAUNT To be a make-peace shall become 117 my age:

Throw down, my son, the Duke of Norfolk’s gage.

KING RICHARD And, Norfolk, throw down his.

GAUNT When, Harry, when?

Obedience bids I should not bid again.

KING RICHARD Norfolk, throw down, we bid; there is no boot 118 .

MOWBRAY Myself I throw, dread 119 sovereign, at thy foot.

Kneels

My life thou shalt command, but not my shame:

The one my duty owes, but my fair name 120 ,

Despite of death that lives upon my grave,

To dark dishonour’s use thou shalt not have.

I am disgraced, impeached 121 and baffled 122 here,

Pierced to the soul with slander’s venomed spear,

The which no balm 123 can cure but his heart-blood

Which breathed 124 this poison.

KING RICHARD Rage must be withstood.

Give me his gage. Lions make leopards 125 tame.

MOWBRAY Yea, but not change his spots 126 . Take 127 but my shame,

And I resign my gage. My dear dear lord,

The purest treasure mortal times afford

Is spotless reputation: that away 128 ,

Men are but gilded 129 loam 130 or painted clay.

A jewel in a ten-times-barred-up 131 chest 132

Is a bold spirit in a loyal breast.

Mine honour is my life; both grow in one 133 :

Take honour from me, and my life is done.

Then, dear my liege, mine honour let me try 134 .

In that I live and for that will I die.

KING RICHARD Cousin, throw down your gage. Do you begin.

BULLINGBROOK O, heaven defend my soul from such foul sin!

Shall I seem crest-fall’n 135 in my father’s sight?

Or with pale beggar-fear impeach my height 136

Before this out-dared 137 dastard 138 ? Ere my tongue

Shall wound mine honour with such feeble wrong,

Or sound so base a parle 139 , my teeth shall tear

The slavish motive 140 of recanting 141 fear,

And spit it bleeding in his 142 high disgrace,

Where shame doth harbour 143 , even in Mowbray’s face.

Exit Gaunt

KING RICHARD We were not born to sue 144 , but to command,

Which since we cannot do to make you friends,

Be ready, as your lives shall answer it,

At Coventry upon Saint Lambert’s day 145 :

There shall your swords and lances arbitrate

The swelling 146 difference of your settled 147 hate.

Since we cannot atone 148 you, we shall see

Justice design 149 the victor’s chivalry 150 .

Lord Marshal, command our officers at arms

Be ready to direct these home alarms 151 .

Exeunt
