grasp

Pronunciation
/ɡrɑːsp/ (GRAHSP) – stress on the first syllable.

Etymology & Root
- From Old English græppian “to seize,” related to Germanic grap- “抓住”。
- Root: Proto-Germanic grap- / greb-* “抓、握” (same family as grip, grab).

English-to-English explanation
1. (verb) To take and hold something firmly with your hands or mind.
- Example: Grasp the handle tightly. / She grasped the concept quickly.

  1. (noun) A firm hold or understanding.
    • Example: He has a good grasp of mathematics.

Common uses: “Beyond my grasp” (too difficult to understand), “grasp an opportunity” (seize it).

utter

Pronunciation
/ˈʌt̬.ɚ/ (UT-ər) – stress on the first syllable.

Etymology & Root
- From Middle English utteren “to speak, reveal,” from Old English ūtian “to put out”。
- Root: Germanic *ūt- “outward” (related to out, outer).

English-to-English explanation
1. (verb) To say or speak something.
- Example: He didn’t utter a single word.

  1. (adjective) Complete or absolute (used for emphasis).
    • Example: It was utter chaos / utter nonsense.

Common uses: “Utter silence” (complete silence), “utter disappointment” (total).

cardinal

Pronunciation
/ˈkɑːr.dɪ.nəl/ (KAR-di-nəl) – stress on the first syllable.

Etymology & Root
- From Latin cardinalis “principal, chief,” from cardo “hinge” (something everything turns on).
- Root: Latin *card- “hinge, pivot” (same family as cardiac “heart,” the “hinge” of life).

English-to-English explanation
1. (adjective) Most important or fundamental.
- Example: A cardinal rule / cardinal principle.

  1. (noun) A high-ranking official in the Roman Catholic Church (wearing red robes).
    • Example: The Pope appointed three new cardinals.
  2. (noun) A bright red North American songbird.
Common uses: “Cardinal sin” (serious wrongdoing), “cardinal directions” (N, S, E, W), “cardinal number” (1, 2, 3… vs ordinal).
