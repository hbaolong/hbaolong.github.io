单词故事

Pronunciation of “witness”

  • American English (most common in the US): /ˈwɪt.nəs/
    • Stress on the first syllable: WIT-ness
    • The “t” is often a light flap (like a quick “d”) in casual speech, sounding roughly like “WIH-dnəs”.
  • British English (Received Pronunciation / General British): /ˈwɪt.nəs/ or /ˈwɪt.nɪs/
    • Also stress on first syllable: WIT-nəs
    • The second vowel can be slightly more open or schwa-like.

Both varieties are very similar; the main difference is subtle vowel quality in the unstressed syllable and occasional flapping of /t/ in American speech.

Root (词根)

The core root is Old English wit (from Proto-Germanic *witą), meaning: - knowledge - understanding - mind / intelligence / sense - to know

This is the same root found in modern English words: - wit (n. = cleverness, intelligence; also the verb “to wit” = namely) - wise (adj.) - wisdom - German wissen (“to know”) - Related to wider Indo-European family words for “see / know” (e.g., Latin vidēre “to see” is a distant cousin via different path)

Etymology (词源)

  • Old English (pre-1150, recorded before 950): witnes or ġewitnes
    → literally “knowledge-state” or “the condition of knowing”
    → formed as wit (knowledge/mind) + -nes (abstract noun suffix, → “-ness”)

  • Originally meant knowledge, testimony, or evidence that comes from personal knowing (not hearsay).

  • By late Old English it already could mean both:

    1. the testimony itself (abstract)
    2. the person who provides that testimony (concrete)

Key historical development & story

  1. Old English period (~950–1100)
    → Mainly “knowledge / testimony / evidence” from personal experience.
    → Also already used for “a person who knows / testifies”.
    → Frequently glossed Latin testimonium (“testimony”) in religious texts.

  2. Middle English (~1150–1500)
    → Form becomes witnesse.
    → Legal sense strengthens (someone who can give sworn evidence in court).
    → Religious sense appears strongly: late 14th century, “witness” translates Greek martys (μάρτυς = witness) → becomes the source of English martyr (“one who bears witness to faith”, especially by dying for it).

  3. Early Modern English (~1500–1700)
    → Shift accelerates toward “eyewitness / one who personally sees”.
    → Why? In law and everyday life, the most reliable form of “knowing” is having seen with your own eyes.
    → Compound “eyewitness” appears ~1530s to emphasize visual witnessing.
    → Verb use (“to witness” = to see personally / to attest) becomes common around 13th–14th century, but explodes in frequency in this period (e.g., Shakespeare uses it frequently).

  4. Modern English (1700–present)
    → Dominant meaning today: “eyewitness” or “one who sees an event” (especially accidents, crimes).
    → Legal meaning remains strong: courtroom witness, expert witness, character witness.
    → Broader figurative use: “History has witnessed many changes” = has “seen / experienced”.
    → Religious group Jehovah’s Witnesses (from 1931) deliberately uses the biblical sense of “bearing witness / testifying about God”.

Summary of the big meaning shift

Started as → “knowledge / testimony from knowing”
Shifted to → “testimony from seeing”
Ended up mostly meaning → “person who saw it happen”

This is a classic example of how English words move from abstract mental states (“knowing”) to concrete sensory experience (“seeing”), because vision is culturally treated as the most trustworthy form of knowledge.
