2. Influences on Wittgenstein

In 1931 Wittgenstein wrote in his notebook of the influences upon him:

I think there is some truth in my idea that I am really only reproductive in my thinking. I think I have never invented a line of thinking but that it was always provided for me by someone else andandandand I have done no more than passionately taken it up for my work of clarification. That is how Boltzmann, Hertz, Schopenhauer, Frege, Russell, Kraus, Loos, Weininger, Spengler, Sraffa have influenced me. (CV, p. 16)

Wittgenstein does not do justice here to his own creativity but the list of influences is revealing. He had originally just written ‘Frege, Russell, Spengler, Sraffa’, but then added the other names above the line. Oswald Spengler (1880–1936), through his main work, The Decline of the West, published in two volumes in 1918 and 1922, only influenced him after the Tractatus was finished, and Piero Sraffa (1898–1983) only influenced him on his return to Cambridge in 1929. As far as the Tractatus is concerned, Wittgenstein’s greatest debt is undoubtedly to Gottlob Frege (1848–1925) and Bertrand Russell (1882–1970), both of whom he singles out in his preface to the Tractatus. Here he did indeed passionately take up their lines of thinking in his work of clarification. We will consider the influence of Frege and Russell in turn,5 before looking, much more briefly, at the influence of Heinrich Hertz (1857–94), Ludwig Boltzmann (1844–1906), and Arthur Schopenhauer (1788–1860).

2.1. Frege’s Influence

Frege is one of the main founders of modern logic—of what is now known as predicate or quantificational logic, a form of logic (understood here as logical theory) that quickly proved to be far more powerful than traditional logic, which had combined (Aristotelian) syllogistic theory and (Stoic) propositional logic. Propositional logic, which is now seen as more basic, and on which Frege also built, concerns the logical relations between propositions, represented as ‘p’, ‘q’, ‘r’, etc., and their logical compounds, propositions combined by means of one or more logical connectives, such as ‘not’, ‘and’, ‘or’, ‘if … then …’, yielding ‘not p’, ‘p and q’, ‘p or q’, ‘if p, then q’, and so on. Here is just a sample of the logical relations that obtain between such propositions and their compounds. The conjunctive proposition ‘p and q’, for example, implies the disjunctive proposition ‘p or q’ (though not vice versa), which we can represent as the conditional proposition ‘If p and q, then p or q’. ‘If p, then q’ is equivalent to ‘if not q, then not p’ (its contrapositive), and ‘p and q’ is equivalent to ‘not (not p or not q)’, showing the interdefinability of the logical connectives. The various relations can be formulated as logical laws. For example, ‘not (p and not p)’ is the law (or principle) of non-contradiction: a proposition and its negation cannot both be true. ‘p or not p’ is the law of excluded middle: of any proposition, either it or its negation is true. It is natural to think that logical laws tell us something, but one of Wittgenstein’s most important claims in the Tractatus is that logical propositions (logical laws and all that follows from them) are senseless, a claim that was seen as clarifying their status (see below).

Syllogistic theory is concerned with the logical relations between four specific kinds of proposition, ‘All As are Bs’, ‘No As are Bs’, ‘Some As are Bs’, and ‘Some As are not Bs’, where ‘A’ and ‘B’ represent the relevant predicate (what is being attributed to something), and ‘all’, no’, and ‘some’ are quantifier terms, stating how much of something is under consideration. Here again there are logical relations to make explicit: ‘All As are Bs’ implies ‘Some As are Bs’, for example, while contradicting ‘Some As are not Bs’ (as Aristotle construed these). While traditional logic gave a coherent account of these kinds of proposition, however, it had difficulty handling propositions with more than one quantifier, such as ‘All philosophers respect some logician’. Frege’s achievement was to invent a notation for quantification that enabled him to formalize propositions involving multiple quantification.

Frege’s key move lay in extending function–argument analysis from mathematics to logic. In analytic geometry, in representing a line as y = ax + b, we exhibit y as a function of x, where x represents the argument of the function and a and b are constants, a being the gradient of the line and b the point where the line cuts the y-axis on a graph. If a = 2 and b = 3, for example, then we have the function y = 2x + 3: for argument x = 1, the value of the function is 5; for x = 2, y = 7, and so on. Frege generalized the idea: not just mathematical equations but all propositions can be analysed in function–argument terms. Take the simplest kind of proposition, ascribing a predicate to a subject, such as ‘Gottlob is human’. Traditional logic had analysed this as having the form ‘S is P’, with ‘S’ representing the subject (‘Gottlob’) and ‘P’ the predicate (‘human’), connected by the copula (‘is’). Frege, however, analysed it as having the form ‘Fa’, with ‘a’ representing the argument (‘Gottlob’) and ‘Fx’ the function (‘x is human’), the variable x indicating where the argument term goes to complete the proposition. ‘Gottlob is human’ is thus taken to be the value of the functional expression ‘x is human’ for the argument term ‘Gottlob’.

Relational propositions are analysed similarly, in this case as functions of two or more arguments. In analysing ‘Gottlob is shorter than Bertrand’, for example, ‘Gottlob’ and ‘Bertrand’ are taken as the argument terms and ‘x is shorter than y’ as the relational expression, symbolized as ‘Rxy’ or ‘xRy’. The power of function–argument analysis, however, only fully comes out in analysing propositions involving quantifiers. Take the proposition ‘All logicians are human’. In traditional logic this was seen as having the same form as ‘Gottlob is human’, the subject being ‘all logicians’ rather than ‘Gottlob’, and the copula plural (‘are’) rather than singular (‘is’). But on Frege’s analysis, it has a very different form, symbolized in modern notation as ‘(∀x) (Lx ⊃ Hx)’, read as ‘For all x, if x is a logician, then x is human’. Here what we have are two functional expressions, ‘x is a logician’ and ‘x is human’, joined by the logical connective ‘if … then …’ (symbolized by ‘⊃’) and bound by the universal quantifier (‘for all x’, represented using an inverted ‘A’). (In the notation that Wittgenstein takes over from Russell, the universal quantifier is symbolized simply by ‘(x)’ rather than ‘(∀x)’.6)

‘Some logicians are human’ is also analysed as having a more complex quantificational form, symbolized in modern notation as ‘(∃x) (Lx andandandand Hx)’, read as ‘There is some x such that x is a logician and x is human’. Again what we have here are two functional expressions joined by a logical connective, in this case ‘and’ (symbolized by ‘andandandand’) and bound by the existential quantifier (‘there is some x’, represented using a backwards ‘E’). (In the notation that Wittgenstein takes over from Russell, ‘and’ is symbolized by the full stop ‘.’ rather than ‘andandandand’. ‘(∃x)’ is used for the existential quantifier.) With such notation, propositions involving multiple quantification can be formalized. ‘All philosophers respect some logician’, for example, turns out to be ambiguous, depending on whether we are claiming that there is some (at least one) logician whom every philosopher respects or merely that, for any philosopher, there is some logician whom they respect (not necessarily the same one). Quantificational logic provides a neat way of clarifying this ambiguity:

  1. (∃y) (Ly andandandand (∀x) (Px ⊃ Rxy)).

  2. (∀x) (Px ⊃ (∃y) (Ly andandandand Rxy)).

The first can be read as ‘There is some y such that y is a logician and for all x, if x is a philosopher, then x respects y’, and the second as ‘For all x, if x is a philosopher, then there is some y such that y is a logician and x respects y’. The difference in meaning is reflected in the order of the quantifiers—either ∃∀ or ∀∃. Furthermore, their logical relations are different, reflected in their asymmetry: while the second can be inferred from the first, the first cannot be inferred from the second (mistakenly thinking that it can is known as the quantifier shift fallacy).

We see here an excellent example of the power of Frege’s logic. The use of quantificational notation clarifies the relevant logical relations and facilitates the drawing and testing of inferences. Frege called the logical notation he created ‘Begriffsschrift’ (literally, ‘concept-script’), a term that Wittgenstein takes over in the Tractatus, though he criticizes the precise form that Frege’s (and Russell’s) notation took (see e.g. 3.325, 5.533). On Wittgenstein’s view, it is only once ‘everything is in order in our sign-language’ that we attain a correct logical conception (4.1213). So here, too, one can see him as taking up in his work of clarification the line of thinking that Frege invented.

In doing so, Wittgenstein also endorsed function–argument analysis: ‘I construe a proposition—like Frege and Russell—as a function of the expressions contained in it’ (3.318). But once again, his own account of this was rather different from theirs. For Frege, the most fundamental logical relation was the relation of an object’s falling under a concept, expressed in the most basic kind of thought we can have, such as in thinking that Gottlob is human: applying the concept human, understood as a function (‘x is human’), to the particular object named Gottlob (the name filling the argument-place in the functional expression). Wittgenstein left it open what the basic kinds of thoughts were, however: this would be revealed by logical analysis. They might be relational propositions of the form ‘Rxy’ or ‘Rxyz’ instead. But he assumed that there was an ultimate level to be reached, comprising what he called ‘elementary propositions’ (4.21), each understood as a function of names (4.24). In elaborating on this, he drew on one of the central ideas of the Tractatus, that a proposition should be seen as a picture of a state of affairs (2.11 ff.). We will return to this in the next section.

As to the relationship between elementary propositions and other, more complex propositions, here, too, he saw a functional relationship: ‘A proposition is a truth-function of elementary propositions’ (5). He elaborates this conception in the rest of the 5s (the remarks that comment on 5), and we will return to this in §4. Once again, then, we can see Wittgenstein as ‘clarifying’ the line of thinking opened up by the possibility of function–argument analysis. At the root of this, for Frege, was the fundamental logical relation of an object’s falling under a concept, but we can see Wittgenstein as taking the more general idea of a functional relation itself as the most fundamental.

Wittgenstein’s account of all this, as elaborated in the Tractatus, was intended to overcome the various problems that he saw in Frege’s logic and philosophy—and, as we will shortly see, also in Russell’s logic and philosophy. Two of the key problems concern Frege’s distinction between object and concept and what is now known as Russell’s paradox. The latter will be taken up in discussing Russell’s influence, so let us consider the first here. Frege regarded the distinction between object and concept as absolute, characterizing it, metaphorically, by describing objects as ‘saturated’ and concepts as ‘unsaturated’, reflecting the fact that while an object is represented by a name, formalized by a letter such as ‘a’, a concept is represented by a functional expression of the form ‘( ) is F’, where the gap indicates the argument-place where the name goes, yielding the sentence ‘a is F’, formalized as ‘Fa’, representing the thought that a is F. Names and concept-words (functional expressions) perform different semantic roles, which Frege took to indicate an ontological distinction between object and concept.

Notoriously, however, this distinction was not something that Frege could formalize in his Begriffsschrift, which he had envisaged as capable of expressing, in principle, every possible kind of thought. We might suggest expressing the distinction in ordinary language by saying ‘No objects are concepts’ or, equivalently, ‘No concepts are objects’, but attempting to formalize this, say, as ‘For all x, if x is an object, then it is not the case that x is a concept’ (‘(∀x) (Ox ⊃ ~Cx)’, symbolizing ‘not’ by ‘~’), presupposes that both names (of objects) and concept-words can go in the argument-place (replacing the variable ‘x’) in both ‘x is an object’ and ‘x is a concept’. But if ‘x’ is replaced by a concept-word of the form ‘( ) is F’, then we get ‘( ) is F is an object’ and ‘( ) is F is a concept’, neither of which is a well-formed sentence (and would remain ill-formed even if the gap was filled, to yield, say, ‘a is F is an object’ and ‘a is F is a concept’). So there is a genuine problem in trying to state the distinction between object and concept. (Objections might in turn be raised to how the foregoing explanation is formulated, or to other attempts to state or explain the distinction.)

The problem raised here is not just a problem for Frege or confined to just one kind of statement. Wittgenstein recognized this early on in his thinking. In the notes dictated to Moore in April 1914, he remarks: ‘In order that you should have a language which can express or say everything that can be said, this language must have certain properties; and when this is the case, that it has them can no longer be said in that language or any language’ (NB, 108). In many ways we can see here the whole project of the Tractatus: to try to say what these properties are, while recognizing that—ultimately—they cannot be said at all. The penultimate proposition (6.54) of the Tractatus is already anticipated. Frege had tried to explain his distinction, on which he felt his whole logic was based, but had himself realized the difficulties in doing so—especially in his 1892 essay ‘On Concept and Object’, which Wittgenstein knew well. In clarifying Frege’s line of thinking in this case, Wittgenstein generalized the problem and attempted to address it.

It is in these notes to Moore that he introduces (for the first time) his key distinction between saying (sagen) and showing (zeigen). Indeed, the notes open with the claim: ‘Logical so-called propositions show [the] logical properties of language and therefore of [the] Universe, but say nothing’ (NB, 108; cf. 6.12). It would be tempting to treat ‘No objects are concepts’ as just such a logical proposition, showing the distinction between object and concept that Frege took to underpin his logical language. On Wittgenstein’s view, however, this temptation should be resisted, since the concepts of object and of concept are what he calls formal concepts, different from ‘real’ concepts such as the concept human. Something is an object, for example, if it can be named and fall under real concepts; and something is a real concept if it can be represented by a functional expression and applied to objects. Something is not an object or concept in virtue of falling under the supposed concept of object or of concept, respectively. Formal concepts, he held, were pseudo-concepts, and should not be confused with real concepts (4.126–4.1274).

According to Wittgenstein, then, when we think it through, ‘No objects are concepts’ turns out to be nonsense, although, in seeing how this is so, we are led to recognize the roles that names and concept-words play in our language, the difference between them being shown in our different uses of the two kinds of expression. Distinguishing—in his work of clarification—between formal and real concepts may also help us in appreciating how many of the things we are tempted to say in ‘explaining’ such apparent statements as ‘No objects are concepts’ are problematic and, strictly speaking, nonsensical. (As we will see, though, he does allow for a genuine category of ‘logical propositions’, which he characterizes as senseless rather than nonsensical.)

2.2. Russell’s Influence

Let us now turn to Russell’s influence, beginning with the paradox that bears his name. This arose in the context of what is called Frege’s logicist project: the attempt to demonstrate that arithmetic can be reduced to logic. Frege’s invention of quantificational logic had been the first step, enabling him to formalize mathematical propositions, where multiple quantification is prevalent, as in ‘Every number has a successor’, and give a logical analysis of mathematical reasoning, such as mathematical induction. The next step was to define the natural numbers, an informal account of which he gave in his philosophical masterpiece, The Foundations of Arithmetic (1884).

The essential idea was to define numbers as classes or extensions of concepts. What is called the extension of a concept is the class or set of things that fall under that concept. Under the concept human, for example, fall Frege, Russell, Wittgenstein, etc. All of these objects (people) are members of the class of humans—the extension of the concept human. According to Frege, this class is itself a kind of object, not a ‘concrete’ (empirical, spatio-temporal) object but an ‘abstract’ object—in this case a logical object, since the idea of a class had traditionally been seen as a logical notion. The task was then to find suitable logical concepts. Again, traditionally, two of the most fundamental concepts of logic are the concepts of identity and of negation. Take the concept of identity, or more precisely, the concept of being identical with itself. (Wittgenstein came to reject this conception of identity (5.53 ff.), but Frege—and Russell—accepted it.) Every object is identical with itself (so it might obviously seem), in other words, every object falls under the concept identical with itself. So the corresponding class has as its members all objects. Now let us add the concept of negation to form the concept not identical with itself. Nothing is not identical with itself. So the corresponding class here has no members at all. This is what logicians call the ‘null class’, and is here defined purely by means of logical concepts, as the class of things that are not self-identical.

This null class can then be used to define the first of the natural numbers, the number 0. The simplest definition is to identify the number 0 with the null class, as is done in modern set theory. Frege offers a more complicated definition, identifying the number 0 with the class of classes that have the same number of members as the null class, for reasons that we need not go into here—except to note his appeal to a class of classes, which we will pick up shortly. Let us accept the simplest definition. We can then form the concept is identical with 0 (i.e. the concept is identical with the null class). Here the corresponding class has just one member, namely, 0 (the null class itself). This class (the class of things that are identical with 0) is distinct from its sole member (0, i.e. the null class), since the former has one member and the latter has no members, so we can identify the number 1 with this class (the class of things that are identical with 0). We now have two objects, and can then form the concept is identical with 0 or 1 (using the additional logical concept of disjunction). This gives us a corresponding class which we can identify with the number 2, and so on. Starting with the null class, then, and using only logical concepts, we can define all the natural numbers.

The strategy is ingenious. What could possibly go wrong? Let us identify two of the key assumptions. First, there is the principle that for every concept, there is a class of things that fall under it. (If nothing falls under it, then there is still a class—the null class.) Second, there is the assumption that classes are themselves objects, in which case they can also be members of classes. All we need is a relevant concept under which they fall. The concept class is one such concept, which means that a class can be a member of itself, as indeed the class of classes is.

These assumptions, however, lead to a contradiction. Consider the class of humans. This class is not itself a human, so the class is not a member of itself. Consider the class of non-humans. This class, too, is not a human, but in this case it means that it is a member of itself. So classes divide into those that are members of themselves and those that are not members of themselves. Consider now the class of all classes that are not members of themselves. Is this a member of itself or not? If it is, then since it is the class of all classes that are not members of themselves, it is not. If it is not, then since this is the defining property of the classes it contains, it is. We have a contradiction.

This contradiction is now known as Russell’s paradox, since it was Russell who discovered and first informed Frege of it in writing to him in June 1902. Given that Frege had defined numbers in terms of classes—and indeed, classes of classes, as noted above—determined by logically legitimate concepts, Russell’s paradox was potentially devastating. Frege recognized its seriousness immediately, and although he tried to respond to it in the appendix to the second volume of his Basic Laws of Arithmetic (1903), in which he had been trying to demonstrate the logicist thesis formally, using his Begriffsschrift, it soon led to his abandoning the logicist project. Russell, however, persevered, and developed what he called the theory of types to resolve it.

Leaving aside the intricacies of the theory as it was eventually formulated in Principia Mathematica, which Russell wrote with A. N. Whitehead (1861–1947), his former teacher and then colleague at Cambridge, the essential idea is that there is a hierarchy of objects and classes. At the most basic level, there are ‘genuine’ objects—objects such as humans, horses, apples, cups, pens, and so on. At the next level, there are classes of objects—such as the class of humans and the class of non-humans (which contains all those genuine objects that are not humans). Then there are classes of classes of objects, and so on up the hierarchy. The key point is that something at any given level can only be a member of a class at the next level up. This automatically rules out any class being a member of itself; so Russell’s paradox cannot be generated.

In explaining his theory of types, however, Russell said just the kinds of thing that Wittgenstein found problematic. Consider the statement ‘No (genuine) objects are first-level classes’. This is just as nonsensical as the statement ‘No objects are concepts’ considered above, and for an analogous reason. To say that something is a first-level class is to say that it is a member of the class of first-level classes, but this latter class is a second-level class and one cannot (sensefully) say or deny of any genuine object that it is a member of a second-level class, only of a first-level class. In trying to state the theory of types we violate the very type-restrictions it encodes. In saying, for example, that ‘Gottlob (as an object) is not a (first-level) class’, we appear to be saying something about the meaning of the name ‘Gottlob’, that it (i.e. Gottlob himself) does not have the property of being a (first-level) class. But this is not how to get across the relevant type-restrictions, which are shown by our senseful use of the relevant expressions (3.326). In 3.33 Wittgenstein writes: ‘In logical syntax the meaning of a sign should never play a role; it must permit being drawn up without talk of the meaning of a sign; it should presuppose only the description of the expressions.’ This gives us insight, he immediately goes on to say, into Russell’s theory of types: ‘Russell’s error is shown in his having to talk of the meaning of signs in drawing up the rules for them’ (3.331).

The theory of types, however, was only one part of Russell’s response to the paradox. The other main part was his theory of descriptions, which was intended to address the problem of how definite descriptions, that is, terms of the form ‘the F’, can contribute to the meaning and truth-value of propositions in which they occur when they lack—or appear to lack—a meaning themselves. ‘The class of all classes that are not members of themselves’ seems meaningful and yet there can be no such class. If classes are not genuine objects at all, as Russell came to believe, then what do class terms refer to, and how do we manage to say true or false things about classes? Analysis of our talk of classes is clearly called for.

Let us consider Russell’s own famous example of a proposition involving a definite description:

(K1) The present King of France is bald.

If we were to treat this as having the form ‘S is P’, in accord with traditional subject–predicate analysis, then we would regard ‘the present King of France’ as the subject term. But if there is no King of France, then what is the proposition about? A non-existent object? An object that has existence in some other sense, say, as ‘subsistence’? How can we understand such a proposition if the subject term fails to refer? Can it have a truth-value in such a case?

In ‘On Denoting’, published in 1905, Russell introduced his theory of descriptions to answer these questions. On the account he offered (reconstructed here), (K1) is analysed into a conjunction of the following three propositions, each of which can be readily formalized in quantificational logic (given in square brackets afterwards), with ‘K’ representing the concept King of France and ‘B’ the concept bald:

(Ka) There is at least one King of France. [(∃x) Kx]

(Kb) There is at most one King of France. [(∀x) (∀y) (Kx andandandand Ky ⊃ y = x)]

(Kc) Whatever is King of France is bald. [(∀x) (Kx ⊃ Bx)]

Each of these constituent propositions has a quantificational structure, and can be interpreted as saying something about a concept, not an object. (Ka) says that the concept King of France is instantiated (by at least one object), and (Kb) says that it is instantiated by at most one object (roughly speaking, that were it to seem as if two objects fell under the concept, then they would actually be the same object). Taken together, (Ka) and (Kb) thus say that the concept King of France is uniquely instantiated. (Kc) says that whatever instantiates the concept King of France also instantiates the concept bald.

Putting all three together, we have:

(K2) There is one and only one King of France and whatever is King of France is bald.

Formalizing this (and simplifying) yields:

(K3) (∃x) (Kx andandandand (∀y) (Ky ⊃ y = x) andandandand Bx).

This can then be interpreted as saying that the concept King of France is uniquely instantiated and whatever instantiates this concept also instantiates the concept bald.

With this analysis we can now answer our earlier questions. The surface grammatical form of (K1) is misleading: it has a much more complex logical form. It is not about a non-existent or subsistent object, but about a concept; and all we need to grasp to understand the proposition are the two relevant concepts, the concept King of France and the concept bald, as well as the relevant logical ideas (conjunction, implication, identity, and existential and universal quantification). Where there is no King of France, that is, where the subject term fails to refer, the first conjunct of the analysis—(Ka)—is false, thereby making (K1) false. So (K1) still comes out as having a truth-value.

Russell’s theory of descriptions has often been taken—rightly—as a paradigm of analysis, although it arguably only offers a correct account of certain uses of definite descriptions. Wittgenstein endorsed it in a letter to Russell in late 1913: ‘your “Theory of Descriptions” is quite CERTAINLY correct, even though the individual primitive signs in it are not at all the ones you thought’ (WC, p. 59). Its philosophical significance lies in the distinction it made explicit between grammatical form and logical form. As Wittgenstein put it in the Tractatus, ‘Russell’s merit is to have shown that the apparent logical form of a proposition need not be its real one’ (4.0031). (K1) looks as if it is a simple subject–predicate proposition, misleading us into thinking that there is a King of France (or must exist in some sense if we can talk about it), but it turns out to have a more complex, quantificational structure. We understand it, not by knowing who the present King of France is (since there is no such person), but by understanding the relevant concepts—and the existential and universal quantification involved.

Russell’s approach here had built on Frege’s analysis of number statements. One of the central claims of Frege’s logicism was that a number statement is an assertion about a concept.7 In saying that Jupiter has four moons, for example, one is saying that the concept moon of Jupiter has four instances,8 which can be readily formalized in quantificational logic. We can adopt a similar approach in analysing our talk of classes. Consider the following simple example:

(C1) The class of logicians is a subclass of the class of humans.

Do classes have to ‘exist’ for (C1) to be true? Not at all, on Russell’s view, since this can be seen as equivalent to, and hence analysed as (on the lines of (Kc) above), the following:

(C2) Whatever falls under the concept logician falls under the concept human. [(∀x) (Lx ⊃ Hx)]

(C1), in other words, is just another way of saying that all logicians are humans, and what (C2) makes clear is that we understand such a proposition by grasping the concepts logician and human together with the relevant logical concepts. Classes, on Russell’s view, are logical fictions, formed from more basic logical concepts in accordance with certain logical principles. The details need not concern us further here.

Russell’s theory of descriptions, as well as the analyses that Frege and Russell provided of mathematical propositions, opened up the possibility of a whole programme of analysis, ‘reducing’ propositions that might puzzle or mislead us in one way or another to more basic propositions whose understanding is (supposedly) less problematic. Russell called the most basic propositions of all ‘atomic propositions’, and Wittgenstein called them ‘elementary propositions’. The project of logical atomism, as it came to be called, was thus to clarify both the nature of these basic propositions and the process by which complex propositions were formed from them. This was the main task of clarification that Wittgenstein took up in response to the lines of thinking opened up by Frege and Russell, convinced as he soon became that it was only by doing this that he could show what was wrong with Russell’s paradox.

2.3. The Influence of Hertz, Boltzmann, and Schopenhauer

There are three others on Wittgenstein’s list of 1931 who also had an influence on the ideas of the Tractatus, and they deserve mention here as well. Of the three, only Hertz’s name appears in the Tractatus—in 4.04 and 6.361. Hertz was a German physicist whose key work, The Principles of Mechanics Presented in a New Form, was published posthumously in 1894, which Wittgenstein may have come across at school and would certainly have been familiar with while studying engineering in Berlin and Manchester. Hertz conceived scientific theories as pictorial representations or models, and laid down three criteria for their acceptability: logical permissibility, correctness, and appropriateness.9 Hertz’s ‘picture theory’ helped shape Wittgenstein’s own account of pictures, although it is hard to say exactly how, since Hertz is only named once in this context. The third criterion is mentioned under the characterization ‘logical multiplicity’ in 4.04, where Wittgenstein refers to Hertz’s Mechanics.

Hertz’s influence is much more visible in the 6.3s, where the idea of different forms of describing the world is considered. Hertz had discussed three different systems of mechanics, including the one he advocated. His proposed system replaced Newton’s laws with a single fundamental law, together with a hypothesis of invisible masses, from which the rest of mechanics could be derived.10 There is an allusion to this in 6.343, without mention of either Hertz or his hypothesis (though they are mentioned in the notebook entry from which this remark came; NB, p. 36). We can also speculate that both Wittgenstein’s idea of a general propositional form and his appeal to simple objects (see below) were inspired by Hertz’s mechanics.

Hertz’s greatest influence on Wittgenstein was arguably his more philosophical views. In discussing the problems we get into in trying to reconcile the various relations that have accumulated around the ideas of force and electricity, Hertz writes:

We have an obscure feeling of this and want to have things cleared up. Our confused wish finds expression in the confused question as to the nature of force and electricity. But the answer which we want is not really an answer to this question. It is not by finding out more and fresh relations and connections that it can be answered; but by removing the contradictions existing between those already known, and thus perhaps by reducing their number. When these painful contradictions are removed, the question as to the nature of force will not have been answered; but our minds, no longer vexed, will cease to ask illegitimate questions.11

This finds a direct echo in Wittgenstein’s own remarks about philosophy in the Tractatus (see 4.003, 4.112, and especially the 6.5s). Wittgenstein took up some of Hertz’s lines of thinking, then, not so much to clarify ideas that Hertz had (though he partly does that) but to apply them in clarifying the problems he found in Frege’s and Russell’s work.

Boltzmann was an Austrian physicist who founded statistical mechanics. As well as a two-volume work on this, he also published a collection of his popular writings (1905), which Wittgenstein owned and read. Wittgenstein had apparently wanted to study with Boltzmann in Vienna after school, but Boltzmann’s suicide in September 1906 resulted in his going to Berlin instead. Like Hertz, Boltzmann developed a ‘picture theory’ of science, which may also have helped shape Wittgenstein’s account of pictures, but it was again his more philosophical views that arguably had the greatest influence. Boltzmann claimed that certain philosophical questions, such as about the value of life or the most fundamental laws of physics, were illegitimate, reflecting the mind’s tendency to ‘overshoot’. If philosophy could thus create a system, Boltzmann wrote, such that ‘it stood out clearly when a question is not justified so that the drive towards asking it would gradually die away, we should at one stroke have resolved the most obscure riddles and philosophy would become worthy of the name of queen of the sciences’.12 There is an echo of this in 6.5 and 6.521 of the Tractatus, and Wittgenstein may well have drawn inspiration from Boltzmann in developing just such a philosophical ‘system’.

Much has been made of Schopenhauer’s influence on Wittgenstein. He apparently read Schopenhauer’s major work, The World as Will and Representation (1818), at the age of 16, and was attracted to its epistemological idealism. Schopenhauer’s philosophy has to be understood as a response to Kant’s philosophy. According to Kant, the world as we experience it—the phenomenal world—is formed by both the spatio-temporal framework and the conceptual scheme that we impose on it. This is his so-called transcendental idealism, specifying the conditions for knowledge to be possible at all. There is sensory input that we work on, so Kant also claimed to be an empirical realist. But he maintained, too, that there is a noumenal world, comprising ‘things-in-themselves’, lying behind the phenomenal world, which somehow gives rise to the sensory content that we synthesize into knowledge. This is what the philosophers who came after him, including Schopenhauer, criticized. To regard things-in-themselves as ‘giving rise’ to the phenomenal world amounts to projecting the concept of causality outside its legitimate (empirical) domain.

Instead, Schopenhauer argued that the world should be conceived as both will (Wille) and representation (Vorstellung), understood as two aspects, the operation of the will taking over the work of Kant’s ‘noumenal causality’. Knowledge of will, as a non-rational impulse, is gained through introspection, according to Schopenhauer. In being aware of our body, for example, we perceive it both as a physical object and as something we can move by willing. Schopenhauer’s account has solipsistic implications, and both these and his general conception of the will are clearly in the background to Wittgenstein’s remarks in the 5.6s. Schopenhauer is not named in the Tractatus, but he is mentioned once in the surviving notebooks:

Good and evil only enter through the subject. And the subject does not belong to the world but is a limit of the world.

One could say (following Schopenhauer): The world of representation is neither good nor bad; only the willing subject is. (NB, p. 79).13

The second sentence of this becomes 5.632, but immediately after these remarks in the notebooks, Wittgenstein goes on: ‘I am aware of the complete unclarity of all these propositions.’ The issue of whether Wittgenstein himself was an idealist of some kind in the Tractatus, and what his attitude to solipsism was, is fiercely debated. But Wittgenstein’s own comment here makes one cautious about any interpretation.
