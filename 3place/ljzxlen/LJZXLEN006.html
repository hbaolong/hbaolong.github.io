3. Wittgenstein’s key early ideas

Before outlining the main structure of the Tractatus and its central claims, let us briefly consider some of the key ideas that Wittgenstein developed in response to Frege and Russell that were later incorporated into the Tractatus. The selection here has been governed by the aim of elucidating some of the most characteristic themes of the Tractatus.

3.1. Repudiation of Logical Constants

In one of his earliest letters to Russell, dated 22 June 1912, Wittgenstein wrote:

Logic is still in the melting-pot but one thing gets more and more obvious to me: The prop[osition]s of Logic contain ONLY APPARENT variables and whatever may turn out to be the proper explanation of apparent variables, its consequences must be that there are NO logical constants.

Logic must turn out to be of a TOTALLY different kind than any other science.   (WC, p. 30; NB, p. 120)

In his own earliest work, Russell had held that any meaningful term must denote something, what it denotes constituting its meaning. This included the logical connectives, ‘not’, ‘and’, ‘or’, and so on. Even when the theory of descriptions showed how some phrases could be ‘analysed away’ to avoid the assumption that they must denote something for sentences in which they occur to have meaning, he did not take this to apply to the logical connectives themselves.

Wittgenstein explains why he rejects Russell’s view at the very beginning of the notes that he left for Russell before departing to Norway in October 1913: ‘One reason for thinking the old notation [i.e. Russell’s] wrong is that it is very unlikely that from every proposition p an infinite number of other propositions not-not-p, not-not-not-not-p, etc. should follow’ (NB, p. 93). The thinking is straightforward: if ‘not’ denoted something, then ‘not-not-p’ would say something different from just ‘p’; and then it would indeed seem remarkable that we can deduce an infinite number of different propositions from just one proposition. When this remark found its way into 5.43 of the Tractatus, itself a comment on 5.4, where Wittgenstein denies that there are ‘logical objects’, he diagnoses the error: ‘All propositions of logic, however, say the same. Namely, nothing.’ Further reasons for this denial are given in other remarks in the 5.4s.

If the logical connectives do not denote anything, then what are they? Wittgenstein’s answer is that they are operations, an idea that first emerges in his notebooks on 13 December 1914 and is made explicit on 23 January 1915: ‘Negation is an operation… . An operation, of course, does not state anything, only its result; and this depends on its object’ (NB, p. 39). Wittgenstein’s account of operations is given in the 5.2s. One crucial idea is that an operation can ‘vanish’, as he puts it (5.254); that is, it can cancel itself out, explaining why ‘not-not-p’ is equivalent to just ‘p’.

3.2. Propositional Unity and The Theory of Types

At the time that Wittgenstein was working with Russell in Cambridge, Russell conceived a proposition as some kind of complex entity composed of simpler entities. But this raised the question of the unity of such a proposition. If the only constituents of a proposition are objects, then how do they combine to generate a meaningful proposition? As Wittgenstein was to put it, what makes ‘Socrates is mortal’ meaningful but ‘mortality is Socrates’ meaningless? Frege’s answer was clear: concepts are ‘unsaturated’, reflected in the fact that the concept-word, represented here by ‘( ) is mortal’, contains a ‘gap’ indicating where the proper name goes to complete the sentence.

Wittgenstein recognized what is in effect Frege’s solution to the problem of the unity of the proposition. Reporting on a meeting he had had with Frege in December 1912, he wrote to Russell: ‘I had a long discussion with Frege about our Theory of Symbolism of which, I think, he roughly understood the general outline … The complex-problem is now clearer to me and I hope very much that I may solve it’ (WC, p. 36; NB, p. 121). On 16 January 1913, he then elaborates:

I have changed my views on “atomic” complexes: I now think that Qualities, Relations (like Love) etc. are all copulae! That means I for instance analyse a subject-predicate prop[osition], say, “Socrates is human” into “Socrates” and “Something is human”, (which I think is not complex). The reason for this, is a very fundamental one: I think that there cannot be different Types of things! In other words whatever can be symbolized by a simple proper name must belong to one type. And further: every theory of types must be rendered superfluous by a proper theory of symbolism: For instance if I analyse the prop[osition] Socrates is mortal into Socrates, Mortality and (∃x,y) εI (x,y) I want a theory of types to tell me that “Mortality is Socrates” is nonsensical, because if I treat “Mortality” as a proper name (as I did) there is nothing to prevent me to make the substitution the wrong way round. But if I analyse [it] (as I do now) into Socrates and (∃x)x is mortal or generally into x and (∃x)ϕ(x) it becomes impossible to substitute the wrong way round, because the two symbols are now of a different kind themselves. What I am most certain of is not however the correctness of my present way of analysis, but of the fact that all theory of types must be done away with by a theory of symbolism showing that what seem to be different kinds of things are symbolized by different kinds of symbols which cannot possibly be substituted in one another’s places. I hope I have made this fairly clear! (WC, p. 38; NB, pp. 121–2)

Wittgenstein’s ‘analysis’ here differs from Frege’s, and he did indeed come to reject this analysis as well as Frege’s; in the Tractatus, he writes that elementary propositions ‘consist of names in immediate combination’ (4.221). But the essential idea that there are different ‘kinds’ of things that can only be shown by a correct symbolism remained. The distinction between saying and showing is not formulated explicitly until April 1914 (see §2.1 above, and below), but it is clearly implicit in this letter. It provides a good example of a line of thinking found in Frege’s philosophy, which Wittgenstein then used in clarifying a problem in Russell’s work—and in this case, too, as we have seen, in Frege’s own attempts to explain his concept/object distinction.14

3.3. Showing and The Ideal Notation

As mentioned in §2.1 above, the distinction between saying and showing first appears in the notes Wittgenstein dictated to Moore in April 1914. Here is the opening remark again together with the remark that follows:

Logical so-called propositions show [the] logical properties of language and therefore of [the] Universe, but say nothing.

This means that by merely looking at them you can see these properties; whereas, in a proposition proper, you cannot see what is true by looking at it. (NB, p. 108)

What logical propositions show are the inferential relations between ordinary propositions, rooted in their own logical structure. He goes on to describe logical propositions as tautologies: while true, they say nothing. One might object, however, to the suggestion that we can see that they are true merely by looking at them; but here is where Wittgenstein’s concern with an ideal notation comes in. The diagrams that appear in 6.1203 of the Tractatus are attempts he made to show the logical properties of propositions with what he called his ab-notation (cf. NB, p. 115). More successful, though, is his use of truth-tables to display the truth-conditions of propositions, as explained in 4.31 and 4.4s (and cf. 5.101). This is now an established method in propositional logic of seeing whether a proposition is a tautology. If the final column in a truth-table representing a proposition displays only Ts, then the proposition is a tautology (and if only Fs, then a contradiction).

It is important at this point to correct a major error of interpretation that Russell made in his introduction to Wittgenstein’s Tractatus. In explaining how to understand the book, he writes:

Mr Wittgenstein is concerned with the conditions for a logically perfect language—not that any language is logically perfect, or that we believe ourselves capable, here and now, of constructing a logically perfect language, but that the whole function of language is to have meaning, and it only fulfils this function in proportion as it approaches to the ideal language which we postulate.15

This misconstrues Wittgenstein’s project, and it is not surprising that Wittgenstein was unhappy with Russell’s introduction. Wittgenstein was not concerned with an ideal language, but with an ideal notation, a notation that made as clear as possible the logical structure that our ordinary language already has, as a condition of being useable—of making sense—at all. In 5.5563 he writes: ‘All propositions of our everyday language are in fact, just as they are, in perfect logical order.—That most simple idea that we have to get across here is not a likeness of the truth but the whole truth itself.’ Language may disguise thought (4.002), but that is why we need a ‘critique of language’ to uncover the real logical form of propositions (4.0031). Wittgenstein’s main concern was to show how language is already logically perfect, which he thought we would see once we properly understand the nature of the proposition.

3.4. The Senselessness of Logical Propositions

After characterizing logical propositions in his notes of April 1914 as tautologies, Wittgenstein goes on to describe them as ‘forms of proof: they show that one or more propositions follow from one (or more)’ (NB, p. 109). Since this remark is incorporated into 6.1264 and holds the key to his claim that logical propositions are senseless, let us consider a simple example of a logical inference to explain this, a logical inference of the following form:

‘Q’ follows logically from ‘P’ and ‘P ⊃ Q’ (‘If P, then Q’), by a rule of inference called modus ponens, one of the most basic rules of logical inference.

Let us now imagine someone objecting that since the inference depends on this rule, it should be made explicit in the reasoning. How might we formulate this rule? The obvious suggestion would be to write ‘(P andandandand (P ⊃ Q)) ⊃ Q’ (‘If P and (if P, then Q), then Q’). So let us add this as the supposed missing premise:

If we do this, however, then an infinite regress threatens. Should we not add as a further premise ‘(P andandandand (P ⊃ Q ) andandandand ((P andandandand (P ⊃ Q )) ⊃ Q)) ⊃ Q’, and so on? How can we ever draw a valid inference?16

Clearly, something has gone wrong. ‘(P andandandand (P ⊃ Q)) ⊃ Q’ cannot be seen as a missing premise: the inference in its original formulation is perfectly valid as it is. The rule of modus ponens governs the inference but is not part of it. For every rule of inference there is some corresponding logical proposition, but such logical propositions have a quite different status to the premises of an inference. (If an argument is compared to a house built of bricks, then rules of inference are like the mortar that holds the bricks together and not the bricks themselves.)

Wittgenstein makes this point by saying that logical propositions lack sense, in other words, are senseless. Unlike ‘P’, ‘P ⊃ Q’ and ‘Q’, all of which may have sense, ‘(P andandandand (P ⊃ Q)) ⊃ Q’ is senseless, since it adds nothing to the inference. It is a form of proof, as Wittgenstein puts it, showing that ‘Q’ follows from ‘P’ and ‘P ⊃ Q’. But in showing such inferential relations, logical propositions are not nonsense. The distinction between senselessness and nonsense was only explicitly drawn in the first draft of the Tractatus (see below), but it is just another way of characterizing the unique nature of logical propositions.

3.5. Simple Objects

In the 2.02s of the Tractatus Wittgenstein notoriously claims that there must be simple objects if the world is to have substance and empirical propositions are to have sense. This is an issue that occupied Wittgenstein throughout his early thinking. If a proposition is to have an analysis at all, it would seem, then it must bottom out into basic (elementary) propositions, whose constituent names must denote something if they and the complex propositions they form are to have a meaning. A number of different lines of thought converge on the claim that there must be simple objects, and we will focus here on just one, which is easy to motivate from Russell’s theory of descriptions.

Suppose we have a basic proposition, which has the simplest form we can imagine, stating that an object a has the property F. The proposition ‘a is F’ is true if a does indeed have the property F, and is false if it does not. But what if ‘a’ does not, in fact, denote anything in the world (like ‘the present King of France’)? Then either ‘a is F’ is meaningless or else we have to treat ‘a’ as a disguised definite description and analyse it in accordance with Russell’s theory of descriptions, which would mean that it is not, after all, a basic proposition: it would be a conjunction of three simpler propositions. So if basic propositions are possible at all, propositions that cannot be further analysed, then their constituent names must denote: there must be simple objects to be the meanings of these names. Wittgenstein never gave examples of simple objects, but he thought that there must be such objects as the necessary condition for the propositions of our language to have sense. Other lines of thought leading to this claim are given in the 3.2s, quite a few of which have sources in his notebooks.

3.6. The Bipolarity of Propositions

Another idea that Wittgenstein had very early on, explained in his notes of 1913, concerns what he called the bipolarity of propositions: to understand a proposition we must know both what is the case if it is true and what is the case if it is false (NB, pp. 94, 98). This was important in his attempt to get clear about ‘the mystery of negation’, as he put it: ‘This is not how things are, and yet we can say how things are not’ (NB, p. 30). Russell had held that there had to be both ‘positive’ and ‘negative’ facts, to act as the meanings of propositions and their negations, respectively. But this leaves unexplained the connection that there must nevertheless be between the corresponding positive and negative fact. In his notes of 1913, Wittgenstein suggests a better account: p and not-p have the same meaning (explaining the connection) but opposite sense (NB, pp. 95, 103).

By the time we get to the Tractatus, this claim has been modified: ‘The propositions “p” and “~p” have opposite sense, but one and the same reality corresponds to them’ (4.0621). Wittgenstein no longer talks of propositions having meaning; it is only names that have meaning. But propositions (unlike names) do have sense, which he characterizes as the state of affairs represented. If that state of affairs obtains, then the proposition is true; if it does not obtain, then the proposition is false. Here what is important is that we can understand a proposition, i.e. grasp its sense, without knowing its truth-value: it is ‘reality’ that determines whether it is true or false (4.02 ff.).

This account also helps explain how the logical connectives ‘enter into’ propositions without being actual constituents. For if understanding a proposition requires knowing what is the case if it is false as well as what is the case if it is true, then understanding it involves grasp of negation. Given the interdefinability of the logical connectives, knowledge of all of them—and of logic, generally—is implicitly presupposed in understanding even the most basic propositions.

3.7. Propositions as Facts

According to Russell, propositions signify facts, but propositions themselves he regarded as complexes (of objects or terms). But how can complexes signify facts, which seem to be a different category—or ‘type’—of thing? Wittgenstein’s answer is that propositions, too, are facts. In his notes of 1913, he writes: ‘In “aRb” it is not the complex that symbolises but the fact that the symbol “a” stands in a certain relation to the symbol “b.” Thus facts are symbolised by facts, or more correctly: that a certain thing is the case in the symbol says that a certain thing is the case in the world’ (NB, p. 96; cf. pp. 98, 106). This is repeated in his notes of 1914 (pp. 111, 116), and later in 3.1432 of the Tractatus: ‘Not: “The complex sign ‘aRb’ says that a stands in the relation R to b”; but: That ‘a’ stands in a certain relation to ‘b’ says that aRb.’ Here it is embedded in a broader discussion of propositions as facts, which we can thus see as elaborating on his criticism of Russell’s view.

3.8. Propositions as Pictures

On 29 September 1914 Wittgenstein wrote in his notebook:

The general concept of a proposition carries with it a quite general concept of the co-ordination of proposition and state-of-things [Sachverhalt]: The solution to all my questions must be extremely simple.

In a proposition a world is put together experimentally. (As when in a Parisian law-court a car accident is represented by dolls, etc.) (NB, p. 7)

Wittgenstein had apparently read about the Parisian law case in a magazine report and it was the trigger for him to think of propositions themselves as models or pictures of the world. We can see his thinking this through in the remarks that follow:

Instead of saying that this proposition has such and such a sense, one can say that this proposition represents such and such a state-of-things!

It depicts it logically.

Only thus can the proposition be true or false: only by being a picture of a state-of-things can it agree or not agree with reality… .

The proposition only states something insofar as it is a picture!   (NB, p. 8)

In the first three sentences Wittgenstein is already connecting the idea of propositions as pictures to his existing conception of a proposition as having sense (representing a state-of-things) and meaning (depicting reality), and agreeing or not agreeing with reality (just as a picture can). The fourth expresses the insight he has now had.

It was not all plain sailing, however. He continues to develop what is essentially an analogy, with all the ups and downs that would inevitably be expected. In mid-April 1915, for example, he writes:

I cannot get from the essence of a proposition to the individual logical operations!!!

I just cannot bring out how far a proposition is a picture of a state-of-things!

I am almost ready to give up all efforts.   (NB, p. 41)

On 9 May he has the hint of an answer: ‘A proposition is a picture of a fact. I can devise different pictures of a fact. (The logical operations serve to do this.)’ (NB, p. 46). Four days later he writes: ‘Just don’t pull the knot tight before being sure of having got hold of the right end’ (NB, p. 47). Whatever the exact development of his thinking in this period, though, it does seem that he was gradually getting closer to the point when he could finally start pulling the knot tight in bringing together all his ideas.
