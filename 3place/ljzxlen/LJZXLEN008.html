Note on the Text

  1. Origin and composition

The composition, publication, and English translation of the text of Wittgenstein’s Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus has a complex history that deserves a separate account of its own.1 Its origin lies in the notebooks in which Wittgenstein wrote down his thoughts from the time he began working with Russell at Cambridge in 1912. Before he departed for Norway in October 1913 he left some notes for Bertrand Russell, extracted from these notebooks, which were posthumously published as Appendix 1 of Notebooks 1914–1916.2 In Spring 1914 G. E. Moore visited him in Norway, where Wittgenstein dictated some further notes, which now constitute Appendix 2 of the Notebooks. Wittgenstein served in the Austrian army during the First World War from August 1914 until he was captured on the Italian Front in November 1918, after which he spent ten months in prisoner-of-war camps in Italy. Throughout the war he continued to write down his thoughts in notebooks, some of which have survived and been translated (by Elizabeth Anscombe) and published as the main text of the Notebooks.3

Wittgenstein had various periods away from the warfront, either on leave or working as a military engineer, and during these periods he composed what eventually became the Tractatus. He began this sometime in the summer of 1915 when he started a new notebook, which has also survived and is catalogued as MS 104 in the Wittgenstein archives. On the first page he wrote down what became the first six main propositions of the Tractatus, with the formula in the sixth added later, together with 1.1, 2.1, 2.2, 3.1, 3.2, 4.1, 4.2, 4.3, and 4.4. It is at this point that he introduced his numbering system, according to which a remark of the form n.1 is a comment on n, n.11 on n.1, and so on. This system gave him an ingenious method to compose his treatise and not merely to indicate the ‘logical weight’ of the propositions, as he puts it in his footnote to the first proposition of the Tractatus. He could add new remarks consecutively in the notebook but by numbering them he could indicate their place in the eventual treatise. Some remarks were indeed new but many were selected from his other notebooks, revised as appropriate. When the notebook (MS 104) was discovered in Vienna in 1965 by G. H. von Wright, it was edited, translated, and published as the Prototractatus in 1971,4 with the remarks arranged in the order indicated by their decimal numbering. It is revealing to see this earlier version of the Tractatus, but its arrangement obscures the chronological order in which the remarks were written or added, which is also helpful for scholars. The original notebook is now available online, however, with impressive editing by Martin Pilch and others, which scholars can consult for a more detailed understanding of the composition of the Tractatus.5

Wittgenstein had a longer leave than normal in the summer of 1918, and this is when the Tractatus took its final form, under the title ‘Logisch-Philosophische Abhandlung’ (‘Logico-Philosophical Treatise’). There are significant changes from the Prototractatus, as later constructed, to the Tractatus itself, but the critical edition of the Tractatus that was published in German in 1989 (LPA) prints each page of the Tractatus with the corresponding remarks of the Prototractatus (and remarks from other notebooks) on the facing page, so that comparison can be made. The Tractatus was essentially finished in August 1918. Several typed copies were made, one of which was eventually sent by his sister to Gottlob Frege (arriving in early 1919) and another of which Wittgenstein took with him to the Italian Front in October 1918. After capture, Wittgenstein continued to make minor revisions while in prisoner-of-war camps, and in March 1919 he wrote to Russell telling him about the book and claiming to have ‘solved our problems finally. This may sound arrogant but I can’t help believing it’ (WC, p. 89). He eventually managed to get his corrected version sent to Russell, in June 1919, but in the meantime he received a copy of Russell’s Introduction to Mathematical Philosophy, which Russell had written while in prison in 1918 and which had been published in March 1919. Wittgenstein was disappointed to find that it showed no understanding at all, as he saw it, of his notes from 1913 and 1914, and he wrote back expressing his hope of meeting Russell in person to explain his ideas (WC, p. 93). This only became possible once he was freed from the prisoner-of-war camp in August 1919.

  1. First attempts at finding a publisher

Wittgenstein had first tried to find a publisher as soon as his book was finished in August 1918. He sent it to Jahoda andandandand Siegel, the publisher of Karl Kraus’s work, but heard back by the end of October that it had been rejected. On his return from Italy, he sent it to Wilhelm Braumüller, the publisher of Otto Weininger’s work, and asked Russell to send a letter of recommendation in support, which Russell did. But Braumüller made publication conditional on payment for the paper and printing, a condition which Wittgenstein refused to accept. Wittgenstein had been in correspondence with Frege throughout the war, and although Frege had professed not to understand Wittgenstein’s treatise when he tried to read it, he nevertheless offered (in a letter dated 30 September 1919) to help find a publisher.6 What Frege had in mind was the publisher of the Beiträge zur Philosophie des deutschen Idealismus (edited by his Jena colleague Bruno Bauch), in which Frege’s own paper ‘Der Gedanke’ (1918) had just appeared. But nothing came of this, either, not least because Frege had recommended that the whole treatise be revised to make the arguments clearer, something which Wittgenstein was also unwilling to do.

In October 1919 he then approached Ludwig von Ficker, whom he had got to know in Vienna before the war in soliciting his help in giving away some of his inheritance. Ficker was editor of Der Brenner, a literary periodical, and was in contact with writers and artists such as Rainer Maria Rilke, Oskar Kokoschka, and Georg Trakl, who then benefited from Wittgenstein’s largesse. In his initial letter offering his manuscript, Wittgenstein said only that ‘the work is strictly philosophical and at the same time literary, but there’s no gassing in it’.7 After a positive response expressing interest, Wittgenstein sent the manuscript with an accompanying letter containing a passage that is now frequently quoted, revealing as it is of the ‘ethical’ dimension of the book:

it will probably be a help to you if I write a few words about my book. You see, I am quite sure that you won’t get all that much out of reading it. Because you won’t understand it; its subject matter will seem quite alien to you. But it isn’t really alien to you, because the book’s point is an ethical one. I once meant to include in the preface a sentence which is not in fact there now but which I will write out for you here, because it will perhaps be a key to the work for you. What I meant to write, then, was this: My work consists of two parts: the one presented here plus all that I have not written. And it is precisely this second part that is the important one. My book draws limits to the sphere of the ethical from the inside as it were, and I am convinced that this is the ONLY rigorous way of drawing those limits. In short, I believe that where many others today are just gassing, I have managed in my book to put everything firmly into place by being silent about it. And for that reason, unless I am very much mistaken, the book will say a great deal that you yourself want to say. Only perhaps you won’t see that it is said in the book. For now, I would recommend you to read the preface and the conclusion, because they contain the most direct expression of the point of the book.8

Ficker, too, however, soon got cold feet (perhaps because of these words), although he offered to publish it as a special favour if no other option availed itself. This was also something that Wittgenstein was not prepared to accept, of course. Ficker said he would ask Rilke if he could help find another publisher, but nothing came of this, either. Wittgenstein realized that his best chance lay in convincing Russell of the value of his work, and he pressed ahead with his arrangements to travel to The Hague, where he and Russell had agreed to meet for a week in the middle of December.

  1. Meeting with Russell in December 1919

In the lead-up to their meeting in the Netherlands, Wittgenstein and Russell had exchanged a series of letters. On 13 August 1919 Russell had written: ‘I am sure you are right in thinking the book of first-class importance. But in places it is obscure through brevity’ (WC, p. 96). He raised a number of specific questions, some of which Wittgenstein briefly answered in his reply of 19 August. But in responding to the general charge of obscurity, Wittgenstein wrote:

some of your questions want a very lengthy answer and you know how difficult it is for me to write on logic. That’s also the reason why my book is so short, and consequently so obscure. But that I can’t help.—Now I’m afraid you haven’t really got hold of my main contention, to which the whole business of logical prop[osition]s is only a corollary. The main point is the theory of what can be expressed (gesagt) by prop[osition]s—i.e. by language—(and, which comes to the same, what can be thought) and what can not be expressed by prop[osition]s, but only shown (gezeigt); which, I believe, is the cardinal problem of philosophy.

I also sent my M.S. to Frege. He wrote to me a week ago and I gather that he doesn’t understand a word of it all. So my only hope is to see you soon and explain all to you, for it is VERY hard not to be understood by a single soul! (WC, p. 98)

This offers an alternative formulation of the point he had made to Ficker about drawing the limits of thought and ethics from the inside, expressed in terms of his distinction between saying and showing, and making explicit that he regards this as the cardinal problem of philosophy. It also reveals the despair that he was clearly feeling as a result of both Russell’s and Frege’s failure to appreciate his book, the primary aim of which had been to solve the problems that he had seen as arising in their own work.

When Russell and Wittgenstein finally met, for the first time in over six years, they spent a whole week together. As Russell reported afterwards, in one of his regular letters to Lady Ottoline Morrell on 20 December:

we discussed his book every day. I came to think even better of it than I had done; I feel sure it is a really great book, though I do not feel sure it is right. I told him I could not refute it, and that I was sure it was either all right or all wrong, which I considered the mark of a good book; but it would take me years to decide which. This of course didn’t satisfy him, but I couldn’t say more.

I had felt in his book a flavour of mysticism, but was astonished when I found that he has become a complete mystic. He reads people like Kierkegaard and Angelus Silesius, he seriously contemplates becoming a monk… . He has penetrated deep into mystical ways of thought and feeling, but I think (though he wouldn’t agree) that what he likes best in mysticism is its power to make him stop thinking. I don’t much think he will really become a monk—it is an idea, not an intention. His intention is to be a teacher.9

On a more practical level, Russell promised to do what he could to get the book published, agreeing to write an introduction to it and even, so it seemed, to translate it into English himself, so that it could be published in Britain. Wittgenstein returned to Vienna in good spirits. As he wrote to Russell on 8 January 1920, ‘I enjoyed our time together very much and I have the feeling (haven’t you too?) that we did a great deal of real work during that week’ (WC, p. 113).

  1. Further attempts—and final success—at finding a publisher

Back in Vienna, Wittgenstein learnt of a new publisher interested in his book: Reclam in Leipzig, after contact had been made with them by his friend, Paul Engelmann. Wittgenstein was hopeful that, with an introduction by Russell, they would accept it. He wrote to Russell on 19 January 1920 to ask for the introduction, and chased it up on 19 March, but that was the very day that Russell sent it, the delay caused by a broken collarbone (WC, pp. 116–17). Wittgenstein replied on 9 April to thank him, adding that it was now being translated into German to accompany his book. At the same time, though, he couldn’t help giving expression to a certain disappointment:

There’s so much of it that I’m not quite in agreement with—both where you’re critical of me and also where you’re simply trying to elucidate my point of view. But that doesn’t matter. The future will pass judgment on us—or perhaps it won’t, and if it is silent that will be a judgment too. (WC, p. 118)

When the translation into German came back, however, the disappointment deepened, as he felt obliged to explain to Russell on 6 May:

now you’ll be angry with me when I tell you something: Your Introduction is not going to be printed and as a consequence my book probably won’t be either.—You see, when I actually saw the German translation of the Introduction, I couldn’t bring myself to let it be printed with my work. All the refinement of your English style was, obviously, lost in the translation and what remained was superficiality and misunderstanding. Well, I sent the treatise with your Introduction to Reclam and wrote saying that I didn’t want the Introduction printed, it was meant to serve only for his own orientation in relation to my work. It is now highly probable that as a result Reclam won’t accept my work (though I’ve had no answer from him yet). But I’ve already comforted myself on that score, by means of the following argument, which seems to me unanswerable. Either my piece is a work of the highest rank, or it is not a work of the highest rank. In the latter (and more probable) case I myself am in favour of its not being printed. And in the former case it’s a matter of indifference whether it’s printed twenty or a hundred years sooner or later. After all, who asks whether the Critique of Pure Reason, for example, was written in 17x or y. So really in the former case too my treatise wouldn’t need to be printed.—And now, don’t be angry! Perhaps it was ungrateful of me but I couldn’t do anything else. (WC, pp. 119–20)

Wittgenstein correctly anticipated Reclam’s response: the book was rejected. Russell did not receive the letter until he had returned from a trip to Russia. With so much correspondence to catch up on, he wrote back, on 1 July, saying simply, but very generously: ‘I don’t care twopence about the introduction but I shall be really sorry if your book isn’t printed. May I try, in that case, to have it printed in England or America?’ (WC, p. 120). Wittgenstein replied on 7 July telling Russell that he could do what he liked with it, as long as he indicated any changes in the text that he made (WC, p. 121). He had by then received his teaching qualification and was soon to take up a post in an elementary school in Trattenbach, a remote village in the mountains south of Vienna.

Russell, however, was about to leave for China, to teach at Peking University for a year, so he entrusted the job of finding a publisher to Dorothy Wrinch, with whom he had discussed the book the previous year. Cambridge University Press, to their shame, turned it down in January 1921, though this may have been because it had been offered without Russell’s introduction. Wrinch approached the editors of three German journals. One said that they would only accept psychological and not philosophical works, one suggested asking again later that year, and the third was Wilhelm Ostwald, editor of the Annalen der Naturphilosophie, who replied as follows: ’In any other case I should have declined to accept the article. But I have such an extremely high regard for Mr Bertrand Russell, both for his researches and for his personality, that I will gladly publish Mr Wittgenstein’s article … Mr Bertrand Russell’s Introduction will be particularly welcome.’10 So it was that Wittgenstein’s work was finally published, in German, with Russell’s introduction newly translated, in November 1921, in what turned out to be the very last issue of Ostwald’s journal.

Russell didn’t hear about its acceptance by Ostwald until he returned from China at the end of August 1921. Nor did Wittgenstein know anything about it until Russell wrote to him on 5 November, informing him and apologizing for the fact that his introduction was also included. Wittgenstein replied on 28 November, saying: ‘I must admit I am pleased that my stuff is going to be printed. Even though Ostwald is an utter charlatan. As long as he doesn’t tamper with it!’ (WC, p. 128) Russell later assured him that Ostwald’s version was alright, although there were indeed misprints and errors. More importantly, in his letter of 5 November, Russell also told him of the plan to include his book in a new series edited by C. K. Ogden, entitled ‘International Library of Psychology, Philosophy and Scientific Method’, published by Kegan Paul. This news went down much better with Wittgenstein: ‘What pleases me most is that the whole thing is going to appear in England. I hope it may be worth all the trouble that you and others have taken with it’ (WC, p. 128).

There was clearly discussion in Cambridge at this time of how to translate ‘Logisch-philosophische Abhandlung’. Moore had suggested ‘Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus’, an allusion to Spinoza’s Tractatus Theologico-Politicus, prompted by Wittgenstein’s talk of viewing the world ‘sub specie aeterni’ in 6.45. Ogden felt that ‘Philosophical Logic’ was better as a ‘selling title’, and Russell seemed to agree, so this became its working title.11 When Wittgenstein finally gave his opinion on the question (in April 1922), however, he had this to say:

As to the title I think the latin one is better than the present title. For al〈l〉though ‘Tractatus logico-philosophicus’ isn’t ideal still it has something like the right meaning, whereas ‘Philosophical logic’ is wrong. In fact I don’t know what it means! There is no such thing as philosophical logic. (Unless one says that as the whole book is nonsense the title might as well be nonsense too.) (LO, p. 20)

  1. First English translation

The decision was taken at the very beginning to publish the book in both German and English: Russell had discussed this with Wittgenstein when they had met at The Hague in December 1919. They had also agreed, it seems, on the translation of key terms. Russell had left the passages he had cited from Wittgenstein’s treatise untranslated when he had first written his introduction (since the introduction was going to be translated into German), but these were now translated for the version that was going to accompany the book in Ogden’s series. He also made some revisions to the introduction, mainly some additions at the beginning. The published version of the introduction is dated May 1922.

Russell did not undertake the translation himself, however, despite what Wittgenstein had understood from their meeting in The Hague; but nor did Ogden, despite his name being recorded as the translator when the book was published. The first draft of the translation was done by F. P. Ramsey, who was just 18 years old when he began it (he turned 19 in February 1922) and a mathematics student at Trinity College Cambridge. Ramsey had been shown the text and was very interested in it. Ogden presumably advised him on the translation of the key terms that Russell and Wittgenstein had discussed. It also appears that Russell made some changes to the draft translation that Ramsey produced.12 Ramsey worked from an offprint of the Ostwald text, however, with its various errors, so there were further revisions still to be made.

Ogden sent the draft translation to Wittgenstein in March 1922, and he replied on 28 March to say that since he did not have the original corrected German text with him in Trattenbach (the version he had sent Russell, which had then gone to Ostwald), there would be a delay in responding (LO, p. 17). He expressed his strong approval, though, of having the original German included with the English translation. Having received the German text, he then wrote again to Ogden on 23 April with corrections to both the German and the English text, sending some twenty pages of detailed comments on the translation.13 In his covering letter, he remarks:

The translation as you said, was in many points by far too literal. I have very often altered it such that now it doesn’t seem to be a translation of the German at all. I’ve left out some words which occur in the German text or put in others which don’t occur in the original etc. etc. But I al〈l〉ways did it in order to translate the sense (not the words). (LO, p. 19)

He concludes the letter, though, by thanking the translators for their excellent work, noting that ‘I now understand that the translators must have had an awful〈l〉 job’ (LO, p. 21).14

Wittgenstein’s comments about the ‘far too literal’ character of the translation were taken on board by Ogden, and provided a reason for having the original German alongside the English, just as Wittgenstein wanted. As Ogden explained in a note that was inserted at the very beginning of the published book:

In rendering Mr Wittgenstein’s Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus available for English readers, the somewhat unusual course has been adopted of printing the original side by side with the translation. Such a method of presentation seemed desirable both on account of the obvious difficulties raised by the vocabulary and in view of the peculiar literary character of the whole. As a result, a certain latitude has been possible in passages to which objection might otherwise be taken as over-literal.

The proofs of the translation and the version of the original which appeared in the final number of Ostwald’s Annalen der Naturphilosophie (1921) have been very carefully revised by the author himself; and the Editor further desires to express his indebtedness to Mr F. P. Ramsey, of Trinity College, Cambridge, for assistance both with the translation and in the preparation of the book for the press.  (TLP, 1922, p. 5)

The vast majority of the revisions that Wittgenstein requested or suggested in his letter of 23 April were made. In May there was a further exchange of letters between Ogden and Wittgenstein, in which Wittgenstein replied to some specific questions that Ogden asked about the translation. (That Ogden was the driving force in getting the Tractatus published and the contact person in communicating with Wittgenstein may have led him to feel justified in taking credit for the translation. But mention of mere ‘assistance’ in this note belittles Ramsey’s contribution.) Ogden also enquired about some ‘supplements’, to which Wittgenstein responded:

When I had finished the book roughly there remained certain prop[osition]s—about a hundred—about which I was doubtful whether I should take them in or not. These prop[osition]s were—partly—different versions of those now contained in the book; for it had often happened that I had written down a prop[osition] in many different forms, when the same thought had occurred to me in different ways during the long time I worked at that business. Another part of the supplements are merely sketches of prop[osition]s which I thought I might some day take up again if their thoughts should ever revive in me. That means: The supplements are exact〈e〉ly what must not be printed. Besides THEY REAL[L]Y CONTAIN NO ELUCIDATIONS AT ALL, but are still less clear than the rest of my prop[osition]s. As to the shortness of the book I am awfully sorry for it; but what can I do?! If you were to squeeze me out like a lemon you would get nothing more out of me. To let you print the Ergänzungen would be no remedy. It would be just as if you had gone to a joiner and ordered a table and he had made the table too short and now would sell you the shavings and sawdust and other rub[b]ish along with the table to make up for its shortness. (Rather than print the Ergänzungen to make the book fatter leave a dozen white sheets for the reader to swear into[o] when he has purchased the book and can〈n〉’t understand it.) (LO, p. 46)15

This is deeply revealing of Wittgenstein’s method of work and a wonderful illustration of his creative use of analogies in making his points and of his self-deprecation concerning his struggles with writing. It also has a message to any translator: that various formulations may have to be tried out in translating a phrase or sentence before settling on one that best captures the intended sense. And here an objection might be raised to Wittgenstein’s artistic perfectionism, which made him reluctant to publish, and even led him to destroy, what he saw as only rough or draft formulations of his thoughts, such as he wrote in his notebooks. Those earlier or variant formulations can certainly be useful to us in attempting to make sense of his ideas, as long as we recognize, of course, their provisional or discarded status, as ladders to reach the formulations he preferred.

The proofs of his book were sent to Wittgenstein in July 1922, and he returned his corrections to them on 4 August (LO, pp. 57–63). The book was finally published in November, just over four years after he had completed the manuscript, and on 15 November he acknowledged receipt of the copies sent to him, commenting ‘They real[l]y look nice. I wish their contents were half as good as their external ap[p]earance’ (LO, p. 68).

  1. From the first to the second edition

Ramsey’s translation of the Tractatus was of major significance in the lives of both Ramsey and Wittgenstein. Shortly after it was published, Ramsey wrote a critical notice of it for the journal Mind (1923), which is remarkable in the depth of its appreciation of both its general concerns and many of its details, when even Frege, Russell, and Moore had struggled to make sense of it. His work on the translation had surely contributed to his deeper understanding. The review was finished in the spring of 1923, and in September that year, Ramsey visited Wittgenstein in Puchberg, where he was then teaching, and they spent two weeks together discussing the Tractatus every afternoon. In doing so, Wittgenstein made various corrections to both the English and the German texts, which were recorded in Ramsey’s copy of the book.

In a letter to his mother, Ramsey reported that Wittgenstein’s ‘idea of his book is not that anyone by reading it will understand his ideas, but that some day someone will think them out again for himself, and will derive great pleasure from finding in this book their exact expressions’ (LO, p. 78). He also remarked that ‘Some of his sentences are intentionally ambiguous having an ordinary meaning and a more difficult meaning which he also believes’ (LO, p. 78), which Ramsey must have realized complicates translation. Ramsey kept in contact with Wittgenstein by letter, and visited him again several times the following year. There was already discussion then of a second edition of the Tractatus, incorporating the corrections that Wittgenstein had made, though the book was not selling as well as Ogden had hoped. The question came up again of adding further material, but in a letter to Ogden, Ramsey wrote: ‘He almost certainly wouldn’t agree to any more explanatory matter, even Russell’s introduction was a strain. I’m sorry so few have sold’ (LO, p. 86).

It is also clear from a letter he wrote to Wittgenstein on 11 November 1923 that Ramsey had been finding out, on behalf of Wittgenstein, about the possibility of his submitting the Tractatus to obtain a degree from Cambridge. It turned out that he would not be able to fulfil the conditions for a BA but that with another year of residence in Cambridge (added to the two years he had spent in 1912 and 1913), he would qualify for a PhD (WC, p. 143). When Wittgenstein finally returned to Cambridge in January 1929, to take up philosophy again, Ramsey officially became his supervisor and helped him in the formal process of submitting the Tractatus as a PhD. His viva took place on 18 June 1929, with Russell and Moore as his two examiners. In his examiner’s report, Moore concluded: ’I am inclined to think that the dissertation as a whole is a work of genius, comparable with acknowledged philosophical masterpieces; but, even if that is not so, I feel quite sure that its quality is such that it would be a sheer absurdity not to grant the Ph.D. degree to Mr. Wittgenstein.’16 With his doctorate awarded, Wittgenstein was eligible for a Research Fellowship at Trinity College, to which he was appointed at the end of 1930, after submitting the manuscript on which he had been working over the previous two years, which was posthumously published in 1975 as Philosophical Remarks.

A second edition of the Tractatus finally came out in 1933, with most of the corrections that Wittgenstein had made in Ramsey’s copy in 1923 incorporated. The publisher Kegan Paul sent the proofs to Wittgenstein in June 1933, to which he made some further corrections, but without adding any additional material, whether ‘explanatory’ or otherwise.17 The first edition had by then sold out, and the book had established itself as a philosophical classic.

  1. Second English translation

Ogden’s note about the ‘over-literal’ translation in his edition was quoted above. For those readers with some German the English text could aid in its comprehension without being taken as providing definitive or elegant formulations. As Wittgenstein’s influence on the development of analytic philosophy deepened, however, and scholarly interest in his writings blossomed after the Second World War, the need for a fresh and more fluent translation grew. Just such a translation (with an accompanying German text retained) was provided in 1961 by David Pears and Brian McGuinness, two philosophers at Oxford, where Wittgenstein’s influence was perhaps stronger than anywhere else in the world at the time. Here is how McGuinness later described their motivation:

A whole generation of English-speaking philosophers came to know the work through a translation which seems to have been not so much liberated as shackled by the presence of the German on the opposite page. It reads as if made from a dead language, though that may be thought to assist the stylistic effect of a revelation which is certainly one of the aims of the original. A smoother, and perhaps more philosophical version has been attempted by the present writer with a colleague.18

Despite what Ogden had said, in the second paragraph of his note, about the proofs of the original translation being ‘very carefully revised’ by Wittgenstein himself, however, Pears and McGuinness were under the impression that Wittgenstein had not played any major role in this respect. This impression may well have come from Elizabeth Anscombe, who in her introduction to the Tractatus, first published in 1959, had said this: ‘English readers of the Tractatus may need to be warned that Ogden’s translation is notoriously very bad. Wittgenstein told me that he had not checked the whole of this translation, but only answered a few questions that were put to him about some passages’.19 As we have seen, this is not true. But the relevant correspondence had not been published at the time, and the view had certainly formed (at least in Oxford) that the original translation was ‘notoriously very bad’, as Anscombe puts it. Anscombe was one of Wittgenstein’s literary executors and had translated his Philosophical Investigations, which appeared in 1953, two years after his death. She also translated Wittgenstein’s notebooks from the period 1914–16, which were published in the same year as the new translation of the Tractatus.

Pears and McGuinness’s translation was reviewed in the Times Literary Supplement on 19 January 1962, and this is what the reviewer said in talking of ‘the badness of the original translation’:

The difference between the experiences of reading the new and the old versions is remarkable. It is not that the old is grossly inaccurate, though it contains inaccuracies, but that it is throughout cloudy, stilted and unnatural, full of sentences which could not have been written by a native speaker of English composing in his own tongue. Exceptionally the sense of the original escapes altogether; but quite generally the effect is to add to intrinsic difficulty an extra quality of baffling mysteriousness from which the new translation is entirely free… . Mr. Pears and Mr. McGuinness, in their happier collaboration, have not only achieved a clear and natural English but have been meticulous in their care for accuracy.

A heated debate in the pages of the TLS ensued, which simmered for years. Mark Haymon responded first on 9 February 1962 stating that Wittgenstein himself had sanctioned the original translation ‘point by point’: ’Wittgenstein insisted that those places where it is not an exact translation of the German should remain because they expressed what he wished to say better than a more exact translation. It is, of course, open to anybody to make a new translation in a more modern idiom, but it is misleading to suggest that such a translation gives a more accurate rendering of Wittgenstein’s thought.’20

Pears and McGuinness replied on 23 February:

It must be obvious to anyone who knows the two languages concerned that Ogden and Ramsey’s translation contains many mistranslations and infelicities which cannot possibly have expressed Wittgenstein’s thought better than the original. If besides these, there are passages where Wittgenstein composed or retained an English version that represented his own second thoughts, clearly they ought not to be simply inserted in what purports to be a translation. We wished to include in our edition a list of any such passages, but did not succeed in obtaining a list from Mr. Haymon, who was acting for Ogden’s executors. He has now written to complain about our silence.

The underlying motivation for the dispute gradually became clear. In his response on 16 March, Haymon admitted that they had indeed asked for such a list and that this was offered to them but only in return for an explicit acknowledgement of ‘the special nature of the original translation and text and the circumstances of the publication and its title’. Others contributed to the debate. On 13 April Victor Purcell supported Haymon’s position in claiming that ‘Ogden’s translation represents Wittgenstein’s thought at that time more exactly than any new translation of the German can possibly do’. In the interests of both the truth as well as justice to Ogden, he went on, ‘it is surely necessary that any new translation should be accompanied by an account of the circumstances in which the original translation was made’. (The present account does just this, in accompanying the new translation offered here, a century later. It should also be noted, of course, that credit must be given to Ramsey as well, to whose work on the translation, it has to be said, Ogden himself failed to do justice.)

Anscombe, Rhees, and von Wright, Wittgenstein’s literary executors, also entered the fray (in a letter to the Editor of the TLS on 18 February 1965), complaining that they had not been allowed to see Ogden’s letters from Wittgenstein and requesting that they be made available. Eventually this material was authorized by Ogden’s estate for publication, and in a final letter to the TLS on 9 October 1970, Wittgenstein’s three executors conceded the correctness of what Ogden had stated in his editorial note of 1922 and admitted that what they had been led to believe by Wittgenstein in his later life was erroneous. Anscombe revised the footnote, quoted above, in the fourth edition of her book as follows: ’Ogden’s translation of the Tractatus has mistakes which Wittgenstein did not detect. He later said (wrongly) that he did not work on the translation; he only remembered answering a list of questions.’21

Wittgenstein’s Letters to C. K. Ogden with Comments on the English Translation of the Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus, edited by G. H. von Wright, was finally published in 1973, and Pears and McGuinness revised their translation accordingly (as they say in their new preface). The second edition appeared in 1974, this time without the German text but still including Russell’s introduction (with Russell’s own translation of terms and sentences in the Tractatus left unchanged). Other than a brief preface and an index (where the German for key terms was given) no other editorial material was provided.

  1. The present translation

As we have seen, two main translations of the Tractatus have been produced over the last century, the first by Ramsey and Ogden in 1922 (revised in 1933), and the second by Pears and McGuinness in 1961 (revised in 1974). (Scholars have, of course, provided their own translations, in whole or part, in their own work.) Wittgenstein died in 1951, which means that in 2021, seventy years later, the copyright on anything he wrote that was published before 1951 lapsed. Anyone is now free to publish further translations of the Tractatus. The question naturally arises, though, as to the need for, or value of, another translation. The answer to the general question here is that there is indeed value in having more than one translation of any (important) text, even if some of the existing translations are of high quality; and if none are of high quality, then of course there is a need for another. Like works of philosophy themselves, translations are children of their time, and fresh translations may well be required in new contexts, not least when more is known about the texts and the contexts. New translations may offer different perspectives on the text, facilitating deeper understanding; and they may shake up the complacency that soon sets in once an existing translation is taken as standard, complacency which can discourage checking or revisiting the original when interpretive claims are made, and inhibit alternative readings.

Let me now switch to a first-person register in motivating this new translation of the Tractatus—as a reminder that any translation is the work of one or more particular individuals at a particular time and place. I am convinced that there is room for a fresh translation, and I certainly hope that what is offered here will have value both to someone new to Wittgenstein’s work and to scholars of his philosophy. Let me comment first on the two translations whose story has been told above. In my view, Ogden’s claim that Wittgenstein himself ‘very carefully revised’ the proofs of the original translation should not be taken at face value, despite the error that Anscombe and others clearly made in denying it altogether. Having gone through, very carefully, all of Wittgenstein’s correspondence to Ogden and his detailed comments on the translation, I regard it as an exaggeration to say that it was ‘sanctioned point by point’ by Wittgenstein, as Russell (and Haymon) claimed. Wittgenstein indicates where he finds the translation wrong, but he is more hesitant when it comes to suggesting improvements, recognizing the limitations of his own English. He was fluent in English but not a native speaker, and it is native-speaker skills that one needs in refining and polishing a translation. He recognized that the translation was ‘in many points by far too literal’, as he told Ogden very explicitly, which is why he wanted the German text included. As Ogden’s note shows, including the German text provided an excuse for leaving the translation in its clunky form.

Saying that Wittgenstein ‘authorized’ the translation is too strong; he ‘allowed’ or ‘accepted’ it, but did not truly ‘endorse’ or ‘sanction’ it. There are many places where the translation lacks fluency, and it requires a native speaker to identify them all (as opposed to just having an impression of its being ‘far too literal’).22 Pears and McGuinness rightly recognized its ‘stilted and unnatural’ character, as McGuinness put it (quoted above), and with the greater sensitivity to the nuances of language that Oxford ‘ordinary language philosophy’ encouraged in the period after the Second World War, their own translation generally reads very well. It sets a high bar for other translations, but it is not an insuperable bar because it is possible to learn from, and hence build on, their work. In my view, in their concern for fluency, in reacting to Ramsey’s translation, they take liberties with the German text in many places, and some scholars today prefer to go back and cite the original translation for just this reason. A fine balance must clearly be struck between faithfulness and fluency, and this is what I have tried to achieve, above all, in this new translation.

As in the second edition of Pears and McGuinness’s translation, the original German text is not included here. The German text is now readily available online. However, I have provided both explanatory notes and a glossary, so that the key German terms can always be read back. Unlike both previous translations, however, the introduction by Bertrand Russell is not included here (although one helpful passage is cited in the main Introduction, §4.6). It is wrong about Wittgenstein’s views, or distorts them, in a number of ways, and Wittgenstein himself was never happy with it, right from the beginning. A century later, a fresh contextualization of Wittgenstein’s work is long overdue.

  1. How to read the text

In the light of what has been said about its composition and history, let me conclude by offering some brief advice on how to read the text. The notebook (MS 104), from which the Prototractatus was constructed, opened with the main propositions stated separately, and both these and the general structure of the Tractatus must be kept in mind. The seven main propositions, of the form n, and all propositions of the form n.1, have accordingly been set out in the structure that follows the translation. In one of his letters to Ficker, Wittgenstein wrote: ‘the decimal numbers of my remarks absolutely must be printed alongside them, because they alone make the book perspicuous and clear: without the numbering it would be an incomprehensible jumble’.23 The decimal numbers are, of course, printed in the present translation, but the numbers themselves have also been indented in accord with their decimalization to remind the reader throughout of the relationships between the propositions and their relative importance.

In a letter to Ottoline Morrell written on 28 May 1912 Russell reported on a conversation he had had with Wittgenstein:

I told him he ought not simply to state what he thinks true, but to give arguments for it, but he said arguments spoil its beauty, and that he would feel as if he was dirtying a flower with muddy hands. He does appeal to me—the artist in intellect is so very rare. I told him I hadn’t the heart to say anything against that, and that he had better acquire a slave to state the arguments. I am seriously afraid that no one will see the point of anything he writes, because he won’t recommend it by arguments addressed to a different point of view.24

We have seen how this manifested itself in Wittgenstein’s method of working: remarks were drafted in his notebooks, from which he would select and refine the ones that he felt best expressed his insights, organizing them into sequences and sub-sequences to help convey those insights. The aim was to present his point of view, with aesthetic considerations partly governing its presentation.

This is not to say, however, that there are no arguments in the Tractatus. On the contrary, there is a wealth of arguments that can be discerned or reconstructed from what he says. Some of the slave labour Russell recommended has been done for at least a small selection of Wittgenstein’s key arguments in §3 of the main Introduction. A sketch has also been provided in §4 of the basic lines of thinking in the seven main propositions, as well as in the sequences of the form n.m (and in one case of a sequence of the form n.0m), not just to shed light on these lines of thinking but also to encourage the reader to do the same for every sequence at the lower levels (such as 3.11, 3.12, 3.13, and 3.14). As emphasized in concluding §4, it is essential to recognize the tree structure of the Tractatus and read it accordingly, beginning with the top-level sequence 1–7 and working down to all the lower-level sequences, which can be combined as one works down with a ‘straighter’ reading of the book. Nor does this preclude comparing remarks from different places in the tree structure: other arrangements of Wittgenstein’s remarks might be useful in answering or clarifying particular questions. The key is to keep in mind the tree structure throughout. The arguments can then be discerned and reconstructed.

The Tractatus, then, is not a text that can simply be read from beginning to end, on the assumption that there is a linear argument, or a set of linear arguments and sub-arguments, leading one to a conclusion which is thereby proved. Indeed, part of the point of the penultimate proposition 6.54 is to make us realize this, redirecting us to what has gone before and making us rethink it all. In his preface Wittgenstein wrote: ‘This book will perhaps be understood only by those who have already thought the thoughts expressed in it themselves—or at any rate similar thoughts. It is thus not a textbook.’ If a reader has not had those thoughts, then they will have to be acquired on a first (or subsequent) reading, helped by relevant ‘background’ reading, so that understanding the book develops gradually. Its value can only be seen by working through it. The Tractatus is not a textbook but an exercise book: a text that has to be thought into a book for oneself.
