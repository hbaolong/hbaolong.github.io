Chronology

1889 (26 April) Ludwig Wittgenstein born in Vienna, youngest child of Karl and Leopoldine Wittgenstein.

1902 Disappearance of Wittgenstein’s oldest brother Hans near Chesapeake Bay, presumed suicide.

1903 (2 May) Suicide of Wittgenstein’s third oldest brother Rudolf, Berlin.

Attends Realschule in Linz; leaves 1906.

1905 Reads Schopenhauer, The World as Will and Representation.

1906 Goes to Berlin to study mechanical engineering at the Technische Hochschule for three semesters.

1908 Goes to Manchester to study aeronautical engineering, 1908–11; becomes interested in the foundations of mathematics; reads Russell’s Principles of Mathematics and Frege’s Grundgesetze der Arithmetik.

1911 Visits Frege for the first time, who recommends that he study with Russell.

(18 October) Meets Russell in Cambridge; attends Russell’s lectures.

1912 (February) Enrols as a research student at Trinity College, Cambridge; attends Russell’s lectures and works with him; also attends Johnson’s lectures on logic.

Visits Frege for the second time.

1913 (20 January) Death of Wittgenstein’s father Karl, Vienna. (May) Criticizes Russell’s theory of judgement, leading Russell to abandon work on his Theory of Knowledge.

(October) ‘Notes on Logic’ compiled for Russell (NB, App. 1).

(14 October) Goes to Norway, stays until June 1914, with break at Christmas.

(December) Visits Frege for the third time.

1914 (April) ‘Notes dictated to G. E. Moore in Norway’ (NB, App. 2).

(July) Enlists in Austro-Hungarian army; serves on the Eastern Front (and later on the Italian Front)

(24 September) Comes up with idea of propositions as pictures (NB, p. 7).

1915 (May) Reads Russell’s Our Knowledge of the External World.

(Summer) Begins new notebook, MS 104; writes down first main propositions of the Tractatus on opening page (MS 104, later reconstructed as Prototractatus).

1916 (September) Adds remarks to MS 104, drawn from his notebooks, in Vienna.

1917 (January) Begins new notebook, subsequently lost or destroyed; adds remarks to MS 104 on ethics, and later on scientific laws.

1918 (January–March) Russell gives lectures on ‘The Philosophy of Logical Atomism’ in London; claims to explain ideas learnt from Wittgenstein.

Turns MS 104 (Prototractatus) into Tractatus, adding remarks on mathematics and philosophy; (Summer) completes more or less final version during leave from the war.

(September) Returns to the Italian Front; (November) captured and held in prisoner-of-war camps in Italy until August 1919.

Death of Wittgenstein’s second oldest brother Kurt, shooting himself rather than surrendering.

1919 Frege receives a copy of the Tractatus in early 1919, but writes on 28 June that he finds it hard to understand.

(October) Ficker declines to publish the Tractatus.

Trains as a schoolteacher in Vienna, October 1919–July 1920.

Meets Russell in The Hague, spending a week in December discussing the Tractatus.

1920 (March) Russell writes introduction to Tractatus.

(September) Begins work as a teacher; works in various Austrian villages until April 1926.

Tractatus rejected by Reclam.

1921 (January) Cambridge University Press rejects Tractatus.

Tractatus is published in German as Logisch-Philosophische Abhandlung in Ostwald’s Annalen der Naturphilosophie.

1922 (March) Ramsey finishes first draft of translation of Tractatus; Ogden sends it to Wittgenstein, who corrects it in April and answers further questions in May; returns proofs in August.

(August) Russell visits Wittgenstein in Austria.

(November) Tractatus published, with Russell’s introduction.

1923 (Spring) Ramsey writes a review of the Tractatus (published in October); (September) visits Wittgenstein in Puchberg for two weeks, discussing the Tractatus each day.

1925 (26 July) Death of Frege, Bad Kleinen, Germany.

(August) Visits England.

1926–8  Joins Engelmann in designing and supervising the building of a house for his sister Margarete in Vienna.

1927 Meets Schlick, and begins discussions with members of the Vienna Circle.

1929 (January) Returns to Cambridge.(18 June) Awarded PhD for the Tractatus, examined by Moore and Russell; receives grant from Trinity College to pursue research.

Writes ‘Some Remarks on Logical Form’.

1930 (19 January) Death of Ramsey, Guy’s Hospital, London.

Completes Philosophical Remarks (published 1964), on the basis of which he is awarded a five-year Fellowship from Trinity College in December.

1933 Completes Philosophical Grammar (published 1975).

Blue Book dictated 1933–4 (published 1958).

1934 Brown Book dictated 1934–5 (published 1958).

1939 Succeeds Moore as Professor of Philosophy at Cambridge (1939–47).

(June) Gains British citizenship.

1941 Works as a porter in Guy’s Hospital, London.

1942 Works in a medical research unit in Newcastle upon Tyne, investigating ‘wound shock’.

1944 Returns to Cambridge, and then goes to Swansea, to work on his Philosophical Investigations.

1946 Completes final version of Philosophical Investigations, Part I.

1947 Resigns his Chair at Cambridge; spends time in Ireland.

1949 Visits Malcolm in Ithaca, New York; (November) returns to Cambridge and is diagnosed with cancer.

1950 Lives in Oxford at Anscombe’s house, before returning to Cambridge.

1951 (29 April) Dies in Cambridge.

1953 Philosophical Investigations published, translated by Anscombe.

1956 Remarks on the Foundations of Mathematics published (written 1937–44).

1961 Notebooks 1914–1916 published, translated by Anscombe.

Tractatus retranslated by Pears and McGuinness; dispute in Times Literary Supplement.

1969 On Certainty published (written 1949–51).

1970 (2 February) Death of Russell, Penrhyndeudraeth, Wales.

1971 Prototractatus published.

1973 Letters to C. K. Ogden published.

1979 Ludwig Wittgenstein and the Vienna Circle published.

1989 Frege’s letters to Wittgenstein published.

2015 Bergen Nachlass edition published online (www.wittgensteinsource.org).

For a chronology of the history of analytic philosophy more generally, see Michael Beaney, ‘Chronology of Analytic Philosophy and its Historiography’, chapter 3 of The Oxford Handbook of the History of Analytic Philosophy (Oxford: Oxford University Press, 2013).
