4. The main structure of the Tractatus

Sometime in the summer of 1915 Wittgenstein began writing a new notebook (MS 104) which marked a watershed in the evolution of the Tractatus. Entitling it ‘Logisch-philosophische Abhandlung’ (‘Logical-philosophical Treatise’), he here formulated, right at the beginning, what were to become the first six main propositions of the Tractatus, together with 1.1, 2.1, 2.2, 3.1, 3.2, 4.1, 4.2, 4.3, and 4.4 (with slight modifications to some of them). A space was left in the sixth for the intended formula, which was added later.17 The seventh and final proposition also came later, added on page 71. Wittgenstein’s thoughts had clearly reached a point when he needed to take stock and find a way to organize and develop them into a coherent whole. The propositions he wrote down and the numbering system he invented provided a brilliant way to do so. The main propositions formed the core of his treatise and the decimal notation allowed him to develop it by adding further remarks in a tree structure, some culled from his other notebooks, revised as appropriate, and some new.

One can thus see how he insisted, in a letter written to the publisher Ludwig von Ficker in December 1919 when he was trying to get the Tractatus published (see Note on the Text, §2), that ‘the decimal numbers of my remarks absolutely must be printed alongside them, because they alone make the book perspicuous and clear: without the numbering it would be an incomprehensible jumble’.18 Now that the key influences on his thinking have been outlined and a sketch offered of some of his key early ideas, we can see how his ideas were crystallized and elaborated in the Tractatus itself, at least as far as its main structure is concerned.

Let us first consider the main propositions, before looking in turn at the corresponding propositions at the next level down, those of the form n.m. Here are the seven main propositions:

1 The world is everything that is the case.

2 What is the case, a fact, is the obtaining of states-of-things.

3 A logical picture of facts is a thought.

4 A thought is a senseful proposition.

5 A proposition is a truth-function of elementary propositions. (An elementary proposition is a truth-function of itself.)

6 The general form of a truth-function is []. This is the general form of a proposition.

7 Of what one cannot speak, about that one must be silent.

There is a clear line of thought here. Beginning with the claim that the world is everything that is the case, we get clarification of what it is for something to be the case—that is, to be a fact (Tatsache)—namely, for states-of-things (Sachverhalten) to obtain.19 Next comes a statement about the relationship between facts and thoughts: a thought is a logical picture of facts; and a thought is in turn characterized as a senseful proposition. This leads to clarification of what a proposition is: a truth-function of elementary propositions; and we are then told what the general form of a truth-function is, which is in turn characterized as the general propositional form. That is where the statement of the main propositions on the opening page of the notebook (MS 104) ends, but already we see many of the key ideas of the Tractatus reflected here: the conceptions of a fact, a state-of-things, a (logical) picture, a thought, a (senseful) proposition, an elementary proposition, a truth-function, and the general propositional form. Proposition 7 draws a conclusion: that instead of trying to say something that does not instantiate the general propositional form (and fulfil the conditions of being a proposition), we should keep silent. We can see this as indicating the second part of his treatise that Wittgenstein had described to Ficker: the part that he had not written (see Note on the Text, §2).

4.1. The Main Structure of the 1s

The first section of the Tractatus, containing the opening proposition and the 1s that follow, is the shortest in the book. There are just two propositions of the form 1.m (with 1.2 added later):

1 The world is everything that is the case.

1.1 The world is the totality of facts, not of things.

1.2 The world divides into facts.

These elaborate on the opening claim that the world is everything that is the case: the world is made up of facts, not things. This introduces the notion of a fact (Tatsache), which is fundamental to the conception of a picture that is explained in the 2s.

4.2. The Main Structure of the 2s

The second section of the Tractatus, comprising the 2s, is much longer than the first section (though still shorter than the remaining sections), but there are also only two propositions of the form n.m:

2 What is the case, a fact, is the obtaining of states-of-things. 2.1 We picture facts.

2.2 A picture has the logical form of depiction in common with what is depicted.

These introduce the idea of a picture and state the basic connection between a picture and a fact, bringing in the key idea of a common logical form in depiction.

These ideas are elaborated in the propositions that comment on 2.1 and 2.2, providing a full account of his conception of a picture. What is missing in 2.1 and 2.2, though, is clarification of the notion of a state-of-things. This is done in a new kind of subdivision, adding propositions of the form 2.0m. These are not, however, remarks on anything numbered 2.0, but are also direct comments on 2. Having formulated 2.1 and 2.2, Wittgenstein presumably realized that there needed to be a further level of immediate clarification of 2, so he inserted propositions between 2 and 2.1. Here are the propositions of the form 2.0m:

2 What is the case, a fact, is the obtaining of states-of-things. 2.01 A state-of-things is a combination of objects (entities, things).

2.02 An object is simple.

2.03 In a state-of-things the objects hang in one another, like the links of a chain.

2.04 The totality of the obtaining states-of-things is the world.

2.05 The totality of the obtaining states-of-things also determines which states-of-things do not obtain.

2.06 The obtaining and non-obtaining of states-of-things is reality.

These summarize Wittgenstein’s conception of a state-of-things, with further elucidation and argument provided in the lower-level propositions (2.011, etc.). With this done, he could then rely on it elaborating his account of pictures in the 2.1s and 2.2s, as in describing them as presenting the obtaining and non-obtaining of states-of-things (2.11, 2.201).

4.3. The Main Structure of the 3s

The third section of the Tractatus, comprising the 3s, is longer than the first two sections combined (though shorter than each of the remaining sections), and contains five propositions of the form 3.m (with 3.3, 3.4, and 3.5 added later):

3 A logical picture of facts is a thought. 3.1 In a proposition a thought is expressed perceptibly.

3.2 In a proposition a thought can be so expressed that to the objects of the thought correspond elements of the propositional sign.

3.3 Only a proposition has sense; only in the context of a proposition does a name have meaning.

3.4 A proposition determines a place in logical space. The existence of this logical place is guaranteed by the existence of the constituents themselves, by the existence of the senseful proposition.

3.5 A propositional sign as applied in thinking is a thought.

We have here an initial clarification of Wittgenstein’s conception of a proposition as expressing a thought. 3.1 and the remarks that follow (of the form 3.1m.) provide an excellent illustration of his method in crafting the Tractatus. 3.1 had originally read: ‘The perceptible expression of a thought is a propositional sign.’ Its final form is sharper, focusing on the key concept of a proposition, with the 3.1s elaborating on it. Two are especially significant. In 3.12 we are told that ‘a proposition is a propositional sign in its projective relation to the world’ (italics added). This makes clear that ‘Satz’, at least in the 3s (and in most other places), should not be translated as ‘sentence’. What would correspond to ‘sentence’ is ‘propositional sign’ (‘Satzzeichen’). A proposition, on Wittgenstein’s view, expresses a thought through a propositional sign. Arguably, though, even ‘Satzzeichen’, as Wittgenstein understands it, does not merely mean ‘sentence’. In 3.14 we read: ‘A propositional sign consists in this, that its elements, the words, stand to one another in it in a determinate way. A propositional sign is a fact.’ This crystallizes one of Wittgenstein’s earliest insights, found in his notes of 1913 (as we saw in §3.7 above) and reiterated in 3.1432: ‘Not: “The complex sign ‘aRb’ says that a stands in the relation R to b”; but: That ‘a’ stands in a certain relation to ‘b’ says that aRb.’ In isolation (as it is in the notes from which it is drawn), this is very cryptic, but we now see that it is an illustration of his conception of propositions as facts. Only facts can represent facts. The obtaining of states-of-things in the world is represented by the obtaining of relations between the elements of a propositional sign as they are projected in thought.

What has just been said is further elaborated in the 3.2s; and 3.3 states what is now known as the context principle, first formulated by Frege in The Foundations of Arithmetic in arguing that the meaning of number terms can only be given by defining the sense of a proposition in which number terms occur.20 Wittgenstein generalizes the principle, taking it to hold of all names, and puts it to different work, not least in diagnosing what goes wrong in Russell’s paradox (briefly stated in the 3.33s). 3.5, concluding the 3s, leads neatly into the 4s.

As in the 2s, we also have propositions of the form n.0m, elucidating his conception of thinking, such as in claiming that we cannot think anything illogical (3.03, defended in the lower-level propositions that follow), as well as a single proposition of the form n.00m, connecting the idea of a state-of-things in the 2s with 3: ‘“A state-of-things is thinkable” means: we can picture it’ (3.001).

4.4. The Main Structure of the 4s

The fourth section of the Tractatus, comprising the 4s, is the second longest section, but with section 3, it forms the philosophical heart of the work. Significantly, in this respect, 4, 4.1, 4.2, 4.3, and 4.4 were all transferred unchanged into the final version of the Tractatus. Only 4.5 was subsequently added. Here they are (with 4.5 drastically shortened):

4 A thought is a senseful proposition. 4.1 A proposition represents the obtaining and non-obtaining of states-of-things.

4.2 The sense of a proposition is its agreement and disagreement with the possibilities of the obtaining and non-obtaining of states-of-things.

4.3 The truth-possibilities of the elementary propositions mean the possibilities of the obtaining and non-obtaining of states-of-things.

4.4 A proposition is the expression of agreement and disagreement with the truth-possibilities of the elementary propositions.

4.5 It now appears possible to specify the most general propositional form … The general form of a proposition is: such and such is the case.

Section 2 had explained the conception of a fact as the obtaining of states-of-things, which can be pictured in thought. Section 3 had elaborated the idea of picturing in elucidating the conception of a proposition as a propositional sign—understood as itself a fact—in its projective relation to the world. Except for the 2.02s (in a different context), however, there is no mention of propositions in section 2, and there is no mention at all of states-of-things in section 3. The connection between them is only made in 4.1: ‘A proposition represents the obtaining and non-obtaining of states-of-things.’ This is undoubtedly one of the key and most distinctive claims of the Tractatus. The claim is anticipated in 2.11: ‘A picture presents a state of affairs in logical space, the obtaining and non-obtaining of states-of-things.’ But it is only once the conceptions of both a picture and a proposition have been clarified, enabling propositions to be seen as a kind of picture, that ‘picture’ in 2.11 can be replaced by ‘proposition’.

4.2 brings in the conception of the sense of a proposition, characterized here as its agreement and disagreement with the possibilities of the obtaining and non-obtaining of states-of-things, and 4.3 introduces two new notions, of truth-possibilities and of elementary propositions, although the latter had been defined (appearing for the first time) in 4.21 (so immediately after 4.2): ‘The simplest proposition, an elementary proposition, asserts the obtaining of a state-of-things.’ This makes the crucial connection between elementary propositions and states-of-things (which can indeed now be seen as ‘atomic’ states of affairs), and leads Wittgenstein to formulate 4.4 as following from (and summarizing) 4.1, 4.2, and 4.3.

In a parenthetical remark in his notebooks dated 3 June 1915, Wittgenstein wrote: ‘The liberating Word [das erlösende Wort], by the way, has not yet been spoken here’ (NB, p. 54; cf. pp. 39, 40). Wittgenstein was concerned throughout his life to find the ‘liberating’ word, which he later characterized as ‘the word that finally permits us to grasp what until then had constantly and intangibly weighed on our consciousness’ (BT, p. 302e). The remark in his notebooks was written just before he came up with his plan for the Tractatus. In starting his new notebook (MS 104) with a formulation of propositions 1, 1.1, 2, 2.1, 2.2, 3, 3.1, 3.2, 4, 4.1, 4.2, 4.3, 4.4, 5, and 6, he seems to have found a constellation of liberating words, a liberating core, around which he could construct his treatise.

4.5 was only formed later, out of several remarks originally numbered as 4.4301, 4.43011, 4.43012, and 4.4303 (as they can be found in the Prototractatus, as later reconstructed by scholars from MS 104). It is one of only two propositions of the form n.m that are amongst the longest in the Tractatus, but draws an important conclusion from the core remarks we have just considered. It also connects back to the very first proposition: ‘The world is everything that is the case.’ Language is now seen as saying what is the case (whether truly or falsely). Wittgenstein insisted that the last sentence in 4.5, ‘Es verhält sich so und so’, be translated ‘Such and such is the case’, not ‘Things are combined in such and such a way’, as Ramsey had first rendered it, commenting that ‘The expression must be one used in every day language to express that something or other is the case’ (LO, p. 30; see the explanatory note to 4.5).

As Wittgenstein says in the second paragraph of 4.5, the most general propositional form must indeed be maximally general, to include all genuine propositions. But if all propositions represent the obtaining and non-obtaining of states-of-things, asserted by elementary propositions, whose truth-possibilities thus determine the truth-possibilities of every proposition, then all propositions do indeed make some claim about how the world is, that such and such is the case. The 4s have provided the philosophical rationale for this, and the 5s will provide a more technical logical account.

As in section 3, we also have propositions both of the form n.0m and of the form n.00m. The 4.00s talk about language in general, ending in the claim that all philosophy is ‘critique of language’, and complimenting Russell for having shown that the apparent logical form of a proposition need not be its real one (4.0031; see §2.2 above). The 4.0s make further remarks about a proposition being a picture or model of reality (4.01), which explains how we can understand a proposition without having its sense explained to us (4.02), how a proposition can convey a new sense to us with old expressions (4.03), and how it can be true or false (4.06), among other things.

4.5. The Main Structure of the 5s

The fifth section of the Tractatus, comprising the 5s, is the longest, supplying the technical apparatus to make more precise Wittgenstein’s claim about the general proposition form and clarifying his conception of a logical operation. Only 5 had appeared in his initial formulation of the main propositions of the Tractatus, and here there is the most change from the version of this section that emerged from his original manuscript (MS 104, reconstructed to form the Prototractatus) to the final version. Here is the final version (with only the first sentence of 5.3 given):

5 A proposition is a truth-function of elementary propositions. (An elementary proposition is a truth-function of itself.) 5.1 The truth-functions can be ordered in series. This is the foundation of probability theory.

5.2 The structures of propositions stand in internal relations to one another.

5.3 All propositions are results of truth-operations on elementary propositions… .

5.4 This shows that there are no ‘logical objects’, no ‘logical constants’ (in Frege’s and Russell’s sense).

5.5 Every truth-function is a result of the successive application of the operation (– – – – – T)(ξ, … .) to elementary propositions. This operation negates each and every proposition in the right-hand brackets and I call it the negation of these propositions.

5.6 The limits of my language mean the limits of my world.

5 is the key claim of section 5, and indeed, one of the key claims of the whole book. Except for a solitary mention in 3.3441, 5 marks the first appearance of the notion of a truth-function, and this is explained in 5.1, drawing out some of its implications, such as for probability theory. A proposition is a truth-function of elementary propositions if its truth-value (truth or falsity) is determined by the truth-values of the elementary propositions. To take one of the simplest examples, the truth-value of the conjunction ‘p andandandand q’ (‘p.q’ in the Russellian notation that Wittgenstein uses) is determined by the truth-values of ‘p’ and ‘q’: it is true if both ‘p’ and ‘q’ are true and false in the other three cases (5.101). We can also say that p (as well as q) follows from p andandandand q (5.12 ff.).

To say that a proposition is a truth-function of other propositions is to say that they stand in internal relations to one another, which can be brought out by seeing the proposition as generated from the other propositions by a logical operation, as explained in the 5.2s. In 5.25 Wittgenstein remarks that operation and function must not be confused, which is itself confusing given his talk of truth-functions. But what he means here is a propositional function, which cannot be its own argument (as he stressed in 3.333 in his diagnosis of Russell’s paradox), while the result of an operation can become its own base (5.251). So truth-functions are functions in a broader sense. Truth-functions of propositions are results of what he calls truth-operations on other propositions, the bases of the operations (5.234). This is summarized in the full version of 5.3, the second of the two propositions of the form n.m that are amongst the longest in the book.

5.4 draws a conclusion that reinforces the ‘fundamental thought’ he had expressed in 4.0312, that the ‘logical constants’ do not represent, that is, are not ‘logical objects’. He offers a number of arguments in what follows. One relies on the interdefinability of the various logical connectives (5.42), which conflicts with the idea that any primitive ideas must be independent of one another (5.451). As he claims at several points in the Tractatus, there are no ‘pre-eminent numbers’ in logic (5.453; cf. 4.128, 5.553). Frege and Russell had taken two logical connectives as primitive in their (propositional) logic: negation and the conditional in Frege’s Begriffsschrift, negation and conjunction in the system of Principia Mathematica. But this is arbitrary. In fact, as Henry Sheffer had shown in 1913, we can actually make do with just one logical connective, symbolized as ‘|’, understood as ‘neither … nor’.21 ‘p|q’ is true if neither ‘p’ nor ‘q’ is true and false in all other cases. All the other logical connectives can be defined in terms of Sheffer’s stroke ‘|’. ‘~p’ (negation) can be defined as ‘p|p’ (neither p nor p), for example, and ‘p andandandand q’ (conjunction) as ‘p|q’ (neither not p nor not q) and hence ‘(p|p)|(q|q)’. But there is nothing uniquely ‘primitive’ about Sheffer’s stroke.

What he says here, however, might seem to conflict with what he goes on to say in 5.5 in introducing his N-operator, symbolized as ‘()’. This generalizes Sheffer’s stroke, extending its use to the infinite case—where there may be an infinite number of elementary propositions (which cannot be ruled out by logic, according to Wittgenstein). If every proposition is a truth-function of elementary propositions, and we can make do with a single logical operator, then every proposition can be generated by successive applications of the N-operator to elementary propositions. But Wittgenstein’s position is not that this is therefore the one primitive logical constant, but that it merely reflects the ideal logical notation for understanding the general propositional form. This is clarified in the 5.5s, when he tries to show (controversially) how general propositions (5.52 ff.), (apparent) identity statements (5.53 ff.), and propositions such as ‘A believes that p’ (5.54 ff.) can be handled in his logical notation.

5.6 returns us to the question of the limits of language, broached in the preface to the Tractatus, cryptically anticipated in the 4.11s, and cryptically answered in 4.51: ‘Suppose that I am given all elementary propositions; then the question arises as to what propositions I can form from them. And the answer is all propositions and this is how they are limited.’ The limits of language and hence of the world are drawn from within language. Those limits are shown by the general propositional form, by all propositions being truth-functions of the elementary propositions.

In the original version of the 5s, there were many propositions of the forms n.00m and n.0m, but all were renumbered and reordered, with the exception of the two that become 5.01 and 5.02.

4.6. The Main Structure of the 6s

The sixth section of the Tractatus, comprising the 6s, is the third longest, and after capturing the general form of a proposition in a single formula, Wittgenstein considers various kinds of propositions that do not fit the formula and offers an account of how they should be regarded.

6 The general form of a truth-function is []. This is the general form of a proposition. 6.1 The propositions of logic are tautologies.

6.2 Mathematics is a logical method. The propositions of mathematics are equations, that is, pseudo-propositions.

6.3 The investigation of logic means the investigation of all regularity. And outside logic all is accident.

6.4 All propositions are of equal value.

6.5 When an answer cannot be stated, neither can the question be stated. There is no such thing as the riddle. If a question can be posed at all, then it can also be answered.

The sixth section of the Tractatus gave Wittgenstein the most trouble and underwent the most changes during its composition. As noted above, the formula in proposition 6 was only filled in later. Initially, it seems, Wittgenstein had been thinking that what eventually became 6.1 would be the main proposition 6. Section 6 was originally conceived as entirely about logic, ending on what is now 6.13 (the last proposition of the 6.1s, combining 6.13 and 6.131 of the Prototractatus): ‘Logic is not a set of teachings [Lehre] but a mirror image of the world. Logic is transcendental.’ The conclusion to the whole book was thus to be the claim that logic is transcendental. (On the idea of the transcendental, see the discussion of Schopenhauer in §2.3 above.22)

In July 1916 Wittgenstein began to think more about ethics, prompted by especially harrowing experiences on the warfront. This led to the decision to add a further subsection consisting of the following remarks numbered 6.2: ‘Ethics does not consist of propositions. All propositions are of equal value.’ These were to become 6.42 and 6.4 of the Tractatus, respectively, with 6.42 derived from 6.4 via 6.41, which suggests that ‘Ethics does not consist of propositions’ was seen as the conclusion to subsection 6.2 corresponding to the conclusion that ‘Logic is transcendental’ at the end of the 6.1s. In due course Wittgenstein also remarks that ‘Ethics is transcendental’, which becomes part of 6.421, making clear that a comparison is being drawn between ethics and logic.

At the same time we have the first appearance of proposition 7: ‘Of what one cannot speak, about that one must be silent.’ We can see this anticipated in remarks Wittgenstein makes in the notebook he began in April 1916 (so not MS 104). In the entry dated 30 July, after discussing the question ‘why should I live happily?’, which revealingly he describes as a ‘tautological question’, he goes on: ‘All this is really in some sense deeply mysterious! It is clear that ethics cannot be stated [sich nicht aussprechen läßt]’ (NB, p. 78). This second remark also ends up as part of 6.421, and it is but a step from this to proposition 7. What all this suggests is that it was his thinking that both ethics and logic are transcendental that led him to formulate what became the overall conclusion to the Tractatus. Proposition 7 was not intended, at least originally, to be a conclusion to anything that is in the 6.5s, as that final subsection had not yet been conceived. (That is not to say that, once the 6.5s had been incorporated, proposition 7 could not function as a conclusion to them as well.)

Throughout this period, Wittgenstein was also continuing to think about the general propositional form, which he later admitted in the Philosophical Investigations was the part of his earlier work that had given him the most headache (PI, §65). In a remark written on 9 July 1916 he makes clear why he thinks that there must be such a form: ‘If the most general propositional form could not be given, then a moment would have to come when we suddenly had a new experience, a logical one, so to speak’ (NB, 75). The problem is still exercising him in November 1916. ‘That it is possible to establish the general propositional form is just to say: every possible form of proposition must be foreseeable’ (NB, p. 89). His thought is very simple: if we have learnt (our) language, then we will understand any possible proposition we may come across in the future, so it must have a general form that enables us to do this. He then goes on to identify the concept ‘and so on’ as the key to establishing this form, a concept he takes as equivalent to the concept of an operation: ‘The concept of an operation is quite generally that according to which signs can be constructed according to a rule’ (NB, p. 90). There is no further clue in the notebook in which this is written as to what exactly the formula is, and the notebook ends soon after (the last entry is dated 10 January 1917, though with no entries made between 2 December and 7 January). But we can see Wittgenstein getting closer to the formula that was eventually added to MS 104, which cannot have happened before December 1916.

Even when Wittgenstein did come up with the formula that found its way into the Tractatus, however, the explanation he gives in 6.001 and 6.002 is inadequate, as Russell recognized when he read it. On this, Russell gives the following helpful account in his introduction to the Tractatus, diplomatically rectifying the explanation:

Mr Wittgenstein’s explanation of his symbolism at this point is not quite fully given in the text. The symbol he uses is

The following is the explanation of this symbol:

 stands for all atomic propositions.

 stands for any set of propositions.

 stands for the negation of all the propositions making up .

The whole symbol [] means whatever can be obtained by taking any selection of atomic propositions, negating them all, then taking any selection of the set of propositions now obtained, together with any of the originals—and so on indefinitely. This is, he says, the general truth-function and also the general form of proposition. What is meant is somewhat less complicated than it sounds. The symbol is intended to describe a process by the help of which, given the atomic propositions, all others can be manufactured. The process depends upon:

(a) Sheffer’s proof that all truth-functions can be obtained out of simultaneous negation, i.e. out of ‘not-p and not-q’;

(b) Mr Wittgenstein’s theory of the derivation of general propositions from conjunctions and disjunctions;

(c) The assertion that a proposition can only occur in another proposition as argument to a truth-function.

Given these three foundations, it follows that all propositions which are not atomic can be derived from such as are, by a uniform process, and it is this process which is indicated by Mr Wittgenstein’s symbol.

Establishing the general form of a proposition was one of the major goals of the Tractatus, but once it had been achieved, it brought about further changes to the structure of the work. The subsection on ethics was moved to begin at 6.4, to make room for two new subsections, one on mathematics beginning with 6.2 and one on scientific laws beginning with 6.3. A final subsection on philosophy was then also added beginning with 6.5. The motivation for these is clear. With the general form of a proposition now established, with all propositions seen as resulting from the successive application of the N-operator to elementary propositions, questions naturally arise about the status of (apparent) propositions that cannot be so generated. These include logical propositions, mathematical propositions, scientific laws, ethical propositions, and philosophical propositions. Wittgenstein devotes a subsection to each of them before concluding with 7.

All these propositions lack sense, according to Wittgenstein, since to have sense just is to have the general form of a proposition. But it would be too quick to dismiss all of them as nonsensical. Logical propositions are characterized as senseless, not nonsensical, since they are tautologies, which say nothing but show the logical properties of language and the world. Mathematical propositions also have a status somewhere between senseful and nonsensical propositions. But he characterizes them not as tautologies but as equations, though they also show the logic of the world (6.21), and not explicitly as nonsensical but as pseudo-propositions, since they express no thoughts (6.2). He presumably stops short of calling them nonsensical because they help us draw inferences outside mathematics (6.211). Wittgenstein is not a logicist (taking mathematical propositions to be logical propositions), but he does describe mathematics as a logical method. Scientific laws and principles also have an intermediate status, many of them being construed by Wittgenstein as offering forms within which to cast empirical propositions (6.32–6.36).

When it comes to ethical and other value propositions (such as in aesthetics), the account is rather different. Value lies outside the world, he claims, and what goes on here is more a matter of the attitude we take to the world. A happy person will experience the world differently from an unhappy person (6.43), and experiencing it as a ‘limited whole’ is the mystical feeling, he writes (6.45). With philosophy, the account is different again. It is only in this final subsection that Wittgenstein uses the term ‘nonsensical’ (‘unsinnig’), once in 6.51 in characterizing scepticism and then, most notoriously of all, in 6.54 in describing the propositions of the Tractatus themselves. Since this is the most controversial issue of all in discussions of the Tractatus, we will devote the whole of §5 of this Introduction to it.

4.7. Proposition 7

We finally come to proposition 7: ‘Of what one cannot speak, about that one must be silent.’23 As noted above, it was first formulated as a conclusion to the dual claims that logic and ethics are transcendental. As such it is impossible to speak of them, according to Wittgenstein, since no propositions about them are possible. But the logical structure of the world is shown by logical propositions, and one’s ethical or religious attitude, we might also suggest (drawing out the implication of what Wittgenstein says), is shown by our behaviour towards others and our feelings about the world. Wittgenstein undoubtedly felt, however, that there was too much ‘gassing’, as he put it in his letter to Ficker, about all this, especially ethics, which is why he wanted to recommend that people just keep quiet about such matters. (‘Schweigen’ can mean not just ‘be silent’ but also ‘shut up’.) His treatise ended up being conceived, at least for such people as Ficker, as providing an argument for this recommendation.

Let us conclude, then, by emphasizing the importance of recognizing the tree structure of the Tractatus. Not only did this structure facilitate the composition of the Tractatus, but it also facilitates our understanding if we follow out each line of thinking along one of the sequential branches, beginning with 1 to 7 and then looking in turn at all the propositions of the form n.m, n.0m, and n.00m, before progressing to the lower-level propositions. Some of these can also be considered separately, if we wish to understand particular propositions on the higher levels (such as in reading 2.11 to 2.19 in understanding his claim in 2.1 that ‘We picture facts’), or we can then proceed to a full reading. As Wittgenstein claimed in his letter to Ficker, the treatise does indeed become perspicuous and clear once we attend to the numbering, and we can thereby build up an understanding of the work, as this section has hopefully shown. The brilliance of the construction of the Tractatus has not been appreciated as much as it should. It is an intellectual creation of the highest order, the tree structure creating the logical space in which his ideas found their expression and meaning. It is the greatest example of a philosophical work of art in the twentieth century, and deserves to be read and understood as such.

  1. A book of nonsense?

The outstanding problem for readers and interpreters of the Tractatus is undoubtedly Wittgenstein’s notorious claim in 6.54 that the propositions in his book are nonsensical. If he is right, then why should we bother to read it? Given his conception of propositions as pictures representing states of affairs and his specification of the general propositional form, his claim seems an unavoidable conclusion, since one is hard pressed to find any of his propositions representing states of affairs. Yet, as we have just seen, a whole range of propositions—in logic, mathematics, science, ethics, and aesthetics—are thereby excluded, and Wittgenstein seems careful not to call them ‘nonsensical’ (‘unsinnig’). In the 6s the only other remark in which the term is used is 6.51, and both 6.51 and 6.54 are comments on 6.5, which opens with the claim that ‘When an answer cannot be stated, neither can the question be stated.’ Wittgenstein goes on to say in 6.51 that ‘Scepticism is not irrefutable, but obviously nonsensical, when it tries to doubt where nothing can be asked.’ Since scepticism is a philosophical position, it is what we say in doing philosophy that Wittgenstein seems to want to claim is nonsensical (cf. 4.003). But if the Tractatus itself is a work of philosophy, then how much of what he says himself is nonsensical?

Over the last twenty years there has been heated debate about how seriously we must take Wittgenstein’s claim in 6.54. A group of ‘new Wittgensteinians’, as they have been called, or ‘resolute readers’, as some have preferred to call themselves, have urged that we must take it very seriously indeed and not ‘chicken out’ in trying to find a way to treat nonsense as illuminating.24 The purpose of the Tractatus, they claim, is ‘therapeutic’: by working through the book, we are brought to recognize how the metaphysical claims we may be inclined to make are illusory. We have already seen examples of such metaphysical claims, such as Frege made in distinguishing between concept and object and Russell made in expounding his theory of types. The Tractatus is indeed concerned with diagnosing what goes wrong here, and it is significant that seven of the fifteen occurrences of ‘nonsensical’ in the book occur in the 4.12s, where Wittgenstein’s diagnosis is offered (see the Glossary to identify all the occurrences).

The resolute readers are quite right to emphasize these and similar passages (such as the 5.473s), which form the core of Wittgenstein’s critique of Frege and Russell, and much light has been shed on the Tractatus by the various readings that have been offered and the responses that they have provoked. But we are on slippery ground, as Wittgenstein’s diagnosis and critique relies on distinctions that he himself draws—most notably, between saying and showing and between real and formal concepts. It is then natural to ask whether his own statements and explanations of these should also count as nonsensical—and in turn, of course, whether this also applies to what the resolute readers say about it all.

Opponents of resolute readings stress that Wittgenstein, both before, in, and after the Tractatus, seems not to have regarded what he was trying to show as entirely illusory, but rather as merely ineffable. In the preface, for example, he states unashamedly that ‘the truth of the thoughts conveyed here is unassailable and definitive’, although he admits how little is achieved once the problems of philosophy have been solved ‘in essence’. He clearly thought that something was shown in his work, and it is the conception of showing that holds the key to understanding what he was doing, difficult as that is to explain.

We cannot do justice to the debate between resolute readers and their critics in this Introduction, let alone settle it. But one further aspect should be mentioned here. The resolute reading was motivated by dissatisfaction with what was called the ‘traditional reading’, according to which Wittgenstein did indeed advance metaphysical claims in the Tractatus, especially in sections 1 and 2 in talking of the world, facts, states-of-things, simple objects, and so on. On the resolute reading, these were only made as part of his therapeutic strategy: by the end we are meant to see them as nonsensical and hence to discard them. But there is an intermediate position here: Wittgenstein did not think of them at the time as metaphysical and hence nonsensical, or at any rate as nonsensical as the claims made by Frege and Russell, but simply as the way to elucidate his dual conception of propositions as pictures and of the general propositional form. They show the form of the world, without which logic and language (as we know it) would not be possible: they are the barest minimum of transcendental conditions. In this respect it is significant that all the propositions in the first two sections (with just a couple of exceptions in the case of the lowest-level propositions) were only formulated by Wittgenstein in the process of coming up with and developing his plan for the Tractatus, in other words, once he finally felt clear about his core conception of propositions as pictures. On 2 August 1916, so shortly after this plan had been hatched, he remarks: ‘My work has expanded from the foundations of logic to the essence of the world’ (NB, p. 78). This may sound metaphysical but the context suggests more of a concern to identify the transcendental conditions of our language and thought—and only through this of the world.

This intermediate position may be unstable, and in retrospect we can certainly see the claims he made as both metaphysical and problematic—as he himself did when he revisited his work later. A remark he makes in the Philosophical Investigations is revealing: ‘The results of philosophy are the discovery of some piece of plain nonsense and the bumps that the understanding has got by running up against the limits of language. They—these bumps—make us see the value of that discovery’ (PI, §119). Wittgenstein had stated explicitly in his preface to the Tractatus that his aim had been to draw a limit to language (and hence to the expression of thoughts) from within language. But the temptation to overstep these limits in doing so is irresistible, and it was perhaps inevitable that he did so himself in the Tractatus. None of this invalidates his project, however, as long as we appreciate what he was trying to do. As he said in 6.54, ‘My propositions elucidate when someone who understands me finally recognizes them as nonsensical by using them to climb up, over, and out of them’ (italics added). In the end, their disputed status as ‘nonsensical’ is far less important than what we can learn from them, and this is dependent on the effort that each of us makes to understand him.

  1. Wittgenstein’s later critique of the Tractatus

After the publication of the Tractatus, Wittgenstein maintained, by and large, his philosophical silence until he was gradually tempted back into philosophy by discussions with members of the Vienna Circle. In 1929 he returned to Cambridge and began re-assessing his old ideas and developing new ones. The story of his later development is long and complex and all that can be done here is to select some of the key ideas and themes and outline what transformations occurred. We shall focus on four moves: from a monistic conception of logic to a pluralist view of what he called language-games; from the acceptance of subsistent simple objects to recognition of the role of samples; from concern with signification to a much greater emphasis on use in understanding meaning; and from preoccupation with the general propositional form to exploring the diversity of particular propositional forms.25

6.1. From Logic to Language-Games

The first problem in his earlier views that Wittgenstein attempted to solve on his return to philosophy concerned the supposed logical independence of elementary propositions (4.211). ‘A is red’, said of a point, might seem to be an obvious example of an elementary proposition, but this contradicts ‘A is green’. Wittgenstein had recognized the problem in his notebooks (NB, pp. 81, 91), but had assumed that physics would provide the analysis to show how it was logically impossible for something to be both red and green (6.3751). In ‘Some Remarks on Logical Form’ (1929), he tried to solve the problem by suggesting that numbers must somehow enter into the logical forms of colour propositions to reflect the degree of quality involved, such as position along a spectrum. But since identifying their logical form would then depend on the results of scientific investigation, this contradicted his fundamental assumption that logic must take care of itself (5.473). He disowned the paper before it was even published (see §1 above) and came to believe, instead, that elementary propositions could be divided into Satzsysteme, or systems of propositions, only one member of which could be true in any given situation. Only propositions from different systems would be logically independent.

What the problem of colour exclusion forced Wittgenstein to recognize was that there are forms of logical inference that cannot be handled within his theory of truth-functions. ‘A is red; therefore A is not green’ is just one such inference. The rules for the logical connectives are thus only part of a much more complex set of rules (cf. WVC, pp. 74, 76; PR, pp. 109, 111). The new project that this then suggests—to provide an account of the logic of the various Satzsysteme—was to develop into the description and elucidation of what he called ‘language-games’ in his later work. There may be language-games for using colour terms, for describing shapes, for counting, for talking about our feelings, and so on. Each language-game would have its own set of rules, which may or may not be formalizable into a logical theory, even if there might be some basic logical rules (such as modus ponens) that are still followed. Rules from different language-games might also interfere with one another, generating further confusions for the philosopher to clear up.

6.2. From Simples to Samples

In thinking further about colour propositions Wittgenstein also explored representing them geometrically—assigning them a position along certain colour axes, as illustrated by the two diagrams below (cf. PR, p. 278; WLL, p. 8).

‘Every statement about colours’, Wittgenstein remarks, ‘can be represented by means of such symbols. If we say that four elementary colours would suffice, I call such symbols of equal status elements of representation. These elements of representation are the “objects”’ (WVC, p. 43). The reference here is to his conception of simple objects in the Tractatus, and within a year he is talking of these ‘elements of representation’ as samples. ‘What I once called “objects”, simples, were simply what I could refer to without running the risk of their possible non-existence; i.e. that for which there is neither existence nor non-existence, and that means: what we can speak about no matter what may be the case’ (PR, p. 72). He goes on: ‘If I want to tell someone what colour some material is to be, I send him a sample, and obviously this sample belongs to language’ (PR, p. 73). A particular set of objects may indeed be presupposed by a given language-game, then, but these objects are not the simples of the Tractatus but the samples that function as the elements of representation, as part of the symbolism itself.

In his later Philosophical Investigations Wittgenstein discusses simples and samples in §§39–64. §50 is the key section. In asking what it is to attribute existence to an element, he writes:

There is one thing of which one can state neither that it is 1 metre long, nor that it is not 1 metre long, and that is the standard metre in Paris.—But this is, of course, not to ascribe any remarkable property to it, but only to mark its peculiar role in the game of measuring with a metre-rule.—Suppose that samples of colour were preserved in Paris like the standard metre. So we explain that “sepia” means the colour of the standard sepia which is kept there hermetically sealed. Then it will make no sense to state of this sample either that it is of this colour or that it is not.

We can put it like this: This sample is an instrument of the language, by means of which we make colour statements. In this game, it is not something that is represented, but is a means of representation… . And to say “If it did not exist, it could have no name” is to say as much and as little as: if this thing did not exist, we could not use it in our language-game.—What looks as if it had to exist is part of the language. It is a paradigm in our game; something with which comparisons are made. And this may be an important observation; but it is none the less an observation about our language-game—our mode of representation.   (PI, §50)

Denying that the standard metre is a metre long might seem absurd, but if measuring something as a metre long requires laying the standard metre alongside it (if this is the language-game we play), then we cannot measure the standard metre itself. According to Wittgenstein, (b) now lacks sense, but this does not mean that the inference becomes invalid, since (b) simply drops out altogether: (c) can be directly inferred from (a). This is also a valid inference, but if we replace ‘Q’ by ‘P ∨ Q’ (‘P or Q’), we can now infer (3) directly from (1): (2) is no longer a required premise, but is simply an expression of the rule licensing the inference (i.e. ‘From “P” infer “P ∨ Q”’). This was, of course, one of Wittgenstein’s key ideas in the Tractatus: logical propositions such as ‘P ⊃ P ∨ Q’ are senseless, merely showing that a certain inference is valid (cf. 6.1201, 6.1221, 6.1264). Although he came to reject the idea that all logical propositions are truth-functional tautologies, he continued to believe that logical propositions—understood as expressions of inferential rules—lacked sense.

6.3. From Signifying to Use

Wittgenstein’s discussion of the standard metre is embedded in a broader critique of what he took to be an underlying assumption of his Tractatus, that ‘a name ought really to signify a simple’, as he puts it in §39 of the Philosophical Investigations. The meaning of a name, he argues, is not the object to which it refers to on a given occasion. That is to mistake its meaning with its bearer: a name does not lose its meaning when its bearer dies (PI, §40). It is in this context that Wittgenstein makes one of his most famous later claims: ‘For a large class of cases of the employment of the word “meaning”—though not for all—this word can be explained in this way: the meaning of a word is its use in the language’ (PI, §43). This is often seen to mark a radical change from his view in the Tractatus, but even there he had stressed that ‘To recognize the symbol by its sign, one must pay attention to the senseful use’ (3.326).

What happens, then, when the samples that are part of the symbolism of a particular language-game are destroyed? The language-game itself changes and may eventually stop being played altogether. Imagine that all the red things in our environment vanish overnight. Initially we may rely on our memory to continue to talk of red things, but over time we will be unable to explain the use of ‘red’ to others, such as those who are born later, and the language-game may wither away (cf. PI, §§56–7). It is tempting to suppose that if we can talk of red things at all, then there must be some universal property (some Platonic form) of redness that ‘exists timelessly’. In resisting the temptation to make such metaphysical statements, Wittgenstein considers treating ‘Red exists’ as saying no more than ‘“Red” has a meaning’ and ‘Red does not exist’ as saying no more than ‘“Red” does not have a meaning’, propositions about our use of language (PI, §58). Commenting on the latter, though, he goes on:

Only we do not want to say that that expression [‘Red does not exist’] says this, but that this is what it would have to be saying if it made sense—that the expression actually contradicts itself in the attempt to say that just because red exists ‘in and of itself’. Whereas the only contradiction lies in something like this: the sentence looks as if it were about the colour, while it is supposed to be saying something about the use of the word “red”.—In reality, however, we quite readily say that a particular colour exists, and that is as much as to say that something exists that has that colour. And the first expression is no less accurate than the second. (PI, §58)

We see here a significant transformation of his approach in the Tractatus. Rather than treating a sentence such as ‘Red exists’ (or ‘Simple objects exist’) as saying something that, strictly speaking, can only be shown, he considers the actual use of such sentences, which may make perfect sense on a given occasion, such as in just meaning ‘Red things exist’. It is the philosopher who gives them a misleading spin or uses them in confused or confusing ways. As Wittgenstein says, ‘What we do is to bring words back from their metaphysical to their everyday use’ (PI, §116). What he had previously assigned to the realm of ‘showing’ he now realizes can be elucidated by carefully describing our various uses of language.

6.4. From the General Propositional form to Particular Propositional Forms

In 4.5 of the Tractatus Wittgenstein characterized the general form of a proposition as: ‘such and such is the case [es verhält sich so und so]’. In §114 of the Investigations he comments directly on this: ‘That is the kind of proposition one repeats to oneself countless times. One thinks that one is tracing nature over and over again, and one is merely tracing round the frame through which we look at it.’ In §115 he goes on: ‘A picture held us captive. And we couldn’t get outside it, for it lay in our language, and language seemed only to repeat it to us inexorably.’ In its place, Wittgenstein urges us to recognize the diversity of propositional forms, or as he prefers to express it, of the different kinds of uses of the things we call ‘signs’ (‘Zeichen’), ‘words’ (‘Worte’), and ‘sentences’ (‘Sätze’, where ‘sentence’ is now the appropriate translation of ‘Satz’). The term ‘language-game’ is introduced in this context, to emphasize that ‘the speaking of language is part of an activity, or of a form of life’ (PI, §23). He gives examples to illustrate the variety of language-games: giving orders, describing an object, reporting an event, making up a story, guessing riddles, translating, and so on (PI, §23).

Here too, though, there are anticipations of the diversity of propositional forms in the 6.3s. Wittgenstein talks in 6.341, for example, of different nets for describing the world. In describing the world, the resulting propositions are presumably all still seen as exemplifying the general propositional form; but on his return to philosophy, he was soon emphasizing that function–argument form, just like subject–predicate form, is only a norm of representation, a mould into which things are squeezed (see e.g. PR, pp. 136–7). Representing all propositions as truth-functions of elementary propositions is itself such a mould, a frame that Wittgenstein had mistakenly taken to be tracing nature itself.

His use of ‘picture’ in §115 is also significant, for it marks a different concern with ‘pictures’ in his later work. On his later view, his conception of propositions as pictures is itself a picture, a way of seeing something that may seem irresistible, that seems to force itself upon us (cf. PI, §140), but which may mislead us in all sorts of ways. What he says in the Investigations about such ‘pictures’ applies to his conception of propositions as pictures: ‘A picture is conjured up which seems to fix the sense unambiguously. The actual use, compared with that traced out by the picture, seems like something muddied’ (PI, §426). At the same time, Wittgenstein takes an interest in the ambiguity of (drawn) pictures such as the duck–rabbit picture (PI, PPF, §xi), which we can see either as a duck or as a rabbit. Yet again, we have an anticipation of this interest in 5.5423 of the Tractatus, but his concern with aspect perception takes on methodological significance, as part of his later philosophical project is aimed at getting us to recognize new aspects of things, or aspects that had become hidden because of their familiarity (PI, §129), in dissolving philosophical problems or puzzlement.

  1. The significance of the Tractatus in the history of philosophy

Wittgenstein is often described as having inaugurated the linguistic turn in philosophy, which began with the Tractatus and continued through his later work. All philosophy, he had declared in 4.0031, is ‘critique of language’. Language disguises thought (4.002) and the aim is to uncover the real logical form of propositions, which may well be much more complex than their surface grammatical form. But as Wittgenstein says in this context, it was Russell’s merit to have shown this, in his theory of descriptions, and we should also see Frege as helping inaugurate the linguistic turn in his analysis of number statements. Wittgenstein’s central role in the linguistic turn is indisputable, however, and the linguistic turn itself has formed a central thread in the development of analytic philosophy, more generally. Even among those who have returned to systematic metaphysical theorizing, careful attention to language is still an essential part.

When we consider the more detailed level, however, assessment of the significance of the Tractatus becomes more complex. One of the first reviews, and certainly the most substantial and insightful, was by Ramsey in 1923, and his own subsequent work on philosophy and the foundations of mathematics was heavily influenced by the Tractatus—and the conversations he later had with Wittgenstein (see Note on the Text, §6). Ramsey tragically died in 1930, however, at the age of just 26, although his own legacy has lived on.

The Tractatus was also a major influence on the logical positivists—the members of the Vienna Circle. They took over Wittgenstein’s conception of logical propositions as tautologies and approved of what they saw as his wholesale repudiation of metaphysics. But they stayed silent about his conception of showing, and focused more on scientific verifiability as the criterion for senseful propositions (although this was also influenced by discussions with Wittgenstein that they later had). When logical positivism was imported into Britain through the work of A. J. Ayer, in his iconoclastic Language, Truth and Logic of 1936, Wittgenstein’s view of ethics influenced the emergence of emotivism, the view that ethical (and aesthetic and religious) propositions are neither true nor false (since they cannot be verified) but are merely expressions of emotion. The distinction between fact and value was to become a fundamental issue in the heated debates about ethics that subsequently took place.

In Britain, in the decade that followed the publication of the Tractatus, inspired also by Russell’s lectures on ‘The Philosophy of Logical Atomism’ (1918), members of what came to be called the Cambridge School of Analysis undertook analyses of all kinds of propositions in an endeavour to uncover their ‘real’ logical form, and engaged in debate about the nature of analysis. Key figures here were Susan Stebbing (1885–1943) and John Wisdom (1904–93). By the mid-1930s, however, scepticism about the whole project of reductive analysis grew and philosophers began focusing more on clarificatory conceptual analysis, aimed at resolving philosophical problems in a piecemeal fashion rather than seeking to develop any systematic account. This proceeded alongside the development of Wittgenstein’s later philosophy and his own critique of the Tractatus, which influenced philosophers through attendance at his lectures and notes that were made of those lectures, as well as dictated material such as the Blue and Brown Books.

With the rise of Nazi Germany, many of the logical positivists—including Carnap, Hans Reichenbach (1891–1953), Herbert Feigl (1902–88), Carl Hempel (1905–97), and Gustav Bergmann (1906–87)—emigrated to the United States, and this boosted the interest there in the Tractatus, in the areas of both mathematical logic and philosophy. Max Black (1909–88), who moved to the USA in 1940 and published the first companion to the Tractatus in 1964, Norman Malcolm, who had studied with Wittgenstein (see §1 above), Wilfrid Sellars (1912–89), Donald Davidson (1917–2003), Hilary Putnam (1926–2016), and Stanley Cavell (1926–2018) are just some of the leading American philosophers who were significantly influenced by Wittgenstein’s work.

The impact of the Tractatus was not restricted to Europe and North America, however. To take just the case of China, it was first translated into Chinese as early as 1927 by Zhang Shenfu (1893–1986), partly inspired by Russell’s visit to Beijing in 1920–1. Russell’s lectures there fuelled the development of mathematical logic and analytic philosophy in the years that followed, led by the so-called ‘new realist school’ at Tsinghua University. Two philosophers were especially influential: Jin Yuelin (1895–1984), who visited Britain in 1922 and founded the School of Philosophy at Tsinghua in 1926, and Hung Tscha (1909–92), who had been a member of the Vienna Circle, writing his doctorate under Schlick’s supervision, before returning to China in 1937 to promote logical positivism. One of Jin’s students was Wang Hao (1921–95), who was to go on to do his PhD with W. V. O. Quine (1908–2000) at Harvard and become a distinguished logician with a deep interest in Wittgenstein’s philosophy.

After the Second World War, what came to be called ‘ordinary language philosophy’, pursued not only by Wittgenstein, whose Philosophical Investigations was finally published in 1953, but also by philosophers such as Gilbert Ryle (1900–76) and J. L. Austin (1911–60) at Oxford, dominated the scene in Britain and gained a hold in North America. At the same time, interest in Frege’s work took off, with translations appearing for the first time, and this led to a renewal of interest in the Tractatus. Whereas, before, Wittgenstein had been associated more with Russell, as the joint architects of the project of logical atomism, the influence of Frege began to be stressed, spearheaded by Elizabeth Anscombe and later by Cora Diamond. Anscombe’s ground-breaking book on the Tractatus was published in 1959, and a series of important articles by Diamond began appearing in the late 1970s, which inspired the ‘new Wittgensteinian’ debate outlined in §5 above. Her later articles were collected in her aptly named Reading Wittgenstein with Anscombe (2019).

As Wittgenstein’s later writings were published, debate about the continuities and discontinuities in the development of his thinking intensified. Today there is scholarly consensus that his earlier ideas were not rejected altogether, but there is much less agreement not just about the details of this development but also about the assessment of his ideas. When Michael Dummett published his pioneering book on Frege, Frege: Philosophy of Language, in 1973, arguing that Frege should be seen as the founder of modern (truth-conditional) semantic theory, Wittgenstein’s Tractatus was co-opted onto this programme, and Wittgenstein’s later critique of his earlier ideas became part of the debate about the viability of theories of meaning. In some quarters his Tractatus is now valued far more highly than any of his later work, echoing a view that Russell famously expressed in comparing Wittgenstein’s earlier and later work:

The earlier Wittgenstein, whom I knew intimately, was a man addicted to passionately intense thinking, profoundly aware of difficult problems of which I, like him, felt the importance, and possessed (or at least so I thought) of true philosophical genius. The later Wittgenstein, on the contrary, seems to have grown tired of serious thinking and to have invented a doctrine which would make such an activity unnecessary.26

This is deeply unfair, however. The seriousness of Wittgenstein’s thinking comes out throughout his life, and if anyone can be called a philosophical genius, then it is surely him. One way to show that Russell is wrong is to bring out the continuities in Wittgenstein’s philosophy. The most fundamental continuity is his concern with the limits of language and thought. Philosophy is the discipline that works at the very cutting-edge of thinking, forging new conceptual tools and creating richer forms of expression in making sense of our evolving world, thought, and language, while continually negotiating the boundaries between sense and nonsense. Inevitably, one transgresses the boundaries in trying to find them, and such transgression may even be necessary to critique and transform our conceptual schemes. Wittgenstein, more than perhaps any other philosopher, was acutely aware of the drive to philosophize and the dangers it inevitably brings, and deeply sensitive to the phenomenology of philosophical thinking. The Tractatus crystallizes the thoughts that he had about all this in the earliest period of his intellectual life and set the agenda not only for his own subsequent thinking but also for much of the philosophizing that was inspired by the linguistic turn that he played such a central role in inaugurating.
