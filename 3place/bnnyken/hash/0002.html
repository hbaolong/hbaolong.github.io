E. B. White (1899–1985): Key Facts and Importance

Full name: Elwyn Brooks White
Born: July 11, 1899, Mount Vernon, New York
Died: October 1, 1985, North Brooklin, Maine (age 86)
Nicknamed: “Andy” (from a tradition at Cornell where students named White were called Andrew after the university’s first president, Andrew D. White)

Major Works

Children’s Books (the classics he is most remembered for today) - Stuart Little (1945) – a mouse born to a human family; groundbreaking for its blend of fantasy and realism. - Charlotte’s Web (1952) – widely considered one of the greatest children’s books ever written; story of a pig (Wilbur) and a spider (Charlotte) who saves him. Often ranked #1 on lists of best children’s literature. - The Trumpet of the Swan (1970) – about a trumpeter swan born without a voice.

Essays & New Yorker Contributions - Contributed to The New Yorker from its founding in 1925 until his death (almost 60 years). - Signature style: clear, elegant, witty, slightly melancholic prose. - Famous essays: “Here Is New York” (1949), “Once More to the Lake” (1941), “Death of a Pig” (1948).

Style and Reference Books - Co-author (with William Strunk Jr.) of The Elements of Style (1959, often called “Strunk & White”). The little book that became the bible of American English usage (“Omit needless words,” “Use the active voice,” etc.). Still in print and used in virtually every college writing course.

Career Highlights

  • 1925–1938: Core writer and columnist at The New Yorker (wrote the famous “Talk of the Town” pieces, often unsigned).
  • 1938–1943: Wrote the monthly column “One Man’s Meat” for Harper’s Magazine from his farm in Maine — later collected into a beloved book.
  • Moved permanently to his saltwater farm in Brooklin, Maine in 1938; lived a deliberately rural, low-key life while producing most of his major works.

Awards & Honors

  • Laura Ingalls Wilder Award (1970) – now called the Children’s Literature Legacy Award
  • Pulitzer Special Citation (1978) “for his letters, essays and the full body of his work”
  • National Medal for Literature (1971)
  • Presidential Medal of Freedom (1963) – awarded by Lyndon B. Johnson

Personal Life

  • Married Katharine Sergeant Angell (The New Yorker’s fiction editor) in 1929; she was 7 years older and already had two children from a previous marriage (whom White helped raise).
  • Stepfather to Roger Angell (famous New Yorker writer and baseball essayist).
  • Loved sailing, farming, animals (especially dogs and pigs), and the Maine coast.
  • Famously private and shy; avoided most public appearances and interviews.
  • Suffered from anxiety and hypochondria throughout his life.

Legacy & Influence

  • Often called the finest American essayist of the 20th century.
  • Helped define The New Yorker’s voice alongside James Thurber, Dorothy Parker, and Harold Ross.
  • Charlotte’s Web has sold over 50 million copies and been translated into 23 languages.
  • The Elements of Style has sold over 10 million copies and is still the standard writing guide in the U.S.
  • His clear, humane, unpretentious prose style remains a model for generations of writers.

One signature quote that sums up his philosophy (from the 1969 preface to The Elements of Style): > “Style takes its final shape more from attitudes of mind than from principles of composition… The beginner should approach style warily, realizing that it is himself he is approaching, no other.”

In short: E. B. White was the quiet giant of 20th-century American letters — master essayist, children’s author of immortal books, and co-creator of the most influential little writing handbook in the English language.
