By Will HuttonLinda Holbeche

PETER FERDINAND DRUCKER – IDEAS, IMPACT AND LEGACY

Peter Drucker is one of the greats. His sustained creativity over nearly fifty years of reflection on management, organization and the wider economy and society is almost unmatched. There is scarcely a contemporary idea—whether the idea of the voluntary knowledge worker to the importance of management itself—that was not prefigured in his writing. Sometimes his enthusiasm ran away with him (we are not strangers to that ourselves): it is not obvious that knowledge, for example, is quite so short lived as he once declared (redundant in ten minutes) and the belief that all the world’s problems can be reduced to managerial solutions is surely overdoing it. But his overall legacy is so outstanding that you have to pay homage—and acknowledge the extent of one’s own personal debt.

He was the Enlightenment thinker par excellence. Drucker’s breadth of vision is reflected in his many fields of inquiry: international law, history, organizations, politics, ethics, aesthetics and the human condition. His wide range of publications reflected his belief that management is central to life, not just to business. He was a true Renaissance man.

His prolific written output included three-dozen books (fifteen on management) and hundreds of articles, carrying on writing into his 90s. He launched his reputation as a management guru with The Practice of Management (1954) which he reinforced by Managing for Results (1964), both hugely influential texts. His writings divide almost equally between management theory and technique and works of economic, political and social analysis, such as The Age of Discontinuity (1969) which pointed to the decline of Britain’s manufacturing industry.

The free spirit

Drucker refused to play the academic game, declining to publish in obscure journals. He was one of the first thinkers to write for management audiences, translating theory into practice. He pioneered courses on leadership in the 1950s and coined the term “followership”. He argued that leaders should spend less time asking “what do I want?” than “what is needed?” He also stated: “you cannot manage other people unless you have learned to manage yourself”.

Consequently he was accused by academia of being a wilful generalist and was never offered major roles in prestigious business schools or universities. Instead he preferred to work for smaller universities less hide-bound by academic dogma. Indeed, though he was Clarke Professor of Social Science and Management at the independent Claremont Graduate University his critique of much business school education would do little to endear him to academic colleagues. He argued (in 2001) that most management education, even at Claremont, is basically the same as 65 years ago and that what is needed is a rethink of the curriculum so that students can relate themselves more fully to the universe of knowledge such as psychology, economics etc. and that social skills in particular are missing. He stated “we do a very poor job of teaching writing, speaking and listening, the communication tools on which all effectiveness in an organization depends” (BizEd, Nov/Dec 2001).

Major universities and funding bodies still have much to learn from Drucker’s broader definition of what constitutes knowledge, especially that which can be translated into valuable practice.

His scepticism about the role of business schools was reflected in many of his later predictions about how work would develop. The heart of business success would increasingly be via creativity and innovation, and it was wrong to suggest that the right approach could be codified in any business school course; thus the context for the slightly outlandish claim that knowledge makes itself obsolete every ten minutes. Creativity is always a response to the challenge of a particular circumstance—and requires a degree of personal responsibility and willingness to experiment. Too many business school graduates took refuge in the notion of a predictable career delivered by the practice of acquired and never changing competences and skills. That was organizational death. Rather every manager had to possess an entrepreneurial understanding; the trick of the leader was to find ways of inculcating such a culture and motivating managers to flower within it—even when there might not be immediate promotion opportunities.

The thought leader

Many of Drucker’s books have become classics of management literature. Where Drucker led, others followed. The Age of Discontinuity prefigured later books in the 1980s and 1990s on managing chaos and disruptive change, anticipating in particular works by Handy (The Age of Unreason), Pascale, Peters, Naisbitt (Megatrends) and others.

Throughout his management writing, Drucker’s emphasis was on the effectiveness of managers—particularly in making good use of their human resources—as the key to a productive and profitable organization. His five basic principles of management have stood the test of time:

• Setting objectives (set out in Management: Tasks, Responsibilities, Practices (1973)) which defines every aspect of managerial skills and is regarded as a DIY business school course.

• Organizing.

• Motivating and communicating (though he has sometimes been criticized for neglecting theories of motivation).

• Establishing measurements of performance.

• Developing people, including self.

He created some of the leading management theories of the 20th century, including:

• Management by objectives (and by self-control). MBO emerged out of Drucker’s work as consultant with General Electric and was later developed further by Humble.

• Putting the customer first and ‘Stick to the knitting’ (later taken up by Tom Peters).

• The role of the chief executive in corporate strategy.

• Structure follows strategy (Hammer and Champy’s approach to re-engineering owes its roots to early work by Drucker).

Similarly, he identified 7 key elements of post-war management development which continue to influence thinking and practice in organizations:

  1. Scientific management of work as the key to productivity.

  2. Decentralization as a basic principle of organization.

  3. Personnel management as the orderly way of fitting people into organization structures.

  4. Manager development to provide for the needs of tomorrow.

  5. Managerial accounting—use of analysis and information as the foundation for firm decision-making.

  6. Marketing.

  7. Long-range planning.

He challenged conventional wisdom. One of the key ideas throughout his writing is not to make assumptions about technology or markets, but to “look at the world upside down” and “build organized abandonment into your system”.

The soothsayer

Drucker’s ability to foresee and interpret trends was legendary. He had remarkable prescience, anticipating by decades some of the major changes in world economics and the impact of technology on the workplace. In The Age of Discontinuity (1969) for instance he introduced the concepts of the coming information economy and the “knowledge worker”. We are both directors of The Work Foundation where we continue to build on Drucker’s ideas in our multi-phased research into the nature and effects of the Knowledge Economy.

His book The New Realities (1989) anticipated global changes including the development of the transnational economy, the democratization of the Soviet Republics, the changing ethos of the US and the demands of the post-industrial, post-business society. He believed that the imperative for growth inevitably pushes organizations beyond their national borders in search of new markets and that the world becomes interconnected by a series of cross-cutting trade relationships in which the interests of managers outweigh the interests of politicians.

In Post-Capitalist Society (1992) he examined the division of society into knowledge and service workers and the economic and social challenges involved. He recognized that workers would need to master self-management, a discipline which he considered to be both challenging and little researched. In his later writings he recognized the growing power of pension funds and the narrow focus of other institutional investors in late twentieth century capitalism.

Similarly, he recognized that (re-)privatization was likely to be a feature of developed economies as the public becomes disillusioned with governments’ inability to produce miracles. He advocated privatization of all institutions, including universities, on the grounds that the role of government was to govern, not to “do” and that the two roles were incompatible. The insight was valuable, but limited; there remains a quality of “publicness” about institutions that deliver public services that has to be retained, even while they need more autonomy, freedom and capacity to devise their own creative responsiveness to citizen users demands rather than carry out centralized diktats and targets. Simple privatization was and is never enough. At The Work Foundation we are developing a programme investigating public value that aims to combine Drucker’s insistence that public sector organizations need to be more decentralized and have more autonomy with their ongoing need to create public value. Drucker, we hope, would have approved.

Many of Drucker’s later writings were about the lessons to be learnt from the way voluntary bodies are run. Another of his later concerns was about what society should do about the fact that people are living longer and retiring earlier. In other words, in the post-industrial economy we are still treating retirement like we did in the mid-20th century, resulting in bored, underutilized talented people who want to still be active in their 70s and resentful younger workers whose taxes support them. One answer, he argued, is to get away from a promotion-oriented career to one where lifelong opportunities to develop personal interests and skills are an important aspect of reward.

The importance of purpose

But perhaps some of the most profound and perspicacious of his ideas relate to the purpose of work itself. To Drucker, the business organization should be “a human, a social, indeed a moral phenomenon”; that customer service rather than profit should dominate management thinking and that profit should be invested in ongoing innovation and improvement. In People and Performance (1973) he defined his view of management as “to fulfil the specific purpose and mission of the organization; to make work productive and the worker achieving; and to manage social impacts and social responsibility”.

He argued that, as power in the workplace shifted from bosses to knowledge workers, they effectively become volunteers, who join and stay with the organization because they believe in what the organization stands for; therefore it is of critical importance to articulate and maintain a strong sense of purpose that keeps them there.

As a visionary and profound humanitarian, he advocated organizational approaches which help people to grow, organizations to flourish and work made more rewarding. There is no higher purpose. Indeed, it is Peter Drucker’s influence and vision which has helped shape the purpose and mission of The Work Foundation which is to inspire “good work for all” in the twenty-first century. It is an inspirational legacy.

WILL HUTTON, CEO, THE WORK FOUNDATION

Linda Holbeche, Director of the Campaign for Leadership, The Work Foundation

Acknowledgments

This book grew out of a suggestion by my long-time American editor, Cass Canfield, Jr., of HarperCollins. It is, however, a very different book from the one Mr. Canfield and I originally envisaged. We thought of a book that would bring together in one volume the best from the management books I have written and published for more than fifty years—a kind of “Drucker Retrospective.” But as I began to work on the book Mr. Canfield suggested, it became increasingly clear to both of us that what was appropriate was not a book looking backward. It was one that looks AHEAD. As a result, this book contains NOTHING that is an excerpt from earlier management books of mine. It supplements them by LOOKING AHEAD. And all the time, while working on this book, I have had—as I have had for many, many years—the benefit of Mr. Canfield’s advice, suggestions, comments—they have greatly improved this book.

But this book also celebrates SIXTY years of close association with my UK publisher, Butterworth-Heinemann. Since the firm—then Wm. Heinemann—published my first book, The End of Economic Man, in 1939, I have had no other publisher in the UK and in the countries of the Commonwealth. It is an association I greatly treasure. I am delighted that this book of mine will again appear under the Heinemann imprint.

As readers see in Chapter Three, I preach piloting the new, that is, testing it on a small scale. And, for once, I practice what I preach. I pilot-test a new book. One way is to distribute early drafts and copies to a few friends—mostly longtime clients—and ask for their candid reaction. Again and again I have changed something, rewritten a section, clarified an issue, as a result of their comments and criticism. But the best pilot-test for my writings, I have found, is to prepublish sections of a forthcoming book in magazines. This does TWO things. I get reactions from readers—and they tell me both what might need changing and where I need to explain or clarify. I owe a great debt to the people—mostly strangers—who write in, comment on or criticize one of these prepublished pieces, and especially to those who—often loudly—dissent. My thanks to them all. But, above all, prepublishing in a magazine gives me the inestimable benefit of being EDITED. I cannot even begin to do justice to what I owe the editors of these magazines—for their questions, their guidance, their cutting, rephrasing, repositioning. Especially thanks are due to Jim Michaels and Rich Karlgaard of Forbes magazine (which prepublished sections of Chapter One and the first part of Chapter Four), to Gunders Strads of the California Management Review (which prepublished an abridged version of Chapter Five), and to Nan Stone of the Harvard Business Review (which prepublished sections of Chapter Four and Chapter Six). They greatly helped to make this a better book.

Introduction: Tomorrow’s “Hot” Issues

Where, readers may ask, is the discussion of COMPETITIVE STRATEGY, of LEADERSHIP, of CREATIVITY, of TEAMWORK, of TECHNOLOGY in a book on MANAGEMENT CHALLENGES? Where are the “HOT” ISSUES OF TODAY? But this is the very reason why they are not in this book. It deals exclusively with TOMORROW’S “Hot” Issues—the crucial, central, life-and-death issues that are certain to be the major challenges of tomorrow.

CERTAIN? Yes. For this is not a book of PREDICTIONS, not a book about the FUTURE. The challenges and issues discussed in it are already with us in every one of the developed countries and in most of the emerging ones (e.g., Korea or Turkey). They can already be identified, discussed, analyzed and prescribed for. Some people, someplace, are already working on them. But so far very few organizations do, and very few executives. Those who do work on these challenges today, and thus prepare themselves and their institutions for the new challenges, will be the leaders and dominate tomorrow. Those who wait until these challenges have indeed become “hot” issues are likely to fall behind, perhaps never to recover.

This book is thus a Call for Action.

These challenges are not arising out of today. THEY ARE DIFFERENT. In most cases they are at odds and incompatible with what is accepted and successful today. We live in a period of PROFOUND TRANSITION—and the changes are more radical perhaps than even those that ushered in the “Second Industrial Revolution” of the middle of the 19th century, or the structural changes triggered by the Great Depression and the Second World War. READING this book will upset and disturb a good many people, as WRITING it disturbed me. For in many cases—for example, in the challenges inherent in the DISAPPEARING BIRTHRATE in the developed countries, or in the challenges to the individual, and to the employing organization, discussed in the final chapter on MANAGING ONESELF—the new realities and their demands require a REVERSAL of policies that have worked well for the last century and, even more, a change in the MINDSET of organizations as well as of individuals.

This is a MANAGEMENT BOOK. It intentionally leaves out BUSINESS CHALLENGES—even very important ones such as the question of whether the EURO will displace the U.S. dollar as the world’s key currency, or what will SUCCEED the 19th century’s most successful economic inventions, the commercial bank and the investment bank. It intentionally does not concern itself with ECONOMICS—even though the basic MANAGEMENT changes (e.g., the emergence of knowledge as the economy’s key resource) will certainly necessitate radically new economic theory and equally radically new economic policy. The book does not concern itself with politics—not even with such crucial questions as whether Russia can and will recover as a political, military and economic power. It sticks with MANAGEMENT ISSUES. There are good reasons for this. The issues this book discusses, the new social, demographic and economic REALITIES, are not issues that GOVERNMENT can successfully deal with. They are issues that will have profound impact on politics; but they are not political issues. They are not issues the Free Market can deal with. They are also not issues of ECONOMIC THEORY or even of ECONOMIC POLICY. They are issues that only MANAGEMENT and the INDIVIDUAL knowledge worker, professional or executive can tackle and resolve. They are surely going to be debated in the domestic politics of every developed and every emerging country. But their resolution will have to take place within the individual organization and will have to be worked out by the individual organization’s MANAGEMENT—and by every single individual knowledge worker (and especially by every single executive) within the organization.

A great many of these organizations will, of course, be businesses. And a great many of the individual knowledge workers affected by these challenges will be employees of business or working with business. Yet this is a MANAGEMENT book rather than a BUSINESS management book. The challenges it presents affect ALL organizations of today’s society. In fact, some of them will affect nonbusinesses even more, if only because a good many nonbusiness organizations—the university, for instance, or the hospital, let alone the government agency—are more rigid and less flexible than businesses are, and far more deeply rooted in the concepts, the assumptions, the policies of yesterday or even, as are universities, in the assumptions of the day before yesterday (i.e., of the 19th century).

How to use the book? I suggest you read a chapter at a time—they are long chapters. And then first ask: “What do these issues, these challenges MEAN for our organization and for me as a knowledge worker, a professional, an executive?” Once you have thought this through, ask: “What ACTION should our organization and I, the individual knowledge worker and/or executive, take to make the challenges of this chapter into OPPORTUNITIES for our organization and me?”

AND THEN GO TO WORK!

Peter F. Drucker

Claremont, California

New Year’s Day 1999
