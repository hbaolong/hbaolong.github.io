42. Viking Raids on Ireland, 795–842

The Annals of Ulster is one of the most important sources of early Irish history. It is a record of medieval Irish affairs, covering the period from the fifth to the early sixteenth centuries, surviving in two late manuscripts. The annals drew on earlier sources, and by the Viking period they were contemporary with the events they chronicle; they have been described as “substantially accurate accounts” of Viking activity, though this is debated. The chronology of the annals is off by one year for the section reproduced here; corrected dates are provided in parentheses. Because of limited space, only entries pertaining to the Vikings are reproduced here; the culling of the text in this manner has the effect of removing much contextual material that illustrates, among other things, that Irish society was extremely violent before the arrival of the Vikings.

Source: trans. G. MacNiocaill. S. MacAirt, ed. The Annals of Ulster (to A.D. 1131) (Dublin: Institute for Advanced Studies, 1983), pp. 251–301, excerpts.

AD 794 [795]

  1. The burning of Rechru [Rathlinn, Co. Antrim, or Lambay, Co. Dublin] by the heathens, and Scí [possibly Skye, but more likely scrin: shrine, or reliquary] was overwhelmed and laid waste.

AD 797 [798]

  1. The burning of Inis Pátraic [St. Patrick’s Island] by the heathens, and they took the cattle-tribute of the territories, and broke the shrine of Do-Chonna, and also made great incursions both in Ireland and in Alba [Scotland].

AD 801 [802]

  1. Í Coluim Chille [Iona, in the Hebrides] was burned by the heathens.

AD 805 [806]

  1. The community of Í [Iona], to the number of sixty-eight, was killed by the heathens.

AD 806 [807]

  1. The heathens burned Inis Muiredaig and invaded Ros Comáin.

  2. A battle between the community of Corcach and the community of Cluain Ferta Brénainn, among whom resulted a slaughter of a countless number of ordinary ecclesiastics and of eminent men of the community of Corcach.

AD 811 [812]

  1. A slaughter of the heathens by the men of Umall. A slaughter of the Conmaicne by the heathens.

  2. A slaughter of the heathens in Mumu, viz. by Cobthach son of Mael Dúin, King of Loch Léin.

AD 812 [813]

  1. The slaughter at Umall by the heathens in which fell Coscrach son of Flannabra and Dúnadach, king of Umall.

AD 820 [821]

  1. Étar was plundered by the heathens, [and] they carried off a great number of women into captivity.

AD 822 [823]

  1. Heathens invaded Bennchor the great [Bangor, Co. Down].

AD 823 [824]

  1. The heathens plundered Bennchor at Airtiu [?], and destroyed the oratory, and shook the relics of Comgall from their shrine….

  2. Étgal of Scelec [Skellig Michael, a tiny, jagged, mountain of rock off the southwest coast of Co. Kerry on which was a small monastic community] was carried off by the heathens, and died shortly afterwards of hunger and thirst.

AD 824 [825]

  1. Dún Lethglaise was plundered by the heathens.

  2. Mag Bile with its oratories was burned by the heathens.

  3. The Ulaid inflicted a rout on the heathens in Mag Inis, in which very many fell.

  4. The heathens inflicted a rout on the Osraige.

  5. The plundering of Inis Daimle by the heathens.

  6. The violent death of Blamac son of Flann at the hands of the heathens in Í Coluim Chille [Iona; see doc. 43].

AD 826 [827]

  1. Lusca was plundered by the heathens and burned; and Cianacht was invaded as far as Uachtar Ugán; and also a plundering of the foreigners of the East.

  2. An encampment of the Laigin was overwhelmed by the heathens, and Conall son of Cú Chongalt, king of the Fortuatha, and countless others fell there.

AD 827 [828]

  1. A great slaughter of porpoises on the coast of Ard Cianachta by the foreigners; and the violent death of the anchorite Teimnén.

  2. The mortal wounding of Cinaed son of Cumuscach, king of Ard Cianachta, by the foreigners; and Lann Léire and Cluain Mór were burned by them.

  3. A battle-rout [was inflicted] on the heathens by Lethlobar son of Loingsech, king of Dál Araidi.

  4. Another battle-rout [was inflicted] on the heathens by Cairpre son of Cathal, king of Uí Cheinnselaig, and by the community of Tech Munnu.

AD 828 [829]

  1. Diarmait, abbot of Í, went to Scotland with the halidoms [relics] of Colum Cille [St. Columba, d. 597]. [This is thought to have been in response to the frequent raiding of Iona by the Vikings.]

AD 830 [831]

  1. Conaille was invaded by the heathens, and Mael Brigte, its king, and his brother Canannán, were taken prisoner and taken away to the ships.

  2. The heathens defeated the community of Ard Macha in a battle at Aignig, and great numbers of them were taken captive.

AD 831 [832]

  1. The first plundering of Ard Macha by the heathens three times in one month.

  2. The plundering of Mucnám, Lugbad, Uí Méith, Druim Moccu Blae, and other churches.

  3. The plundering of Dam Liac and the sept [division of a clan] of the Cianacht with all their churches by the heathens.

  4. Ailill son of Colgu was taken captive by the heathens.

  5. Tuathal son of Feradach was taken away by the heathens, and Adamnán’s shrine from Domnach Maigen.

  6. Ráith Luraig and Connaire were plundered by the heathens.

AD 832 [833]

  1. Niall and Murchad routed the foreigners in Daire Calgaig.

  2. Cluain Dolcáin was plundered by the heathens.

  3. Druim Inasclainn was burned by the heathens.

AD 833 [834]

  1. Dúnadach son of Scannlán, king of Uí Fhidgeinte, won a battle against the heathens, in which many fell.

  2. Glenn dá Locha was plundered by the heathens. Sláine and Finnubair Abae were plundered by the heathens.

AD 834 [835]

  1. Ferna and Cluain Mór Maedóc were plundered by the heathens.

  2. Mungairit and other churches of Iarmumu were burned by the heathens.

  3. The foreigners plundered Druim Ing.

AD 835 [836]

  1. Cell Dara was plundered by heathens from Inber Dea, and half of the church was burned.

  2. The first prey was taken by the heathens from southern Brega, i.e. from Telcha Dromáin and Dairmag of the Britons; and they carried off many prisoners, and killed many and led away very many captive.

  3. A most cruel devastation of all the lands of Connacht by the heathens. The heathens inflicted a slaughter in a battle won over the Déis Tuaisceirt.

AD 836 [837]

  1. A naval force of the Norsemen sixty ships strong was on the Bóinn, and another one of sixty ships on the river Life. Those two forces plundered the plain of Life and the plain of Brega, including churches, forts, and dwellings. The men of Brega routed the foreigners at Deoninne in Mugdorna of Brega, and six score of the Norsemen fell.

  2. The heathens won a battle at Inber na mBárc against the Uí Néill from the Sinann to the sea, in which an uncounted number were slaughtered, though the principal kings escaped.

  3. Inis Celtra was plundered by the heathens.

  4. The churches of all Loch Éirne, including Cluain Eóis and Daiminis, were destroyed by the heathens.

  5. Saxolb, chief of the foreigners, was killed by the Cianacht.

AD 837 [838]

  1. The heathens won a battle against the Connachta, in which Mael Dúin son of Muirgius and many others fell.

AD 838 [839]

  1. A raiding party of the foreigners were on Loch nEchach, and from there they plundered the states and churches of the north of Ireland.

  2. The heathens won a battle against the men of Foirtriu, and Eóganán son of Aengus, Bran son of Óengus, Aed son of Boanta, and others almost innumerable fell there.

  3. Ferna and Corcach were burned by the heathens.

AD 839 [840]

  1. Lugbad was plundered by the heathens from Loch nEchach and they led away captive bishops and priests and scholars, and put others to death.

  2. In this year below the Norsemen first came to Ireland, according to the senchus [Senchus Fer n-Alban (History of the Men of Scotland), written in the tenth century].

AD 840 [841]

  1. The heathens were still on Loch nEchach.

  2. There was a naval camp [longport] at Linn Duachaill from which the peoples and churches of Tethba were plundered. There was a naval camp at Duiblinn from which the Laigin and the Uí Néill were plundered, both states and churches, as far as Sliab Bladma.

AD 841 [842]

  1. The heathens still at Duiblinn.

  2. Mael Dúin son of Conall, king of Calatruim, was taken prisoner by the heathens.

  3. Cluain Moccu Nóis was plundered by heathens from Linn Duachaill.

  4. Biror and Saiger were plundered by heathens from Duiblinn.

  5. A naval force of the Norsemen was on the Bóinn at Linn Rois. There was [also] a naval force of the Norsemen at Linn Sailech in Ulaid.

  6. Mórán son of Indrechtach, abbot of Clochar Mac nDaiméni, was taken prisoner by the foreigners of Linn, and later died on their hands.

  7. Comán, abbot of Linn Duachail, was fatally wounded and burned by heathens and Irish.

  8. Dísert Diarmata was plundered by heathens from Cael Uisci.
