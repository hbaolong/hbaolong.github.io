《小王子》三、四章单词学习

abruptly

Pronunciation
/əˈbrʌpt.li/ (uh-BRUPT-lee) – stress on the second syllable.

Etymology & Root
- From Latin abruptus “broken off” ← ab- “away” + rumpere “break”.
- Root: rupt- “break” (same as rupture, interrupt).

English-to-English explanation
Suddenly and unexpectedly, often in a rude or surprising way.
- Example: The meeting ended abruptly.

alas

Pronunciation
/əˈlæs/ (uh-LASS) – stress on the second syllable.

Etymology & Root
- Old French a las “ah, weary” ← Latin lassus “tired”.
- Root: lass- “weary”.

English-to-English explanation
An expression of sorrow, regret, or pity (literary/old-fashioned).
- Example: Alas, the king is dead.

earnestly

Pronunciation
/ˈɜːr.nɪst.li/ (URN-ist-lee) – stress on the first syllable.

Etymology & Root
- From Old English eornoste “serious” ← Germanic ern- “zeal”.
- Root: ern-* “serious effort”.

English-to-English explanation
With sincere and serious intention; seriously and eagerly.
- Example: She spoke earnestly about her plans.

asteroid

Pronunciation
/ˈæs.tə.rɔɪd/ (AS-tə-royd) – stress on the first syllable.

Etymology & Root
- Greek aster “star” + -oid “like” → “star-like”.
- Root: aster- “star” (same as astronomy, disaster).

English-to-English explanation
A small rocky body orbiting the sun, mostly in the asteroid belt between Mars and Jupiter.

astronomer

Pronunciation
/əˈstrɑː.nə.mɚ/ (ə-STRON-ə-mər) – stress on the second syllable.

Etymology & Root
- Greek astron “star” + nomos “law/arrangement” → “one who studies the laws of the stars”.
- Root: astro- “star” + nom- “law”.

English-to-English explanation
A scientist who studies stars, planets, and other objects in space.

brick

Pronunciation
/brɪk/ (BRIK)

Etymology & Root
- Middle English brike ← Old French brique ← Dutch bricke.
- Root: Uncertain, possibly Germanic.

English-to-English explanation
A rectangular block of hard material (usually baked clay) used for building walls.

bury

Pronunciation
/ˈber.i/ (BERR-ee) – rhymes with “merry”.

Etymology & Root
- Old English byrgan “to hide/protect” ← Proto-Germanic berg- “protect”.
- Root: bhergh-* “hide, protect” (same as harbor, borough).

English-to-English explanation
To put a dead body in the ground; to hide or cover something completely.

contemplation

Pronunciation
/ˌkɑːn.təmˈpleɪ.ʃən/ (kon-təm-PLAY-shən) – stress on the third syllable.

Etymology & Root
- Latin contemplatio “gazing at” ← con- + templum “temple/open space for observation”.
- Root: templ- “observe”.

English-to-English explanation
Deep, thoughtful looking at or thinking about something.

costume

Pronunciation
/ˈkɑː.stuːm/ (KOS-toom)

Etymology & Root
- French costume ← Italian costume “custom, fashion”.
- Root: Latin consuetudo “habit”.

English-to-English explanation
Clothing worn to look like a particular character, period, or for a special occasion.

curiosity

Pronunciation
/ˌkjʊr.iˈɑː.sə.ti/ (kyoor-ee-OS-ə-tee) – stress on the third syllable.

Etymology & Root
- Latin curiositas “desire to know” ← curiosus “careful/eager to know”.
- Root: cur- “care”.

English-to-English explanation
A strong desire to know or learn something; something unusual and interesting.

demonstration

Pronunciation
/ˌdem.ənˈstreɪ.ʃən/ (dem-ən-STRAY-shən) – stress on the third syllable.

Etymology & Root
- Latin demonstratio “pointing out” ← de- + monstrare “show”.
- Root: monstr- “show”.

English-to-English explanation
The act of showing how something works or proving something; a public protest.

dictator

Pronunciation
/ˈdɪk.teɪ.tɚ/ (DIK-tay-tər)

Etymology & Root
- Latin dictator “one who dictates” ← dicere “say”.
- Root: dic- “say”.

English-to-English explanation
A ruler with total power, often one who has taken control by force.

dove

Pronunciation
/dʌv/ (DUV) – rhymes with “love”.

Etymology & Root
- Old English dūfe ← Germanic dūbōn.
- Root: Uncertain, possibly imitative of its call.

English-to-English explanation
A small white or grey pigeon; a symbol of peace.

elegance

Pronunciation
/ˈel.ɪ.ɡəns/ (EL-i-gəns)

Etymology & Root
- Latin elegantia “tasteful” ← eligere “choose”.
- Root: leg- “choose”.

English-to-English explanation
Graceful beauty and refined good taste.

essential

Pronunciation
/ɪˈsen.ʃəl/ (i-SEN-shəl)

Etymology & Root
- Latin essentia “essence” ← esse “to be”.
- Root: es- “be”.

English-to-English explanation
Absolutely necessary; basic and fundamental.

figure

Pronunciation
/ˈfɪɡ.jɚ/ (FIG-yər)

Etymology & Root
- Latin figura “shape” ← fingere “form”.
- Root: fig- “shape”.

English-to-English explanation
A number, shape, or person’s body shape; to appear or be featured.

forbearance

Pronunciation
/fɔːrˈber.əns/ (for-BAIR-əns)

Etymology & Root
- forbear “refrain” + -ance ← Old English forberan “endure”.
- Root: ber- “bear”.

English-to-English explanation
Patient self-control; tolerance and restraint.

fumble

Pronunciation
/ˈfʌm.bəl/ (FUM-bəl)

Etymology & Root
- Probably Germanic fammelen “grope”.
- Root: Imitative of clumsy handling.

English-to-English explanation
To handle something clumsily or nervously; to fail to catch or hold.

geranium

Pronunciation
/dʒəˈreɪ.ni.əm/ (jə-RAY-nee-əm)

Etymology & Root
- Greek geranos “crane” (seed pod looks like crane’s bill).
- Root: geran- “crane”.

English-to-English explanation
A garden plant with bright red, pink, or white flowers (commonly Pelargonium).

gleam

Pronunciation
/ɡliːm/ (GLEEM)

Etymology & Root
- Old English glǣm “brightness”.
- Root: Germanic glīm- “shine”.

English-to-English explanation
A small, soft light or shine; to shine softly.

grief

Pronunciation
/ɡriːf/ (GREEF)

Etymology & Root
- Old French gref “heavy” ← Latin gravis “heavy”.
- Root: grav- “heavy”.

English-to-English explanation
Intense sorrow, especially caused by someone’s death.

impenetrable

Pronunciation
/ɪmˈpen.ɪ.trə.bəl/ (im-PEN-i-trə-bəl)

Etymology & Root
- Latin in- + penetrare “enter”.
- Root: penetr- “enter”.

English-to-English explanation
Impossible to pass through or understand.

Pronunciation

Etymology & Root
- impress + -ive ← Latin imprimere “press into”.
- Root: press- “press”.

English-to-English explanation
Causing admiration because of size, quality, or skill.

indifference

Pronunciation
/ɪnˈdɪf.ər.əns/ (in-DIF-ər-əns)

Etymology & Root
- Latin in- + differens “different”.
- Root: differ- “differ”.

English-to-English explanation
Lack of interest, concern, or sympathy.

irritate

Pronunciation
/ˈɪr.ɪ.teɪt/ (IR-i-tayt)

Etymology & Root
- Latin irritare “excite, provoke”.
- Root: irrit- “provoke”.

English-to-English explanation
To annoy or make someone angry.

misfortune

Pronunciation
/ˌmɪsˈfɔːr.tʃən/ (mis-FOR-chən)

Etymology & Root
- mis- + fortune.
- Root: fort- “chance”.

English-to-English explanation
Bad luck or an unfortunate event.

modestly

Pronunciation
/ˈmɑː.dɪst.li/ (MOD-ist-lee)

Etymology & Root
- modest + -ly ← Latin modestus “moderate”.
- Root: mod- “measure”.

English-to-English explanation
In a humble or unpretentious way.

peal

Pronunciation
/piːl/ (PEEL)

Etymology & Root
- Middle English pele “loud ringing”.
- Root: Imitative of bell sound.

English-to-English explanation
A loud ringing of bells or a burst of sound (e.g., laughter).

post

Pronunciation
/poʊst/ (POHST)

Etymology & Root
- Latin postis “pole, doorpost”.
- Root: pos- “place”.

English-to-English explanation
A vertical piece of wood or metal fixed in the ground; to send a letter or publish online.

presence

Etymology & Root
- Latin praesentia “being at hand” ← praesens “present”.
- Root: sent- “feel”.

English-to-English explanation
The state of being present; bearing or manner.

queer

Pronunciation
/kwɪr/ (KWEER)

Etymology & Root
- Possibly Germanic quer “oblique, perverse”.
- Root: Uncertain, perhaps twerh- “cross”.

English-to-English explanation
Strange or odd (traditional meaning); now also a reclaimed term for LGBTQ+ identity.
