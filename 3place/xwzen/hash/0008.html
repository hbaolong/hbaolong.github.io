《小王子》第五、六、七章单词学习

abruptly

Pronunciation
/əˈbrʌpt.li/ (uh-BRUPT-lee) – stress on the second syllable.

Etymology & Root
- From Latin abruptus “broken off” ← ab- “away” + rumpere “break”.
- Root: rupt- “break” (same as rupture, interrupt).

English-to-English explanation
Suddenly and unexpectedly, often in a surprising or rude way.
- Example: The conversation ended abruptly.

baobab

Pronunciation
/ˈbeɪ.oʊ.bæb/ (BAY-oh-bab) – stress on the first syllable.

Etymology & Root
- From Arabic bu hibab “fruit with many seeds”.
- Root: Arabic origin.

English-to-English explanation
A large African tree with a very thick trunk that stores water, known as the “tree of life” (also called “upside-down tree”).

bore

Pronunciation
/bɔːr/ (BOR) – rhymes with “door”.

Etymology & Root
- Old English borian “pierce”; figurative “make dull” from 1760s.
- Root: Germanic bur- “pierce”.

English-to-English explanation
1. (verb) To make someone feel tired and uninterested.
- Example: His long speech bored everyone.
2. (noun) A dull or uninteresting person or thing.

bush

Pronunciation
/bʊʃ/ (BOOSH)

Etymology & Root
- Old English busc “shrub”.
- Root: Germanic busk-.

English-to-English explanation
A low plant with many branches; a shrub.

castle

Pronunciation
/ˈkæs.əl/ (KAS-əl)

Etymology & Root
- Latin castellum “fortress”.
- Root: cast- “fort”.

English-to-English explanation
A large fortified building or group of buildings from the Middle Ages.

catastrophe

Pronunciation
/kəˈtæs.trə.fi/ (kə-TAS-trə-fee)

Etymology & Root
- Greek katastrophē “overturning” ← kata- “down” + strephein “turn”.
- Root: streph- “turn”.

English-to-English explanation
A sudden event causing great damage or suffering; a disaster.

chance

Pronunciation
/tʃæns/ (CHANS)

Etymology & Root
- Old French cheance “fall of dice” ← Latin cadere “fall”.
- Root: cad- “fall”.

English-to-English explanation
A possibility of something happening; luck or opportunity.

departure

Pronunciation
/dɪˈpɑːr.tʃɚ/ (di-PAR-chər)

Etymology & Root
- Old French departir “divide” → “leave”.
- Root: part- “divide”.

English-to-English explanation
The act of leaving a place, especially to start a journey.

discipline

Pronunciation
/ˈdɪs.ə.plɪn/ (DIS-ə-plin)

Etymology & Root
- Latin disciplina “teaching, learning” ← discipulus “pupil”.
- Root: disc- “learn”.

English-to-English explanation
Training to obey rules or a code of behavior; self-control; a branch of knowledge.

distinguish

Pronunciation
/dɪˈstɪŋ.ɡwɪʃ/ (di-STING-gwish)

Etymology & Root
- Latin distinguere “separate” ← dis- + stinguere “mark”.
- Root: stingu- “prick/mark”.

English-to-English explanation
To recognize or treat as different; to see or hear clearly.

grave

Pronunciation
/ɡreɪv/ (GRAYV)

Etymology & Root
- Old English græf “dig” → “tomb”.
- Root: Germanic grab- “dig”.

English-to-English explanation
1. (noun) A place where a dead person is buried.
2. (adjective) Serious or solemn.

herd

Pronunciation
/hɜːrd/ (HURD)

- Old English heord “group of animals”.
- Root: Germanic herdō.

English-to-English explanation
A large group of animals of the same type that live and move together.

Pronunciation
/ɪnˈfest/ (in-FEST)

Etymology & Root
- Latin infestare “attack” ← infestus “hostile”.
- Root: fest- “strike”.

English-to-English explanation
To be present in large numbers and cause damage or problems (usually insects or pests).

To live in a place.

- Root: mor- “custom”.

English-to-English explanation
A person who teaches or promotes morality, often critically.
